# JTBD — Session 5: Anxiety and Habit — The Friction

> *"There's this fuel to say like this is something I want to do. At the same time, there's two forces that actually are friction to the change. They hinder the change."*
> — Bobby Moesta (Jan 27, 2026, Memrise session)

---

## The simplest true statement

Push and pull create the *fuel* for change. Anxiety and habit create the *friction*. Most products focus almost entirely on the fuel side — making the product more attractive, adding features, sharpening the pitch. But Bobby's two biggest sales bumps in the condo business (28% and 14%) came entirely from working on the friction side. The fuel was already there. The question was why people weren't moving despite it.

---

## Where we are

In Session 3, we introduced all four forces. In Session 4, we went deep on push and pull — the two forces that *promote* change. This session flips to the other side: the two forces that *block* change, even when push and pull are strong.

From Bobby's Growth Summit slides:
- **Anxiety of New Solution** = Uncertainty. "Surrounding a new choice." A purple arrow pointing back toward the old way.
- **Habit of the Present** = Historical Allegiance. "The tug." Another purple arrow pointing back toward the old way.

Both point in the same direction: backward. Together they explain one of the most frustrating experiences in product — the customer who clearly has the problem, clearly wants the solution, and still doesn't switch.

---

## The asymmetry: they're different kinds of friction

Habit and anxiety don't work the same way. They block change at different points in the journey.

**Habit prevents people from *leaving* the old way.** Even if the old situation is genuinely bad, even if push is strong, there's something they love about where they are. The familiar is safe. The trade-offs are known. The thing they have to give up — even if it's irrational — feels like a loss.

**Anxiety prevents people from *arriving at* the new way.** Even if pull is strong, even if the new solution is credible, there are questions that haven't been answered. What if it doesn't work? What about this specific thing? What happens when...? Unanswered questions are the raw material of anxiety.

Bobby's language from the Memrise session:
> *"Uncertainty is usually framed as big questions they need to answer that if they can't answer they actually won't make the progress."*
> *"Habits are really the trade-offs they have to make, the things they have to give up in order to make the progress."*

Two different beasts. Two different treatments. The first step is learning to distinguish between them.

---

## Anxiety: unanswered questions, not fear

This is the reframe. Anxiety in Bobby's framework isn't an emotional state you have to soothe. It's a list of specific questions the customer needs answered before they can move.

From Bobby's condo example, the anxieties looked like this:
> *"Where are the kids going to sleep when they come? Who are my neighbours? Where's the grocery store? How much can I get for my house?"*

These aren't feelings. They're information gaps. And Bobby's observation is sharp: for every question like this that goes unanswered, the probability of the sale drops. It doesn't matter how strong the push and pull are. If the customer is sitting with five unanswered questions, they're not moving.

The Growth Summit slides frame this precisely. Anxiety sits on the right side of the forces diagram — specifically resisting movement *toward* the new solution. It's not about the old product being better. It's about the new product being uncertain. The customer simultaneously wants to switch and is afraid to. This is not cognitive dissonance. It's a rational response to incomplete information.

The implication for product is important: **anxiety reduction is often more valuable than feature addition.** More features don't reduce unanswered questions. They add new ones.

---

## The counterintuitive lesson: features increase anxiety

Bobby makes this point explicitly, and it runs directly against the instinct to add:

> *"What I learned in business school is they told me just add more features and people will buy. Well, that's actually not true. The more features I add, the more anxiety I get — because I don't need all those features."*

Think about what happens when a customer sees a product that does far more than they need. The questions multiply:
- Do I have to learn all of this?
- Will I be paying for things I'll never use?
- Is this actually designed for my situation, or am I just one of many use cases they're trying to cover?

When Intercom showed all four jobs bundled into one product at one price, customers didn't say "wow, great value." They asked for a discount — because the excess didn't add pull, it added noise. The product looked like it was built for someone else, and the mismatch between what was offered and what was needed created doubt.

Bobby's principle: **the goal is to answer the specific questions that matter for this person's specific progress. Not to impress them with everything else you can do.**

---

## The 28% bump: reducing anxiety about the move itself

This is Bobby's first big number from the condo business, and it's the clearest demonstration of anxiety reduction in action.

Customers were putting down deposits and then coming back six weeks later to back out. Their explanation:

> *"We have nine closets in the house. We spent the last six weeks cleaning out two closets. It took us literally six weeks to clean out two closets. We've got a full basement we have to clean out and we had to use three boxes of Kleenex just to basically go through... we're never going to be able to move."*

The anxiety wasn't about the condo. It wasn't a product problem. It was about the sheer physical and emotional weight of clearing out a home they'd lived in for decades. The *process* of leaving was the anxiety, not the destination.

Bobby's response: he didn't add a feature to the condo. He eliminated the anxiety directly:

> *"I built a storage facility basically across the street. I ended up contracting with a moving company and basically built a sorting room in the clubhouse. So when you did move, you could actually have the stuff stored for two years for free and you could actually end up being able to sort it and give your kids all the stuff they needed and it gave you time to think through what you had to do as opposed to being a big rush job."*

**28% increase in sales.**

Not a better kitchen. Not an extra bedroom. Not a lower price. A storage facility and a sorting room — interventions that had nothing to do with the product's features and everything to do with answering the customer's actual unanswered question: *how am I supposed to deal with all this stuff?*

This is what Bobby means when he says you have to sometimes build things that aren't "features of the house." The line between product and process dissolves when you're focused on the customer's progress, not the product's spec sheet.

---

## Habit: the emotional bank account

The dining room table story is one of Bobby's most memorable and precise illustrations of how habit actually works.

The setup: Bobby's condo was designed without a dining room, because when he interviewed customers, they said they didn't want one. They were done hosting holidays. Their kids were old enough. They wanted something smaller and simpler. Rational request. He accommodated it.

Then reality:

> *"The dining room table became one of the biggest anxieties in their move — because ultimately what is the dining room table to your parents?"*

Sonia answered from the call: *"An opportunity to have all the family around."*

Bobby:

> *"Yeah. Yeah. It's the emotional bank account of their entire life. Every holiday, every birthday."*

Not a piece of furniture. A symbol. A physical container for decades of family memory. And the practical implication was absolute: if there was nowhere to put the dining room table in the new place, they wouldn't move. Not couldn't. Wouldn't. The logic of the decision — that they didn't *want* a dining room anymore — dissolved the moment it became concrete that the table would have to go.

Bobby's solution:

> *"We gave them the option to literally make the second master suite smaller and we built a place where you could put a dining room table, but the reality is it was so small that the dining table would fit, but to eat around it was almost impossible."*

The table could be placed. It couldn't really be used. But that was fine. The customers were willing to make that trade-off. They didn't need to eat at it — they needed to *have* it. As a shrine, Bobby says. As proof that the thing wasn't lost.

**14% increase in sales.** From a space that was almost too small to eat in.

Bobby's reflection:

> *"This is where you have to realize — even though they say it, most of the time people are saying things in the abstract and they don't actually think about them in the right context. It's not what they say; it's what they do that's most important."*

This is the habit force in concrete form. People said they didn't want a dining room. What they *did* when faced with the specific trade-off — the actual loss of the table — told the true story. The habit wasn't a preference. It was an identity.

---

## Habit is not what they use. It's what they love.

It's worth clarifying what Bobby means by "habit of the present." He's not talking about frequency of use. He's not talking about features people rely on. He's talking about the things that have become part of who they are and how they see themselves.

From the Growth Summit slides, Bobby labels this force "Historical Allegiance." Not just preference — *allegiance*. The word matters. You don't switch allegiances lightly. You need a reason that's bigger than the one that created them.

This is also why Bobby frames habit as the trade-off the customer has to make:

> *"Habits are the things they have to give up in order to make the progress."*

The question for any product isn't just: what do customers love about us? It's: what do they love about the thing they already have? What would they be giving up? If you don't know the answer to that, you can't design for the transition. You might be offering them everything they said they wanted and still losing them to a dining room table they didn't know they needed to keep.

---

## The double barrier: working both sides

Bobby's breakthrough in the condo business came from treating anxiety and habit as a system — two separate problems requiring two separate interventions.

**The 28% bump** came from reducing anxiety. Specifically: answering the unanswered question about how to physically execute the move. The storage facility and sorting room were not features of the condo. They were answers to a question that was blocking progress.

**The 14% bump** came from honouring the habit. Specifically: giving the dining room table somewhere to go. Not a beautiful dining room. Not a practical one. Just a space that acknowledged the thing they couldn't let go of. The product met the customer where the habit actually lived, not where the customer said it lived.

Together, those are 42 percentage points of sales growth. Without changing the price. Without adding a bedroom. Without improving the kitchen.

That's the case for working the friction side.

---

## Why people don't switch even when they say they want to

This is the practical punchline. Bobby's framing:

> *"As much as they might have a big push and pull, they might have a lot of anxiety and a lot of habit that keeps them from from actually making the move."*

The four forces are a system. Push and pull don't automatically win. If anxiety and habit are strong enough — if the questions are unanswered, if the trade-offs feel too large — people stall. They stay in passive looking indefinitely. They complain about the old situation but don't act on the new one.

This is also why the timeline isn't linear. From Session 4: Bobby's observation that "nine times out of ten we lose to another struggling moment, not to a competitor." When that second struggling moment hits — when life gets complicated — people don't have the bandwidth to manage both the anxiety of switching and the anxiety of whatever else is happening. They retreat to the familiar. The habit reasserts itself.

Churn, in this frame, is often a habit story. Not that users stopped finding value. But that the emotional cost of continuing (the friction of engagement when life got busy, the loss of whatever they had before) outweighed the pull of the progress they were making.

---

## What this means for Memrise

Two questions Bobby would want the team to answer — not as hypotheticals, but from actual user interviews:

**On anxiety:**
What are the three or four specific questions a new Memrise user can't answer in their first week that prevent them from committing? Not vague concerns — specific information gaps. "Will I actually make progress if I only have ten minutes a day?" "How do I know which level to start at?" "What happens if I miss a few days?" These are anxiety questions. Every unanswered one is a friction point. The product doesn't need more features. It needs to answer these questions faster, more visibly, and more specifically.

Bobby raised this directly in the Memrise session when Sonia asked about time and motivation as barriers:

> *"Sometimes we actually have to make the struggle bigger — help them realise that if you don't learn this, then you have a bigger choice to make."*

This is a separate but related point: if the push isn't strong enough to overcome the friction, the answer isn't always to reduce the friction. Sometimes it's to help users feel the weight of their actual situation more clearly, so the fuel becomes powerful enough to move through the resistance.

**On habit:**
What does the user have to give up to use Memrise regularly? Not features of a competitor — habits of their own life. What fills the time they'd need to give to Memrise? What identity are they letting go of (the person who doesn't do structured learning, the person who keeps meaning to start, the person who "isn't a languages person")? And what would be the Memrise equivalent of the dining room table space — a small, perhaps impractical acknowledgement of that thing they're giving up, that makes the transition feel less like a loss?

## Three things to hold onto

1. **Anxiety is unanswered questions, not vague fear.** It's the specific things a customer can't answer about the new solution. Identify them. Answer them. More features don't answer them — they add new ones.

2. **Habit is what people love, not what they use.** The dining room table. The routine. The identity. It's the emotional bank account of their current way of doing things. You have to know what they're giving up before you can help them give it up gracefully.

3. **Work the friction side.** Push and pull get all the attention. The 28% and the 14% came from anxiety and habit. If your customers have strong push and pull and still aren't switching, that's a friction problem — not a fuel problem.
