# JTBD — Session 20: The Struggling Roadmap

> *"Instead of building a technology roadmap, we build a struggling roadmap — once we solve one problem, there's a new problem."*
> — Bobby Moesta, January 27 2026

> *"The struggling moment is the seed for all innovation. And by understanding where people struggle and how they struggle, it actually becomes the roadmap of the things we should go build."*
> — Bobby Moesta, same session

---

## The simplest true statement

You cannot plan your next product move from inside the building. The next right thing to build is only visible from inside your users' experience — after they've tried the version you just shipped.

The roadmap is not a plan. It is a question: *what is the next struggling moment we need to go address?*

That is the whole session. The rest is mechanics — how to ask that question systematically, and what the answer looks like in practice.

---

## Where we are

This is the final session. Everything before this — the four forces, the six phases, supply vs. demand, non-consumption, the JTBD interview, prototyping to learn, the rule of three, trade-offs — has been building toward one practical output: a product roadmap that is generated by struggling moments rather than by internal ambition or competitive mimicry.

Bobby named this at the very start of Session 1 at Memrise. It is worth hearing it in full:

> *"Once you get good at this, you start to see struggling moments everywhere and you start to realise that some struggles that people want to solve and others are accumulating struggles that will eventually be something they want to solve in the future. And to be honest, one of the things we do is instead of building a technology roadmap, we build a struggling roadmap — of like, once we solve one problem, there's a new problem."*

The phrase is intentionally direct. A technology roadmap is built from what you *can* build. A struggling roadmap is built from what your users *cannot yet do*.

The first comes from supply-side thinking. The second from demand-side thinking. Bobby has spent 40 years arguing that the second produces better products, more reliably, than the first.

---

## The iPhone as a struggling roadmap

The best case study Bobby gave for the struggling roadmap in action was not a startup or a case study. It was the most successful consumer product ever built.

> *"Think of Apple when the iPhone came out. It was basically replacing a PDA, a phone, and a music player. And it wasn't great at all three, but it was good enough. And I didn't have to carry three devices. And to be honest, the phone out of all of it worked the worst."*

This is a critical point. The first iPhone was not the best phone. It was not the best music player. It was not the best camera. It was the best bundled response to a specific struggling moment: the burden of carrying multiple devices that each did one thing.

Once that struggling moment was addressed, a new struggling moment emerged:

> *"Round one was the struggling moment was reception. And so, they basically worked on antennas, worked on ways to make sure it connected to the network, worked on the chip. And then once they had that, they had problems of battery life. And then all of a sudden they started to work on how do we do more things and conserve more power. And then the next set of struggling moments was the camera."*

> *"You start to realise that the roadmap wasn't picked — that they needed to work on the battery and then the camera. It was more like: at the end of every launch, what's the next set of struggling moments we need to go address. It's the iteration of what struggling moments are we going to attack, as opposed to what features do we need to build."*

That distinction — struggling moments to attack vs. features to build — is the whole idea.

A feature is a supply-side unit. A struggling moment is a demand-side unit. The iPhone roadmap, at every stage, was driven by the demand-side: what are users unable to do that they need to be able to do? Once users could make and receive calls reliably, the battery became the next wall. Once battery was addressed, the camera became visible. The roadmap revealed itself through use — you could not have planned it from inside the building in 2007.

---

## The mechanism: struggle reveals struggle

Why does the struggling roadmap work this way? Because users can only tell you about the struggle they are in right now. They cannot see around the corner.

Bobby made this point when discussing the six phases in Session 6. A person in passive looking does not experience the struggles of active looking yet. A first-time user does not experience the struggles of ongoing use. The phases are sequential, and the struggles are phase-specific.

This means:

- **Before launch:** you can interview non-consumers and early switchers to find the struggle that creates the first thought, the passive look, the active look, and the first hire decision.
- **After launch:** you have to go back and interview users who are now *in* consuming and ongoing use to find the struggles that live there.

The product did not exist before launch. The struggles in the later phases did not exist before launch either — or at least, they could not be surfaced with any precision. You had to build round one to make round two visible.

This is not a failure of planning. It is the logic of progress. Bobby:

> *"The struggling moment is the seed for all innovation. The struggling moment of the consumer causes them to think about doing something new — but it also is the seed for us to actually go build something new."*

The seed metaphor is exact. You plant the seed (ship the product). The plant grows (users adopt and use). The plant reveals what it needs next (water, light, space — which in product terms means: faster onboarding, clearer progress signals, better re-engagement). You address those needs. A new plant grows. And so on.

## AutoBooks: the struggling roadmap applied to sales

The Growth Summit slides gave a clean B2B example of how the struggling roadmap logic extends beyond the product itself into the entire customer acquisition process.

AutoBooks, a fintech company in Detroit, had a six-step sales process that ended in a product demo. The demo was a problem:

> *"Every 90 days they would end up re-working the demo because it did not work."*

The reason the demo did not work was a supply-side assumption: that all prospects were in the same place on the buying journey. AutoBooks was giving **one demo to everyone**, regardless of whether the prospect was in passive looking, active looking, or deciding.

Once the JTBD team mapped the buying process against the six consumer systems, the solution became obvious. Different phases = different struggling moments = different demos:

- **Demo 1 (Passive Looking / Learning How):** The goal is to create a question in the bank's mind. Don't show the product. Show the problem. "Does this struggling moment sound familiar?"
- **Demo 2 (Active Looking / Seeing Possibilities):** Show what's possible. Contrast against the current approach. Give the bank language to take back internally.
- **Demo 3 (Deciding / Making Trade-offs):** Show the specific trade-offs relevant to *this bank's* context. Help them commit.

The slides described the aim as: "almost beg for the next demo." Each demo was designed so that the prospect would naturally want to pull themselves forward to the next stage.

The results:

- **3x** lead conversion
- **Half the time** from lead to customer
- **6x** bank partners
- **9x** small business users
- **5x** revenue growth

The struggling roadmap was not applied to the product features. It was applied to the sales process. The insight was the same: match your intervention to the user's current struggling moment. Stop giving people things they are not yet ready for.

## The three tips as a synthesis

Bobby closed the Growth Summit with three tips. They are not new. They are the full curriculum, compressed:

**Tip 1: Find the struggling moments — current and future — inside and out.**

"Current" means the struggling moment that is causing users to look for you right now. "Future" means the struggling moment that will emerge once they've been using you for 30 days. "Inside" means what happens within your product. "Outside" means what happens in their life — the downstream context that the product is meant to serve.

This is Sessions 1, 2, 6, and 10 compressed into one instruction. Find the moment. Don't describe the product. Describe the wall.

**Tip 2: Think progress, not products or services.**

Bobby quoted Einstein on the slide: *"If I had an hour to solve a problem I'd spend 55 minutes thinking about the problem and 5 minutes thinking about solutions."*

This is Sessions 3–5 and 8 compressed. The four forces, the supply-demand gap, the non-consumption framing — all of it is in service of this one directive. Your product is not the point. The progress is the point. The product is the means.

**Tip 3: Identify the trade-offs — choose what to suck at.**

*"You cannot be good at everything. Know the things you can (should) be bad at, and manage appropriately."*

This is Sessions 16–19 compressed. Prototyping to learn, the rule of three, paired comparisons, the cinnamon gum principle — all of it lands here. The operating window. The decision. The conscious choice of who you are not building for.

## What a Memrise struggling roadmap would look like

Bobby confirmed during the January session at Memrise that the framework applies directly. Sonia said the Intercom example resonated with them — four distinct Jobs, four different products, four different competitive contexts.

Applying the struggling roadmap to Memrise means asking, for each of the Jobs the team has identified:

**1. What struggle creates the first thought?**
This is the marketing question. What context causes someone to realise that the way they're currently managing language learning (or ignoring it) is no longer good enough? What is the question Memrise could ask that creates the space in the mind? Casper asked "how did you sleep last night?" and didn't answer it. What's the Memrise equivalent?

**2. What struggle blocks the hire decision?**
This is the anxiety question. For the adult language learner who has tried apps before and quit, what is the anxiety that prevents them from committing again? What is the habit — the existing workaround or the resignation — that competes with hiring Memrise?

**3. What struggle surfaces in the first week of use?**
This is the early retention question. Users who survive the first week are dramatically more likely to stay. What is the wall that users hit in days 2–7? Bobby's language: what are the "early signs of progress" that users need to see to believe the Job is going to be done?

**4. What struggle surfaces at 30, 60, 90 days?**
This is the churn question. In the condo example, users came back after six weeks to cancel — not because they'd changed their mind about the destination, but because the journey had become overwhelming. What is the equivalent in Memrise? The moment the content runs out. The moment the routine breaks. The moment that the streak-based model stops feeling like progress and starts feeling like obligation.

**5. What struggle exists in non-consumers who are not even considering Memrise?**
This is the growth question. SNHU did not grow by taking students from other universities. They found people who had never even considered online education — adults who had written off learning entirely because of time, money, or the feeling that they were too far behind. What is the Memrise equivalent? The people who "have always wanted to learn a language" but have never downloaded an app — not because they tried and quit, but because they don't believe any tool can help them.

Each of these questions, answered with real interview data, is a row on the struggling roadmap. The order of priority is not set by internal ambition or competitive pressure. It is set by where the struggling moment is biggest and where addressing it would unlock the most progress for the most users.

## The full arc

Let's trace the curriculum from the beginning one more time, in the order Bobby intended it.

**The premise** (Session 1): People don't buy products. They hire them to make progress.

**The seed** (Session 2): The struggling moment is the seed for all innovation. Find the wall users are hitting. Start there.

**The fuel and the friction** (Sessions 3–5): The four forces. Push and pull are the fuel. Anxiety and habit are the friction. You need to both amplify the fuel and reduce the friction — and adding features usually increases anxiety, not decreases it.

**The timeline** (Sessions 6–7): The six phases of progress. Each phase is its own system, with its own struggling moments, its own inputs, its own signals of success. You have to match your intervention to the phase the user is actually in.

**The gap** (Session 8): Supply-side vs. demand-side. Companies talk about features. Users think about progress. The wall between them is the product-market disconnect. Crossing it requires you to learn to speak the user's language, not teach them yours.

**The growth lever** (Session 9): Non-consumption. The biggest market is always the people who are not using anything — not because they don't have the struggling moment, but because no existing product has solved it for their context: their time, their money, their access, their confidence.

**The investigative method** (Session 10): The JTBD interview. You're not running a survey. You're reconstructing a decision — understanding the full causal chain from first thought through hire. Who interviewed themselves into understanding? Who studied people who had already made the move?

**The case studies** (Sessions 12–15): Intercom, SNHU, AutoBooks, Memrise. In each case, the company stopped talking about their product and started talking about the user's struggling moment. In each case, growth followed.

**Prototyping to learn** (Sessions 16–18): Once you understand the Job, you have to learn what product serves it best. Prototyping is not verification — it is discovery. The rule of three: enough contrast to teach you something, not so much that the system becomes noise. The L4 array: four prototypes, six pairs, systematic learning.

**Trade-offs** (Session 19): Prototyping generates data. Trade-offs are the decision layer. You cannot satisfy everyone. Choose what to suck at — consciously, in service of the Job you have chosen to do, for the users you have chosen to serve.

**The roadmap** (Session 20): All of the above converges here. The struggling roadmap is not a deliverable. It is a practice. It is the discipline of returning, after every launch, to the question Bobby posed at the start: *what is the next set of struggling moments we need to go address?*

The question does not change. Your users' answers do. And following those answers — consistently, systematically, with curiosity rather than certainty — is how a product roadmap becomes a compounding asset rather than a project plan that is obsolete the day it is written.

## Three things to hold onto

1. **The roadmap is a question, not a plan.** "What is the next struggling moment we need to address?" That question, asked after every launch, with real user interviews to answer it, is more durable and more valuable than any feature backlog. The iPhone roadmap was not planned. It was discovered, one struggling moment at a time.

2. **Your users can only tell you about the struggle they are in right now.** This is not a limitation of users — it is a property of how progress works. You have to ship something to surface the next set of struggles. The struggling roadmap is iterative by design. You cannot front-load it.

3. **The entire JTBD toolkit is in service of this one output.** The four forces tell you what is causing the struggle. The six phases tell you where in the journey the struggle lives. The JTBD interview tells you how to uncover it. Prototyping tells you what to build in response. Trade-offs tell you what to let go. The struggling roadmap is where all of it lands — one prioritised list of walls to tear down, in the order your users most need them removed.

The curriculum is complete. But Bobby said something at the end of the January session that is worth sitting with as the final question:

> *"If there's only one thing you take away from this presentation, it should be the fact that the struggling moment is the seed for all innovation."*

Here is the final question:

> **If you had to identify the single struggling moment at Memrise right now — the one that, if addressed, would unlock the most growth — what would it be?**
>
> Not the most interesting one. Not the one with the most elegant solution. The one with the most energy behind it: the biggest push, the clearest pull, the most users hitting the same wall at the same time.
>
> Bobby's test: *Can you describe the context that puts someone in that struggle? Can you name the outcome they're trying to make? Can you say what existing behaviour they're using to cope with it today — and why that behaviour is no longer enough?*
