# JTBD — Session 13: Case Study: SNHU — Help Me Step It Up

> *"They ran that ad for one month. Instead of the usual 1,000–1,200 applications a year, they got 10,000 applications in one month."*
> — Bobby Moesta (27 January 2026, Memrise JTBD Session)

> *"It didn't need to be as good as Harvard. It just had to be better than nothing."*
> — Bobby Moesta

---

## The simplest true statement

A 2,500-student regional university in New Hampshire found that 80% of the students it most wanted to serve were all in the same struggling moment. One emotional ad, precisely aimed at that struggling moment, got 10x their normal annual applications in a single month. Then they rebuilt everything — admissions, advising, course design, cost — around that single job.

Today SNHU has 250,000 students. It is the largest university in the world.

This session is about how that happened, and what it reveals about the single most underestimated concept in JTBD: **non-consumption**.

---

## Where we are

You've now seen JTBD applied to a software company (Intercom), a mattress brand (Casper), and a property developer in Detroit. In every case, the pattern was the same: understand the struggling moment first, then let the product, the price, and the communication follow.

SNHU is different in one important way. Intercom's growth was about **finding the jobs that already existed** and organising around them. SNHU's growth was about **finding the people who wanted to make progress but had given up trying**.

That's non-consumption. And it's where Bobby says the real source of growth lives.

---

## The situation in 2008

Paul LeBlanc became president of SNHU — Southern New Hampshire University — in 2008. At the time, it had about 2,500 students. It was a perfectly decent regional accredited university, about 90 minutes north of Boston.

But Paul had noticed something. A group of students were enrolled but barely showing up. The university recorded lectures on video. Some students watched everything online, came in only for tests and quizzes, and clearly wanted nothing to do with the physical campus.

Paul didn't treat this as a problem to solve. He treated it as a signal to investigate.

Bobby had been teaching JTBD at Harvard with Clayton Christensen at the time. Paul found him after one of those sessions and said: *"I think I know what job I'm serving, but I'm not sure. Would you mind helping me understand what's going on with these students?"*

---

## The five jobs

The team went out and did interviews. A few more than the usual ten — the situation warranted it. They asked the same core question: *"Why are you here? What caused you to decide today was the day to go back to school?"*

Five patterns emerged.

**Job 1 — Help me escape.**
*"I'm in a job I hate. I need to become something else."*
These were career switchers. A teacher who wanted to become a nurse. A factory worker who wanted to move into tech support. The struggling moment was clear: I am in the wrong life, and school is the way out.

**Job 2 — Help me step it up.**
*"I have more responsibility now than I did before. I need to get to the next level."*
These were people who had tried school before — sometimes failed out, sometimes life got in the way — but now something had changed. A new child. A sick parent they had to take care of. A divorce that meant they were the only earner. Somehow, a new sense of responsibility had arrived and they couldn't stay where they were anymore. They weren't escaping — they were responding to a call.

**Job 3 — Help me extend myself.**
*"I love learning. I want to go deeper on something I'm already interested in."*
No specific career outcome. No urgency. Just genuine intellectual appetite. These people were hard to move because their progress had no clear finish line.

**Job 4 — Help me execute my plan.**
*"I know exactly what I want. I need the credential to get there."*
Clear-eyed, outcome-focused. Already knew the job title, the salary, the plan. School was a step in a sequence they had already mapped.

**Job 5 — Help me do what's expected of me.**
*"My family expects me to have a degree. My employer expects it. So here I am."*
External pressure rather than internal drive. These students had the credential as the goal, not the progress it represented.

---

## The number that changed everything

Bobby: *"Almost 80% of them were in Help Me Step It Up."*

Not escape. Not execute my plan. Not any of the others. Four out of five students were in one job — responding to a new responsibility they hadn't had before, trying to get from an hourly life to a salaried one, from stuck to somewhere.

This was a shock to the admissions team. When Bobby asked them what job they thought students were primarily in, they said: *"Help me execute my plan."* That's what they'd been designing for. That's the student they'd imagined — purposeful, directed, already decided.

The real student was something different. Uncertain. Scared. Had tried this before and it hadn't worked. Now had a kid at home and couldn't afford to stay still.

Bobby: *"The beauty of 'Help me step it up' was that they could see beyond school. They could see where they wanted to go — which meant the line was much easier to help navigate."*

The person trying to extend themselves, by contrast, had no destination. Nothing to navigate towards. No moment when progress could be declared complete.

This is the demand-side truth that the supply-side had entirely missed.

---

## The ad

In October 2011, Paul's team ran a test. They took the "Help me step it up" job and turned it into an emotional ad. They didn't talk about SNHU. They didn't mention accreditation or tuition or online flexibility. They just showed the struggling moment.

Bobby described it: a single parent, working an hourly factory job. Studies at night after putting the kids to bed. Studies during lunch breaks. Gets a phone call offering a better role. Takes it. The family celebrates.

*"You really don't even see SNHU until the end."*

That's it. Thirty seconds of someone living the struggling moment — and then the resolution. No features. No product demo. Just: here is someone like you, and here is what stepping it up looks like.

SNHU normally received 1,000 to 1,200 applications in a year.

In the one month they ran that ad, they received **10,000 applications**.

---

## What they changed — and why it mattered

Paul's response wasn't to run more ads. It was to ask a harder question: *"If these people want to do this and they want to do it on their own time, how do we change the institution to make it possible?"*

That question led to three structural changes. Each one targeted a specific form of friction — anxiety or habit — that was keeping non-consumers out.

**Change 1: The application process.**

The existing process took four months. There was a full admissions apparatus built around traditional students who needed housing, class scheduling, and in-person everything. None of that applied to online adults with full-time jobs.

Paul said he wanted to get it to one day. The admissions department said no — at best, maybe three months. Paul didn't argue. He built a small team in a corner to see what was actually possible.

What they built: an application process where a student could answer questions about their context and outcome on Monday or Tuesday, have their transcripts located, get approved, and have a plan to start by Friday.

The four-month process was four months of anxiety — a month per week of wondering if it was even going to happen, whether it was worth applying, whether the system wanted them. One day removed almost all of it.

**Change 2: Academic advisors became life coaches.**

The original advisors were what you'd expect: they told students what classes to take, helped with career planning, did the administrative choreography of getting a degree.

But the "Help me step it up" student doesn't live a linear academic life. They have divorces. They have bereavements. They get cancer. They have weeks where the child is sick and the job is threatening and there's no mental space left for coursework.

Paul's insight: we need to know they need a tutor before they know they need a tutor. We need to be their coach, not their administrator.

He changed the mandate for advisors entirely — they became advocates, partners, life coaches. He lost **90% of his original academic advisors** in the transition. Replaced them with people oriented to the job the students were actually in.

The result: churn dropped by almost **90%**.

Bobby: *"He lost 90% of his academic advisors and reduced churn by almost 90%. Think about what that means. The people he'd hired for the wrong job were producing the wrong outcome."*

**Change 3: Professors stopped designing courses.**

Paul took course design away from the faculty and gave it to instructional designers and adult learning experts. The question was no longer "what does the professor want to teach?" It was "how does an adult with a job and a family and a commute actually learn this material?"

Different question. Completely different course design. Paced, structured, built for intermittent attention rather than full immersion.

---

## The results

- **Applications:** From 1,000–1,200 per year to 10,000 in a single month
- **Students:** From 2,500 (500 online) to 250,000 — the largest university in the world
- **Cost:** Reduced by 70%
- **Churn:** Dropped nearly 90% after the life coach switch
- **Infrastructure:** Paul had to build a **stadium** for graduation, because the only time most students ever came to campus was to receive their degree

Bobby: *"He retired last year. And the reason I tell this story is the non-consumption angle. He realised: how many people want to go back to school but can't? That number is massive."*

---

## What is non-consumption, exactly?

Non-consumption is when someone wants to make progress but **can't** — because of **access, knowledge, money, or inconvenience**.

Not because they don't want to. Because they've hit a wall that makes progress feel impossible.

The traditional university market was full of consumption — 18-to-22-year-olds going to campus, living in dorms, following the standard path. SNHU competed in that market and was, at 2,500 students, doing fine. Unremarkable, but fine.

The non-consumption market was something else entirely: adults who had wanted to finish their degree for years, had maybe tried and failed, had life circumstances that made the traditional model impossible, and had eventually just stopped trying. Not because they'd given up on the goal — but because the product that existed didn't fit their life.

Bobby: *"It didn't need to be as good as Harvard. It just had to be better than nothing."*

That's the non-consumption bar. Not excellence. Not superiority. Just: good enough that people who had previously been locked out can finally get in.

Intercom was not the best CRM in the world. Casper was not the best mattress. The transistor radio sounded like crap. The iPhone camera was inferior to a proper DSLR. In every case, the product that tapped non-consumption was inferior to the existing market leader — but it was accessible to people for whom the market leader was out of reach.

Once you reach those people, the competition changes. You're not competing with Harvard. You're competing with **not going back to school at all**. That's a very different bar to clear.

---

## The contrast with the supply-side view

The admissions team had built their entire process for the student they imagined: organised, directed, executing a plan. Four months seemed reasonable because that's how long the process had always taken, and for an 18-year-old applying in the spring to start in September, it didn't matter much.

For a 34-year-old single parent who finally worked up the courage to apply in October, four months means: four months of wondering if I'm even going to get in, four months of paying full rent and working full hours with no status change, four months in which life can intervene and pull you back. The anxiety was built into the process itself.

Paul saw this because he started from the demand side. Not "how do we fill the pipeline?" but "what is actually stopping people from being here?"

The question revealed the four-month application as a feature of the supply side that was invisible friction on the demand side.

---

## The Memrise resonance

Bobby made this observation at the end of the session, in the context of the question Sonia raised about time and motivation as barriers:

*"Some of what you're describing — users saying they don't have time, they lack motivation — those are elements of non-consumption. The question is whether we need to make the struggle bigger, or whether we need to remove the wall that's stopping them from starting."*

These are two completely different interventions, and they're easy to confuse:

- **Make the struggle bigger:** Help users feel the cost of not making progress. What happens if you don't learn this language? What do you miss out on? What does it cost you? (This is Bobby's suggestion around Sonia's question — sometimes the push isn't strong enough, so you have to surface the negative consequences of staying still.)

- **Remove the wall:** Identify what's creating the anxiety or inconvenience that's blocking them and eliminate it. The four-month application was a wall. The academic advisor model was a wall. The course design built for 20-year-olds living on campus was a wall.

At Memrise, users who say "I don't have time" may be experiencing both: not enough push (the progress they want feels abstract or distant) and too much wall (sessions that are too long, onboarding that requires effort before they feel anything). The "Help me step it up" student at SNHU felt both — the push was their new responsibility, the wall was the traditional university model.

The question SNHU asked — and the one Memrise could ask — is: who wants to make this progress but has stopped trying, because the product as it exists doesn't fit their life?

## Three things to hold onto

1. **Non-consumption is the real source of growth — not stealing share from competitors.** SNHU didn't take students from Harvard. They reached people who had stopped trying. The transistor radio didn't take listeners from hi-fi audiophiles. It gave radio to people who'd never had it. If you're fighting over the existing market, you're ignoring the bigger one.

2. **80% in one job is your signal to go all-in on that job.** SNHU had five jobs. Four of them mattered. One of them was the market. When Bobby saw 80% in "Help me step it up," the strategic question became obvious: design everything for this person. Not the other four. This one. The ad, the application, the advising, the course design, the cost. All of it aimed at one struggling moment.

3. **Anxiety built into your process is indistinguishable from rejection.** The four-month application wasn't designed to discourage people. It was designed for a different customer. But for the non-consumer — the person who'd built up the courage to apply and was already expecting to be turned away — a four-month wait felt like a door closing. Removing the anxiety (one day instead of four months) removed a barrier that no amount of better marketing could have overcome. When you find non-consumers, look at your process, not just your product.

## Open Question

> The SNHU admissions team believed 80% of students were in "Help me execute my plan." Bobby's interviews showed 80% were in "Help me step it up." Both descriptions involve a student returning to school. From the outside, they look the same. But they have completely different emotional content, different anxieties, different pull, different walls.
>
> The admissions team had spoken to thousands of students. Bobby spoke to fewer than fifteen. And he found something the admissions team had missed entirely.
>
> **Why?**
>
> Here's one hypothesis: the admissions team was listening for what people *said* they wanted. Bobby was listening for what *caused* them to act. "Execute my plan" is what people say when they're presenting a coherent, confident self. "Step it up" is what you find when you ask: *"But why now? What changed?"*
>
> Think about how Memrise currently describes the people who use it — the personas, the marketing copy, the onboarding questions. Are those descriptions built from what users *say* their goal is, or from what caused them to *act*? Are they supply-side approximations of demand-side reality?
>
> And: if Bobby ran ten interviews with people who had recently **churned** from Memrise — not cancelled out of laziness, but genuinely tried and then stopped — what do you think he'd find? What struggling moment had Memrise failed to serve?
