# JTBD — Session 19: Trade-offs — Choose What to Suck At

> *"Trade-offs — how to make trade-offs — because that's the essential part of doing these three things."*
> — Bobby Moesta, February 26 2026

> *"You want to make it work for everybody. But you can't. It's impossible."*
> — Bobby Moesta, same session

---

## The simplest true statement

You cannot satisfy everybody. Trying to will make your product worse than accepting that you can't.

That is the whole session. Everything else — the cinnamon gum story, sensory segments, the Duolingo diagnosis, the "false notion" — is an unpacking of why that statement is true and what the alternative looks like in practice.

---

## Where we are

Sessions 1 through 18 have built the full JTBD toolkit: the struggling moment, the four forces, the six phases, the supply-demand gap, the JTBD interview, and three sessions on prototyping — the four roles, the rule of three, the L4 array, paired comparisons.

There is one question left unasked at the end of all that: once you have the data — once you know what people prefer and what they reject — what do you *do* with it?

The answer is trade-offs. This is the decision layer. Prototyping generates information. Trade-offs convert that information into product decisions.

Bobby named this explicitly at the start of the February session, before anything else:

> *"Some are better for one thing and some might be better for another thing and you don't know how to make that decision. So we'll frame systems thinking, we'll frame prototyping to learn, and then we'll frame trade-offs — how to make trade-offs — because that's the essential part of doing these three things."*

---

## The scientist trap

Most product people are trained, either formally or culturally, to look for the one right answer. Run the test. Find the winner. Implement it.

Bobby named this as a specific trap:

> *"I feel like we've been trained as scientists that we should find one answer. And at some point in time in product, sometimes there's two answers. And so we have to realise that we want to look at the data and kind of say: well, like, if I look at the averages — here's the things they have in common — but like as you do more of them and you do the pairing, the fact is you'll actually start to learn about not only what makes people like it more, but what actually makes them hate it more."*

This is subtle. The scientist frame says: converge. Find the answer. The JTBD frame says: look at the distribution. Find the pattern. There may not be one answer — there may be two, or three, or a split that tells you something more important than a winner.

---

## Sensory segments: when the data splits

Harry Snow showed Bobby a real split: two users, different prototypes, different preferences. One preferred app-generated vocab. One preferred AI-prompted, user-chosen vocab. Harry's instinct was to search for the answer — which one is right?

Bobby gave the split a name: sensory segments.

> *"This brings up a really important aspect. We're looking for the optimum, but ultimately we might actually find what we call sensory segments. And so you might actually end up having to have both features — because some people want to generate it, some want you to generate it."*

Sensory segments are preference splits that are real and durable — not noise, not small-sample error, but genuine differences in what users need from the same product. When you find a sensory segment, the trade-off decision isn't "which one wins." It's: do we build optionality into the product (let the user choose), or do we pick one segment and serve them best, accepting we'll lose the other?

That is a hard decision. Bobby is not pretending it is easy. But it is the right decision — and it can only be made with the data that paired comparisons give you. Without the contrast, you'd never even know the split exists.

> *"We've been trained as scientists to find one answer. But sometimes there's two."*

---

## The cinnamon gum principle

Here is the insight that changes how you read product data.

Most product teams optimise for love. More stars. Higher NPS. Bigger fans. The belief underneath this is that the more people love your product, the more they use it and the more it grows.

Bobby's experience at Wrigley refuted this directly:

> *"The more I actually make people like it, the more I would actually make people hate it. So because there's this preference notion — if I just minimise the number of people who hate it, all of a sudden there's a whole bigger market out there that will take it. And this is what I did in gum. Some people loved really, really hot cinnamon and some wanted more. So the hotter I made it, the more people loved it — but there was also more people who hated it. And so what I actually ended up doing is realising: how do I just minimise the people who hate it?"*

> *"Cinnamon gum is the most popular of all gums because it's actually set at a point that nobody really loves it, but nobody really hates it."*

Read that again. The most popular gum in the world is not anyone's favourite gum. It is the gum that the fewest people actively reject.

The implication for product teams: your minus scores are at least as important as your plus scores. When you run paired comparisons, the prototype that consistently gets a minus — the one people avoid, the one they rank last — is telling you something just as valuable as the one that gets all the pluses.

Bobby made this explicit when coaching Harry on how to evaluate his four prototypes:

> *"Which one did they hate more — one or three? And so part of it is if we can get them to rank order them, we actually might get a better understanding of like what's there — because again, it might not be about making it so they all like it. It's about just getting rid of the stuff that they don't like."*

---

## The false notion

Bobby named this as a "false notion" — one of the few times in any session where he used that phrase. That framing is deliberate. He is not saying this is a common mistake. He is saying it is a *category error*:

> *"The false notion is: the more they like it, the more they'll use it. It's just not true."*

Why not? Because the decision to use a product is not purely driven by how good it feels. It is driven by whether the product gets the Job done — whether it creates the progress the user is trying to make. A product people love but that doesn't move them forward in their life will not retain them. A product that feels unremarkable but reliably delivers the outcome they care about will.

This is the supply-side/demand-side gap from Session 8, showing up in the prototyping data. Product teams, working supply-side, optimise for what feels good. Users, working demand-side, continue using what makes progress.

Bobby's correction: when you ask people which prototype they prefer, the better question is:

> *"Which one do you believe would give you the best progress for you?"*

That question aligns the evaluation with the Job. Love is a signal. Progress is the measure.

## The operating window

Bobby used Sandra Zavala's "fun and progress" job for language learners to introduce the trade-off problem concretely. Sandra was trying to find what "fun" meant for her user segment. Bobby reframed the goal:

> *"You stop prototyping when you can predict what people will say — because then you know what's going to happen. And so now it's a question of: what are the trade-offs you have to make? Because you can't satisfy everybody, and not everybody has the same definition of fun. But how do I maximise the window so I have the minimum amount of people who say it's not fun, and the minimum amount of people who say it's too much fun? This is why trade-offs is so important — because you want to make it work for everybody, but you can't. It's impossible."*

The "window" is the core deliverable of prototyping. You are not looking for an optimum. You are looking for a range — a region in the design space where the minimum number of people hate the product.

Bobby drew this out as two axes for Sandra: fun (too little to too much) and progress (too little to too much). The operating window is the intersection of "enough fun" and "enough progress" without crossing into "too much" on either dimension. A product that lives inside that window won't excite everyone — but it won't lose anyone either.

This is the trade-off. To widen the operating window, you accept that you will not maximise any single dimension. You will not be the funniest. You will not be the most rigorous. You will be inside the window — and inside the window is where growth lives.

---

## Fun is a mechanism: the danger of pabulum words

One of Bobby's most important practical warnings came during the same Sandra discussion. The word "fun" was causing the team trouble — it was appearing in every user interview but meant something different every time.

Bobby's diagnosis: "fun" is a pabulum word. It sounds specific but is actually a package:

> *"Words are just packages of emotions and actions. And how do we actually figure out what are the actions and emotions that are compacted into the word that they say is fun?"*

> *"The challenge I would give every one of you: the moment somebody says 'I just want it to be fun,' the next question is: what kind of fun are we talking about? Are we talking about Christmas morning fun? Are we talking about playing on the playground fun? Are we talking about reading a book fun? Let them give you language of the kind of fun they mean — because it will lead us to where we really need to go."*

Sandra gave a perfect example of this working. She had asked a Norwegian user what other things in his life felt fun. He talked about a ukulele he'd commissioned from a maker — told the maker "I don't care if it takes 10 years." His definition of fun was patient, craft-oriented, intrinsically motivated. No streak. No gamification. No external pressure. The opposite of Duolingo.

Bobby's conclusion:

> *"It's dangerous for us to interpret what fun is. It's up to us to elicit the kind of fun they like — what causes them to say it's fun. And then: what happens that makes something that's fun not fun anymore? Because it's almost like a state, and we have to understand the causal mechanisms of 'it's fun in the beginning but it's not fun in the end.' Fun is a mechanism. We have to figure out what it is."*

The trade-off decision depends on which definition of fun you're designing for. Get it wrong and you're building inside the wrong operating window entirely.

---

## Duolingo — irrational behaviour becomes rational with context

The best case study for trade-off thinking came from Kasia Bania's interview with a long-term Duolingo user: 500-day streak, cannot say a sentence in Italian. She described the user as "not going to switch."

Bobby's diagnosis:

> *"Irrational behaviour becomes rational with context. So if we hear something from them that sounds irrational, it actually means we don't understand the full story well enough. Because once you understand the full story, all of a sudden the irrational becomes very rational."*

> *"It's like: I'm just doing this because it's a routine. And I have a commitment. And I have this. So when you really want to learn language, come to Memrise. If you just want to do a morning routine, Duolingo works great for you. Like, call it out."*

This is trade-off thinking applied to competitive positioning. Duolingo is not a language learning app. For most of its users, it is a morning routine app — a habit vehicle that happens to use language as the content. If Memrise is trying to take that user from Duolingo, it is fighting on the wrong battleground. The correct trade-off is: Memrise does not compete for the routine job. Memrise does the learning job.

Bobby:

> *"We see Duolingo as a competitor as it relates to language learning. But they might not be in the language learning business at all. Or 80% of the learners are just looking for a morning routine."*

The trade-off Memrise makes by focusing on actual language progress is that it will lose users who want gamification and streaks. That is not a failure. That is a choice. And "choose what to suck at" is the discipline that makes that choice consciously, rather than by accident.

## Three things to hold onto

1. **You cannot satisfy everybody. This is not a problem to solve; it is a constraint to design within.** The trade-off decision is: which people do you accept losing, in exchange for serving the people you keep, better? Making this decision consciously — explicitly — is the difference between a focused product and an incoherent one.

2. **The cinnamon gum principle: minimise hatred, not maximise love.** The minus scores in your paired comparisons matter as much as the plus scores. The most popular product in a category is often not the most loved — it is the most broadly tolerable. When you push hard to maximise love, you will produce more hate simultaneously. The goal is the widest possible operating window, not the highest peak.

3. **Words like "fun" are pabulum. Get past them.** "Fun," "simple," "engaging," "relevant" — these words sound like insights and contain no information. Every user uses them to mean something different. Your job is to elicit the specific definition behind the word. What kind of fun? Compared to what? What makes it stop being fun? The word is just the door. The trade-off decision lives behind it.

## Open Question

Bobby said the moment you can predict what people will say is the moment to stop prototyping and start making trade-offs.

Here is the question to sit with:

> **For Sandra's "fun and progress" job at Memrise — do you think the team is at the prediction threshold yet? Can they look at a new prototype and say with confidence "this will fall outside the operating window"?**
>
> And if not, what's missing? Is it sample size (more interviews)? Is it contrast (prototypes not different enough)? Or is it something earlier — that "fun" hasn't been unpacked past the pabulum level, so the variables being tested aren't actually the right variables?
>
> Bobby's test: *Can you predict the response before the test runs?* If you can, stop prototyping and decide. If you can't, you haven't reached the operating window yet — keep going, but go wider.
