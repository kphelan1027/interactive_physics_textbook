# JTBD — Session 12: Case Study: Intercom — Four Jobs, 500% Growth in 18 Months

> *"They actually had four really, really different products — competing with different companies in each job category."*
> — Bobby Moesta (27 January 2026, Memrise JTBD Session)

> *"The moment they went back to talking about struggling moments — acquire, engage, support — that's when everything turned around."*
> — Bobby Moesta

---

## The simplest true statement

Intercom had one product, one price, and four completely different kinds of customers. Nobody knew it. Not even Intercom.

When Bobby interviewed ten of their customers in 2013, none of them were using Intercom the same way. One was using it to convert website visitors into buyers. One was using it to stop customers from churning. One was using it to understand which features were broken. One was using it to stop their support queue from collapsing.

Same product. Same features. Same price. Four entirely different jobs. Four entirely different competitors. Four entirely different struggling moments.

What happened when Intercom figured that out is one of the cleanest demonstrations of JTBD in action. In 18 months they grew 5× in customers and 3× in sales. They went from $5M to on track for $75M by September 2015.

What happened in 2018, when a new CMO decided to go back to talking about features, is equally instructive. Sales went down.

This session is the whole story.

---

## Where we are

You've now seen every major framework Bobby uses:
- The struggling moment as the seed of demand
- The four forces as the engine of behaviour change
- The six phases as the map of decision-making
- Supply-side vs demand-side as two completely different ways of seeing the same product
- Non-consumption as the largest untapped market in most categories

Intercom is where all of it lands at once. It's not a thought experiment. It happened, in real time, and you can trace the cause and effect.

---

## The founding premise: the iPhone of SaaS tools

Dez Trainer co-founded Intercom in 2010. He had built two startups before. Both times, he'd had to buy marketing automation, a customer database, live chat, email, a help desk — all separately — and wire them all together.

His insight: *"What if all the tools talked to each other in one place?"* It was the iPhone logic: not the best at any one thing, but good enough at all of them with the data in one place. He aimed it at startups, who were proliferating fast and needed the full stack without enterprise pricing.

The original tagline: *"Manage your customer from cradle to grave."*

That tagline was the problem.

---

## The growth stall

Year one (2012): $3.5M in sales. Year two: $5M. They had expected $10M. The engine wasn't firing.

Dez called Bobby: *"I think I know what the job is, but I'm not sure. Would you mind talking to some customers?"*

Bobby's approach was the same as always. He didn't interview 50 or 100 people. He found ten customers — diverse, from very different companies and contexts — and asked a simple question:

**"Why in the world did you buy Intercom?"**

---

## The four patterns

Ten interviews. Four unmistakable patterns.

**Job 1 — Acquire:**
*"I have a lot of people coming to the website but nobody is actually converting. I use Intercom to chat with them in real time, answer their questions, and walk them through the process to become a customer."*

The struggling moment: website traffic with zero conversion. The progress: acquiring customers. The competitor: HubSpot.

**Job 2 — Engage:**
*"I have more than enough people signing up, but they're not sticking around. I need to onboard them properly and make sure they keep coming back — because though they might have bought it, the value is created by them using it on a regular basis."*

The struggling moment: strong top of funnel, terrible retention. The progress: keeping customers engaged and building habit. The competitor: a different set of tools entirely.

**Job 3 — Observe:**
*"We use it to watch the customer inside the app. We can see where people struggle. It tells us which features to build next."*

The struggling moment: flying blind on product decisions. The progress: using customer behaviour data as a product roadmap. No obvious competitor — this was more of an internal use case than a paid job.

**Job 4 — Support:**
*"When we went from one person running support to two, everything broke. We needed a proper way to manage and route tickets before we completely lost control."*

The struggling moment: scaling from ad-hoc support to a manageable system. The progress: handling support load without things collapsing. The competitor: Zendesk.

---

## What four jobs meant

Bobby's synthesis: *"They actually had four really, really different products."*

Not four features of one product. Four different products. Each one:
- Competed with a different company
- Served a different struggling moment
- Was used by a different person in the organisation (sales, customer success, product, support)
- Required different onboarding, different metrics, different marketing

The single-price, all-features bundle made sense from the supply side. It made no sense from the demand side.

Here's how bad the mismatch was: when a salesperson sat down with a prospect who wanted Intercom for Acquire, they showed them all the features. Support features. Observe features. Engage features. Things the customer didn't need and didn't understand. The prospect's response was rational: *"I only need some of this — can we get a discount?"*

Bobby's framing: *"People were asking for a discount not because they didn't value it — it's because they'd been shown an overengineered product."*

They were paying for four products when they needed one.

---

## The three changes

By Q2 2014 — roughly 90 days after Bobby's work — Intercom made three changes.

**Change 1: Segment the marketing by job, not by feature.**

Instead of listing what Intercom could do, they started leading with the struggling moment:
- *"Are people coming to your site but not converting?"* → Acquire
- *"Are people signing up but not sticking around?"* → Engage
- *"Is your support ticket load getting out of control?"* → Support

If a visitor wasn't in one of these struggling moments, they'd bounce. That was the right outcome. Intercom stopped trying to be everything to everyone. They let people self-select into the job they actually had.

Bobby's observation on the economics: *"Problem keywords were 50% cheaper than feature keywords."* Intercom's competitors were bidding on feature keywords ("live chat software", "customer database"). Nobody was bidding on the problem keywords ("people coming to our site aren't converting", "customer churn too high"). The demand was there. Nobody was talking to it.

**Change 2: Show only the features relevant to the job.**

When someone clicked into Acquire, they saw the Acquire features. Not engage features. Not support. The features that were irrelevant to their job became invisible — which meant no discount requests, because there was nothing extraneous to discount.

They also built a bridge: if you outgrew Intercom and wanted to move to HubSpot, you could export your data easily. They borrowed this from Basecamp. It removed anxiety of lock-in. It made Intercom feel safe to start with.

**Change 3: Charge separately for each product.**

One price for Acquire. A separate price for Engage. A separate price for Support. Observe was made a free tool — and eventually dropped, because people weren't willing to pay for it and it didn't actually help them make progress directly. It turned out to be a demand-side insight, not a monetisable product.

Pricing Acquire at 20% less than HubSpot and Support at 20% less than Zendesk wasn't a race to the bottom. It was a positioning move: Intercom was simpler to set up, easier to start with, and easy to graduate out of when you grew. *"Half a step toward HubSpot"* — exactly right for early-stage startups who couldn't afford HubSpot yet.

---

## The results

- **5× growth in customers** over 18 months
- **3× growth in sales** (because once a company used Acquire, they'd develop the Engage problem, then the Support problem — natural expansion)
- **$5M → on track for $75M by September 2015**

The CMO at the time, Matt Hodgees, gave a talk about it. Bobby paraphrases: *"You could say that was me, but it's not."* He credits JTBD explicitly as the frame.

Intercom used half the Series A capital for technology. The other half: running conferences worldwide on onboarding, jobs to be done, customer success — teaching customers how to use the product in the context of their job. This is supply-side investment that directly serves the demand side. They became a community, not just a software vendor.

---

## What happened in 2018 — and why it matters

In 2018, Intercom raised a large Series B. The founding CMO left. A new CMO came in with a different view: let's go back to talking about the product.

The new positioning: *"Live chat. Onboarding and retention. Customer support."*

Sales went down. Bobby's explanation: *"It didn't resonate with people in the right way, because nobody really knew what that was and how it worked — and it made them sound like everybody else."*

They were describing features and categories. Their customers only understood problems. When the language shifted from struggling moments to product capabilities, the demand-side connection broke.

By 2020, Intercom returned to the original framing: Acquire, Engage, Support. The jobs. The struggling moments.

By 2023: **$1.3 billion valuation.**

---

## The AI transition — and why it worked

Bobby: *"They didn't do AI for AI's sake. They basically built AI to help with exactly these things — acquire customers, engage customers, support customers."*

Their AI product is called Finn. It's consistently reviewed as one of the top AI agents in the support category. What made it work?

They didn't build a general-purpose AI. They focused Finn on specific struggling moments within the support job: handling repetitive queries, routing complex ones, giving agents context faster. They marketed it the same way they'd always marketed everything — not by talking about the technology, but by talking about the struggling moments Finn solved.

Bobby: *"They are no longer a SaaS company — they are an AI business."*

The transition worked because the foundation — understanding the jobs — never changed. The technology changed. The jobs didn't.

---

## How they organised around the jobs

Post-2015, Intercom had five teams:
- **Acquire team** — product, marketing, sales, support for the Acquire job
- **Engage team** — same, for Engage
- **Support team** — same, for Support
- **Observe team** — maintained as a thin, free offering
- **Infrastructure team** — platform, shared services

People would move from one job team to another to learn the full business before becoming an executive. This is an unusual org design. It's possible only if you know what the jobs are. Without the job architecture, there's no natural unit of organisation — everything bleeds into everything.

The org chart followed the demand side. That's rare, and it's what Bobby means when he says the company that stayed closest to JTBD sustained its performance.

## Three things to hold onto

1. **One product can have multiple jobs — and if you don't know which job a customer is in, you'll accidentally sell them the wrong product at the wrong price.** Intercom was charging one price for four products. The customers knew something was off (they asked for discounts). Intercom didn't know why until they looked at the demand side.

2. **Problem keywords are 50% cheaper than feature keywords — because nobody else is bidding on them.** Marketing to struggling moments is more accurate and more efficient than marketing to features. The people who need your product most are searching for their problem, not your solution. If you lead with the problem, you find them at the point of highest motivation.

3. **The jobs don't change when the technology does.** Intercom's AI transition worked because Finn was built to serve existing struggling moments within established jobs — not because AI was impressive. The technology was new. The jobs were the same ones they'd understood since 2013. Any company that anchors product decisions to jobs can absorb technological change without losing the thread.
