# JTBD — Session 4: Push and Pull — The Fuel

> *"Questions create spaces in the brain for solutions to fall into."*
> — Clay Christensen, as quoted by Bobby Moesta (Jan 27, 2026, Memrise session)

---

## The simplest true statement

Push and pull are the fuel for change. Push creates a question in someone's mind. Pull fills that question with a vision of what progress could look like. You need both. Push alone produces complaints. Pull alone produces daydreams. When push and pull act together, at the right intensity, in the same moment — that's when someone actually moves.

---

## A note on where we are

In Session 3 we surveyed all four forces. This session goes deep on just the first two: push and pull. These are the *fuel* side of the equation — the forces that promote a new choice. (Anxiety and habit, the friction side, get their own session next.)

Bobby's own framing from the Growth Summit slides:
- **Push of the Situation** = "Make it better" — the problem-solving impulse
- **Pull of New Solution** = "Relevant news" — the attractive vision of what's possible

They work in tandem around the struggling moment. The struggling moment is the place Bobby keeps returning to: it's the gap between where someone is and where they want to be. Push is what makes the gap feel unbearable. Pull is what makes the other side feel real.

---

## Push: the situation that builds

The push of the situation is **not** a problem with a product. It is a problem with a *situation*. This distinction is important enough that Bobby stated it explicitly in the Memrise session and it shows up throughout every case study he uses.

Here's the condo example. When do your parents decide to move out of the house they raised you in?

Not because they've been thinking for years that the house is "suboptimal." The push isn't an abstract dissatisfaction. It accumulates from a series of very specific situational irritants:

> *"The kids have left home. The house is way bigger than they need. They had four or five bedrooms, a lot of upkeep, yards. And all of a sudden it's like, I don't want to do the yard work anymore. We can't travel because I can't leave the house for two or three weeks."*
> — Bobby Moesta (Jan 27, 2026)

Lawn maintenance. Travel restrictions. The upkeep of space they no longer use. No single one of these is a crisis. Together they form a weight. At some point the weight gets heavy enough that the question appears in their mind: *should we actually look at this?*

That is the push. A situation-driven question, not a feature-driven complaint.

Bobby's framing language for push: **"When I am..."**

- *"When the house is too big..."*
- *"When I don't want to do the outside maintenance work..."*
- *"When I want to travel but I can't..."*

Notice the language is empathetic. It places you inside their experience. It describes what they can't do or what they have to endure. No product attribute appears anywhere in a push statement. Products don't push people. *Situations* push people.

---

## "Bitching ain't switching"

Bobby's best line — and it's worth understanding precisely:

> *"This is where your parents will complain about the house, but they won't do anything. All they do is complaining, complaining, complaining."*

Push creates the question in the brain. It does *not* create the answer. Without a pull — without a concrete, credible vision of what progress could look like — the question just sits there unanswered, and people vent. They complain. They wish things were different. They do not change.

This is the failure mode of most "pain-focused" product marketing. You get very good at amplifying the problem. You make people aware of exactly how much their situation is costing them. You articulate their struggle in terms they recognise. And then... nothing. Because you haven't given them anywhere to go. You've pushed without pulling.

Bobby's phrase — *"bitching ain't switching"* — is a short description of an extremely common product and marketing failure.

---

## Push activates the first phase of progress

In the six-phase model from Session 3, the first phase is "first thought." This is the moment the question first appears. Push is what generates it.

Bobby connects this directly to Clay Christensen's observation:

> *"Questions create spaces in the brain for solutions to fall into."*

This is beautifully precise. Before someone has a first thought, they are not available for your product. They're not looking. They're not evaluating. They're not even passive recipients of your message. The space doesn't exist.

Push creates the space. It makes the brain available.

Bobby's advertising insight from the condo business makes the point sharply. He moved his ads from the real estate section of the newspaper — full of people already in active looking mode — all the way back to the *obituaries*. Because that's where people were having first thoughts.

> *"When we went to our friend John's funeral... on the way home we had the conversation: we should probably look at this, because I don't want to move without you and you don't want to move without me. We need to figure this out."*

The push event was the funeral. Not the product. Not the advertising. A friend dying, and the practical, emotional realisation that followed. Bobby didn't create that push. He found where it was happening — and showed up there.

Result: **38% increase in traffic, 70% reduction in advertising cost**.

The ad in the obituaries didn't describe condos. It described the struggling moment:

> *"Need to move — not sure how to do it. We have a solution team that can help you."*

It spoke to the push, not the product. And it worked because the people reading the obituaries were, in many cases, having exactly that first thought.

---

## Pull: the vision of progress

Pull is the other side of the fuel equation. If push creates the question, pull has to create a credible answer — a vision of what progress looks like on the other side.

Bobby's framing language for pull: **"So I can..."**

- *"So I can have single-floor living..."*
- *"So I don't have to navigate the stairs..."*
- *"So I can travel without worrying about the house..."*

These are outcomes in the context of someone's life. Not features. Not specifications. Not technical descriptions of a product. They describe what life is *like* on the other side of the decision.

The condo pull moment in Bobby's example is the couple who pull into the development saying they're "just looking" or "here for decorating ideas." Both are comfortable lies. What's actually happening:

> *"Oh my gosh, this is perfect. It's got the gourmet kitchen. You know what? The old house needs a roof — we don't have to pay for the roof anymore because it's part of the homeowners association. And you start to realize that ultimately the fact is there's this fuel to say like this is something I want to do."*

Walk through what just happened. The gourmet kitchen mattered — not because of the kitchen, but because of what it represents about their life in that space. "No roof costs" mattered — not as a contractual technicality, but because it removed a looming anxiety about future obligation. "Single-floor living" mattered — not as an architectural feature, but because of what it enables, physically and practically, as they get older.

Every pull statement is an outcome, in context, in their life. Full stop.

---

## Context and outcome — not pain and gain

This is where Bobby explicitly corrects conventional marketing thinking. In the transcript, he pauses on the push/pull dynamic and says:

> *"Most people would say that's pain and gain. But I would tell you it's more context and outcome. Being able to understand context and outcome."*

The difference is subtle but consequential.

**Pain and gain** frames the story from the product's perspective. You have a problem (pain). My product solves it (gain). This is still supply-side thinking dressed up in customer language.

**Context and outcome** frames the story from the customer's life. They are in a specific situation (context: kids have left home, yard is too much, travel is restricted). They are trying to reach a specific state (outcome: freedom to travel, less maintenance, a home where the kids can still visit).

The product appears only in the gap between those two things. It's a means to progress, not the point.

Bobby's Growth Summit slides make this structural:

> *A JTBD is: "...the PROGRESS that a person is trying to make in a particular struggling CIRCUMSTANCE."*

Struggling circumstance = context. Progress = outcome. This is the grammar of demand.

When you write from context and outcome, you're speaking the customer's language. When you write from pain and gain, you're speaking your product's language and hoping it translates. It usually doesn't — or it translates into something generic, because "pain and gain" could describe almost any product.

## The Casper masterclass: creating push without solving it

Bobby used Casper's marketing as a clean example of how to deliberately activate push — to create a question in the brain without immediately answering it.

The struggling moment for Casper wasn't "I need a better mattress." It was deeper:

> *"Most people are so afraid of it because they really don't know how to buy a mattress. They don't know what the mattress is... they only buy it once every five to fifteen years."*

The real struggle was the buying process itself. Unfamiliar. High stakes. High effort. Difficult to evaluate. A market of non-consumers — people who wanted a new mattress but were blocked by access, knowledge, or inconvenience.

Casper's marketing question: **"How did you sleep last night?"**

Bobby's analysis:

> *"[They] just keep asking that question — how did you sleep last night? The reality is it creates the space. When the answer is 'I didn't sleep well,' the fact is they start to think about what they have to do."*

If the answer is "fine, actually" — no space opens. You bounce off. But if the answer is "terrible, I've been waking up at 3am for six months" — the question lands differently. The space opens. And now the person is available. The pull can begin.

This is deliberate push activation. Casper didn't tell you your mattress was bad. They asked a question that made *you* discover whether your mattress was bad. The push was already latent in many people's experience — they just hadn't articulated it as a purchasing problem. Casper gave the push a name.

## Why more pull (more features) often weakens the fuel

This is the counterintuitive moment from Session 3 that belongs here too, because it's specifically about the pull side:

> *"What I learned in business school is they told me just add more features and people will buy. Well, that's actually not true. The more features I add, the more anxiety I get — because I don't need all those features."*

Pull has to be *specific* to work. A vague vision of progress doesn't pull anyone anywhere. "The best mattress for you" is not a pull. "90-day returns, ships to your door, good/better/best — pick one" is a pull, because it resolves the specific context that was blocking non-consumers: the buying process itself.

When you add features to increase pull, what you often actually do is:
1. Dilute the specificity of the pull (now it's about more things, which means it's less clearly about *their* thing)
2. Increase complexity, which increases anxiety
3. Create the impression that you're overbuilding for a need they don't actually have

Bobby's observation from Intercom is worth repeating here: when customers saw all four jobs bundled into one product at one price, they asked for a discount — not because they didn't value the product, but because they didn't need 75% of it. The excess didn't add pull. It signalled mismatch.

The pull has to match the push. You're not trying to show the customer everything you can do. You're trying to show them that you can help them cross their specific river.

## The AutoBooks evidence

Bobby's Growth Summit included the AutoBooks case study, which is a B2B version of the same dynamic. Their problem was a demo that kept failing — they'd rebuild it every 90 days trying to get it to land.

The JTBD diagnosis: they had three different kinds of buyers experiencing three completely different pushes. Trying to use one demo for all three meant the pull never matched any particular buyer's push precisely enough.

The solution was three demos, aligned to three buying timelines:
- **Demo #1 (Learning):** for buyers in the passive looking phase — speak to the push, surface the question, don't oversell
- **Demo #2 (Possibilities):** for buyers in active looking — show the pull, concrete outcomes, options
- **Demo #3 (Trade-offs):** for buyers in deciding — help them make the specific trade-offs that move them to commitment

Result: **3x lead conversion, half the time to close, 5x revenue growth**.

The same product. The same features. Different framing of push and pull at each phase. That's the lever.

## The asymmetry of push and pull

One last thing worth noting: push and pull don't have to arrive at the same moment.

Bobby's timeline from Session 3 isn't linear. Someone can have a push event — the funeral, the kids leaving home, a bad night's sleep — and then sit in "passive looking" for months. The pull (a specific credible vision of progress) might come much later, triggered by something as simple as driving past the right building.

And the pandemic example is the reverse: someone in active looking, close to deciding, gets pushed *back* to passive looking by an external event. The push didn't go away. The pull didn't go away. But another struggling moment took priority.

This is why Bobby says:

> *"Nine times out of ten we lose to another struggling moment — not to a competitor."*

You're not mainly competing with other products. You're competing for attention against every other thing in the person's life that is pushing and pulling them somewhere else. The question is whether your push/pull pairing is strong enough and timely enough to win that competition.

## What this looks like for Memrise

Both forces deserve scrutiny.

**The push side:**
- What situation brings someone to Memrise? A trip coming up. A new job. An embarrassing moment in a foreign country. A relationship. A deadline.
- Is that push situation-specific or vague? ("I've always wanted to learn French" is not a push. "My partner's family speaks only Italian and we're visiting in October" is a push.)
- Is it strong enough to sustain? Or does it dissolve after the first week when daily life reasserts itself?

Bobby addressed this directly in the Memrise session when Sonia raised "time and motivation" as barriers:

> *"Sometimes we actually have to make the struggle bigger — help them realise that if you don't learn this, then you have a bigger choice to make."*

If users are churning because they "ran out of motivation," the question to investigate isn't "how do we make the product more motivating?" It's: *was the push ever strong enough?* And if not, how do you help users feel the real weight of their situation before you try to sell them a vision of progress?

**The pull side:**
- What does the early product experience promise? Can a new user see, concretely, what their language looks like in 30 days?
- Is the pull specific to their push — or is it generic? ("Learn a language!" is a pull for nobody in particular.)
- Does the first session deliver an early sign of progress that reinforces the pull they came in with?

Bobby's language from the six-phase model: System 5 (first use, ongoing use) is where the early signs of progress have to show up. The pull they felt when signing up has to be validated quickly. If it isn't, they have their answer — and it's the wrong answer.

## Three things to hold onto

1. **Push creates the question. Pull creates the answer.** Push makes the brain available. Pull fills the space with a vision of progress. You need both, at the right intensity, at the right moment.

2. **Context and outcome, not pain and gain.** Push is always situational — framed as "when I am." Pull is always an outcome in the customer's life — framed as "so I can." These are not product statements. They are life statements.

3. **You're competing with other struggling moments, not other products.** Nine times out of ten, churn isn't defection to a competitor. It's another thing in life claiming priority. The strength and specificity of your push/pull pairing determines whether you win that competition.
