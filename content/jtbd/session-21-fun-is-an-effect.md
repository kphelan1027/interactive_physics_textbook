# JTBD — Session 21: Fun Is an Effect, Not a Cause

> *"Fun is an effect. It's not a cause. And so the question is: what are the things we have to do to cause them to say fun?"*
> — Bobby Moesta, March 5 2026, Prototyping Workshop, Memrise

> *"Words are just packages of emotions and actions. And how do we actually figure out what are the actions and emotions that are compacted into the word that they say is fun?"*
> — Bobby Moesta, same session

---

## The simplest true statement

You cannot design "fun." You can only design the things that *cause* someone to feel fun. Until you know what those things are, the word is useless to you.

That is the whole session. But let's slow down — because the implications of this are wider than fun. They apply to any word your users give you that feels like an answer but is actually a starting point.

---

## Where this came from

This is an extension of the 20-session curriculum. It comes directly from the March 5 2026 prototyping workshop at Memrise — Bobby's third live session with the product team.

Sandra Zavala presented her team's prototyping work. They had interviewed users, identified "problem words," and were working through what variables to test. Sandra had arrived at a smart conclusion: "fun" wasn't a variable she could prototype against directly. It was more like a constraint — a quality that all the variables should produce.

Bobby stopped her there. Not to correct her, but to sharpen the frame:

> *"Fun is an effect. It's not a cause. And so the question is: what are the things we have to do to cause them to say fun — which is a bizarre thing to think about, but ultimately that's what we're trying to do as designers and builders: cause these effects like trust."*

Sandra was already close. Bobby pulled the idea one level further. Fun is not just a constraint to satisfy. It is an *effect* — something produced by a chain of causes you need to identify. Trust is the same. Confidence. Delight. Belonging. These are all downstream effects, not upstream inputs.

Design happens on the cause side. The effect is what you measure to know whether your design worked.

---

## The implication: you cannot prototype an effect

This is why the insight matters practically.

If your brief says "make the product more fun," you have no idea what to build. Fun is an output. You need to find the inputs — the specific choices of content type, interaction pattern, pacing, progress signal, framing, feedback — that combine to produce the feeling the user calls fun.

Sandra's five variables for the Memrise job were exactly this kind of upstream work:

- **Content type:** native speaker videos vs. app-created content
- **Capability framing:** session-by-session understanding vs. cumulative milestones ("after X activities, I can discuss this topic")
- **Progress signal:** progress through comprehension vs. progress through what you can now *do*
- **Content depth:** structured advanced path vs. AI-generated, user-driven content
- **Re-engagement:** passive availability vs. intrinsically relevant notifications

None of these are "fun." All of them, in combination, are *what causes* someone to call the product fun. Or to stop calling it fun.

Bobby praised the breakdown:

> *"I would praise the variable breakdown. You've basically got the right set of things to compare against. Now the goal is: let's measure them by having people compare different concepts in pairs — and listen to the language they give you."*

The paired comparison is the learning mechanism. But the learning you're after is not "did they prefer A or B." It's: *what language did they use to justify the preference?* That language reveals what is actually inside the word "fun" for them.

---

## "What kind of fun are we talking about?"

Bobby's challenge to the room was direct:

> *"The moment somebody says, 'Well, I just want it to be fun' — the next question is: what kind of fun are we talking about? Are we talking about Christmas morning fun? Are we talking about playing on the playground fun? Are we talking about reading a book fun?"*

These are not rhetorical alternatives. They map to fundamentally different design directions:

- **Christmas morning fun** = anticipation, surprise, reward. The product needs to create mystery and payoff. The next lesson feels like a gift.
- **Playground fun** = freedom, exploration, no consequences for failing. The product needs to remove stakes and let users wander.
- **Reading a book fun** = immersion, absorption, flow. The product needs to remove friction and create a state where time disappears.

Each of these points to a different set of design variables. Each would produce a different product. If you don't unpack the word, you have no idea which direction to go — and you will almost certainly design for the kind of fun *you* mean, not the kind the user means.

Bobby:

> *"It's dangerous for us to interpret what fun is. It's up to us to elicit the kind of fun they mean — what causes them to say it's fun."*

Interpret vs. elicit. This is a discipline, not just a technique. Interpretation is the assumption you carry in from outside. Elicitation is the information you extract from the person in front of you.

---

## The other half of the question

Bobby added a second dimension that is easy to miss:

> *"The other half of that question is: what happens that makes something that's fun not fun anymore? Because it's almost like a state — and we have to understand the causal mechanisms of it's fun in the beginning but it's not fun in the end."*

This is the transition point. And it is where most product teams fail — not at creating fun, but at *sustaining* it.

Think about any game or learning product you've used and abandoned. It was probably fun at the start. Something changed. The challenge curve flattened. The content ran out. The reward loop became predictable. The novelty evaporated. You stopped.

Bobby's question forces you to design for the transition: when does fun go away, and what do you insert at that point to make it fun again?

For Memrise specifically, Sandra's team had already flagged this in the re-engagement variable. The question of how to pull a user back once the intrinsic motivation wanes is exactly the question of: *what causes the fun to restart?* Not a notification. Not a streak. Whatever specific mechanism causes *this user's* specific kind of fun to re-activate.

## The ukulele story: fun is peace

Sandra had already done the work of eliciting. She shared what she found when she asked a Norwegian learner what else in his life felt fun:

> *"He gave me a story about playing a ukulele. He started over the pandemic. He found someone to build him a ukulele. And he told this maker, 'I don't care if it takes you 10 years to do it.' Why? Because he said: 'I'm like the dude in The Big Lebowski. Life is already too complicated. I just want to do things in my life that feel peaceful.'"*

This is what was inside the word "fun" for this user. Not excitement. Not achievement. Not social recognition. **Peace.** Simplicity. The absence of stress. Something he chose for himself, with no obligation to anyone.

That maps directly to the job Sandra's team had defined: *"help me improve myself through learning a language for fun and personal growth — feeling closer to a culture through a language, without adding stress or obligation to my day."*

The ukulele story confirmed the second half of that job statement. "Without adding stress or obligation" is not a qualifier. It is the load-bearing wall. For this user, anything that feels like homework, like performance, like external accountability — immediately stops being fun.

Bobby:

> *"We shouldn't interpret what fun is. We should elicit their definition of it, because it will lead us to where we really need to go."*

The ukulele story is a design brief. It says: the product should feel like something you chose, not something that chose you. The pacing should be unhurried. The progress signals should be gentle and cumulative. The re-engagement mechanism should feel like an invitation, not a reminder.

You could not have derived that from a feature list or an analytics dashboard. You could only find it by asking "what else do you do that feels fun?" and listening all the way to the end of the answer.

## The Duolingo insight: when the Job isn't the Job

This session produced one of the most important lines in all three Bobby Moesta sessions at Memrise — and it came almost as an aside.

Kasia Bania raised a concern about user "illogicality": a user who maintained a Duolingo streak religiously but reported they could not speak Italian.

Bobby did not see this as illogical. He saw it as a different Job:

> *"80% of the learners are just looking for a morning routine."*

He expanded:

> *"People may use language learning apps like Duolingo as a substitute for a morning routine. The app performs a different job than solely teaching language. Irrational behavior becomes rational when the full context is understood."*

And then Sandra and Kasia confirmed it from their own interviews: one user was using Duolingo before job interviews. Not to learn Italian. To have a ritual that signalled "I'm the kind of person who shows up." The streak was commitment, not fluency.

This is the deepest implication of the "words are packages" idea. It doesn't just apply to the word "fun." It applies to the word "learning." A user who says they are learning Italian may not be hiring a language-teaching product at all. They may be hiring a *routine-anchor*, a *daily proof of self*, a *signal of identity*. The product just happens to be shaped like a language app.

Kasia:

> *"The app fulfils a job in users' lives, such as providing a morning routine before job interviews for an unemployed developer — indicating that Duolingo might not be in the language learning business for all its users."*

This is exactly the Intercom lesson from Session 12. Intercom thought they were in the "customer communication software" business. They were actually running four separate businesses for four separate Jobs. Duolingo may think it is in the language-learning business. For a large portion of its users, it is in the morning-routine business, or the identity-signalling business, or the streak-completion business.

What is Memrise? The honest answer, based on interview data, might be more surprising than the roadmap assumes.

## Words as packages: a generalisation

Bobby's most precise formulation of the session:

> *"Words are just packages of emotions and actions. And how do we actually figure out what are the actions and emotions that are compacted into the word that they say is fun?"*

The word "fun" is the example. But the principle applies to any word users hand you that feels like an answer:

| What users say | What they might mean |
|---|---|
| "It's fun" | Peace / achievement / surprise / absorption / play |
| "I want to improve" | Get a job / reconnect with heritage / feel smart again / impress someone |
| "It's too complicated" | I'm afraid of failing / I can't see the end from here / I don't trust the product yet |
| "I want something practical" | I need it for a trip next month / I hate theory / I want to feel capable, not educated |
| "I want progress" | I want visible proof I am moving / I want to feel the gap closing / I want a story I can tell |

Every one of these is a package. Every one contains a specific emotional state, a specific context, and a specific set of actions that the user cannot yet do but wants to. The job of the JTBD interview is to unpack them.

Bobby in the interview context:

> *"I get them to where they have no more language and they just can't articulate more. And then I'll say, 'Well, if it was a movie, what kind of movie would it be? Is it a comedy or a drama?' And all of a sudden they have all new language to start talking to you. So it's this notion of introducing metaphors."*

This is prototyping inside an interview. When a user's language runs out, you don't accept the last word they gave you as the answer. You inject a new metaphor — a new set of options — that gives them fresh vocabulary to express what they're trying to say.

"What kind of fun?" is that metaphor. Christmas morning. Playground. Reading a book. You're not leading them to an answer. You're giving them a vocabulary upgrade so they can reach the answer themselves.

## Application to Memrise

The March 5 session was the Memrise product team doing live JTBD work. Sandra's five-variable prototype was the most advanced practical example in all three sessions. And it started from exactly this insight: the team had spent time in interviews, found "fun" as a recurring word, and — crucially — did not accept it as an answer.

They unpacked it. They found that for users in the "fun and personal growth" job, fun meant:
- Feeling connected to a culture
- Surprising yourself by recognising something
- Doing something that is purely yours — no obligation, no external accountability
- Peace, not achievement

That is a design brief. It is not a vibe — it is a set of constraints that rules out entire categories of features:

- **Rules out:** streak mechanics as the primary engagement driver (feels like obligation)
- **Rules out:** leaderboards and social comparison (feels like performance)
- **Rules out:** heavy grammar instruction (feels like school)
- **Rules in:** cultural immersion content (videos, stories, songs from the language's country)
- **Rules in:** gentle progress signals tied to what you can now *understand*, not what you can recite
- **Rules in:** content that feels self-directed — you explore, the app doesn't push

Every design decision becomes a question: does this cause the kind of fun this user means? Or does it accidentally import a different kind of fun — one that adds pressure, obligation, or the feeling of being tested?

## Three things to hold

1. **Fun is an effect, not a variable.** You cannot prototype "more fun." You prototype the causal chain — content type, progress framing, re-engagement mechanism — and measure whether the output is the effect you intended. This applies to any quality word users give you: trust, confidence, connection, simplicity.

2. **Words are packages.** When a user says "fun," "easy," "practical," "better," they have handed you a container. Your job is to open it. Bobby's technique: ask "what kind?" Give them metaphors. Let them reach for new language. Don't stop at the package — find what's inside.

3. **The Job might not be what it says on the tin.** The Duolingo user is not always learning Italian. They are sometimes managing a morning routine. The user who says they want to "speak the language" may actually want to feel like the kind of person who speaks the language. The product that actually serves their Job is not always the one that does what it says.

## Open Question

Bobby said something in this session that applies directly to any product team doing this work:

> *"I will warn you — I will break you. You will not be able to see the world the same way. You'll be able to break down every day into these things. Which, in some cases, might make it more complicated. But you have to realise there are times you have to shut it off."*

Here is the question:

> **What words do Memrise users give you most often — and what is actually inside them?**
>
> Not "fun." You've seen how that one opens. Pick another: "easier," "better," "more," "practical," "natural." Take one word that comes up repeatedly in your feedback or interviews.
>
> Now: what kind? What are three versions of "easier" that point in completely different design directions? What specific context produces the feeling a user calls "natural"? What does "more" mean — more content, more variety, more challenge, more reassurance, more proof of progress?
>
> Bobby's test: *Can you name the causal mechanism? Can you describe the design decision that would cause someone to use that word? And can you name the transition point — when that feeling goes away, and what you'd insert to bring it back?*
>
> If you can answer all three, you have a design variable. If you can't, you have more unpacking to do.
