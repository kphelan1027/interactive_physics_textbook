# JTBD — Session 10: The JTBD Interview — How to Uncover Struggling Moments

> *"The only way you really learn this is by going and doing interviews with people."*
> — Bobby Moesta (Memrise session, 2026)

> *"Consumers don't lie with malice. They omit things because they don't think they're relevant."*
> — Bobby Moesta

---

## The simplest true statement

The JTBD framework is a theory of demand. But a theory only becomes useful when it's grounded in reality — in the specific, particular, messy truth of why actual people bought an actual thing on an actual day. And that truth only surfaces through one mechanism: the interview.

Bobby's version of the interview is not market research. It's not a survey. It's not a focus group. It's closer to an investigation — specifically, a post-purchase investigation of the decision someone already made. You are not asking people what they *might* do. You are asking them what they *did do*, and why, and when, and what the world looked like just before they did it.

This session is about how to do that.

---

## Where we are

Sessions 1–9 built the conceptual toolkit. You understand the struggling moment as the seed of demand. You understand the four forces and how push, pull, anxiety, and habit interact to create or suppress a switch. You understand the six phases and the six systems. You understand non-consumption.

But all of that is a diagnostic framework. It tells you what categories to look for. The interview is how you fill those categories with real signal — the specific language, the specific context, the specific moment when this particular person's push became strong enough to act.

Bobby's claim: theory without interviews is a map without territory. You can have a perfect understanding of what struggling moments are, and still build the wrong product, because you filled in the details from your own imagination instead of your customer's memory.

---

## Why you interview people who bought — not people considering

This is the most important structural rule of the JTBD interview, and it's counterintuitive.

Most market research asks potential customers what they might want. You recruit people who are "in the market" for something. You ask them about their preferences. You test concepts with them. This feels sensible — you're targeting the exact people you want to acquire.

Bobby doesn't do this. And the reason is precise.

When you ask someone what they *might* do, they give you their stated preferences — their conscious, rational, self-presentational version of what a sensible person would want. They say they want granite countertops and hardwood floors. They say they'd pay more for better quality. They say they care about sustainability. These answers are not lies, exactly. But they're also not the truth that explains purchasing behaviour.

When you ask someone what they *did* do — when they already bought, already switched, already made the decision — something different happens. The decision is in the past. The emotional heat is gone. There's nothing to prove, no choice to justify in advance. You're not asking them to predict or decide. You're asking them to remember. And memory, when properly excavated, contains the real causal chain: the specific circumstances, the specific moment, the specific trigger.

Bobby's phrase: *"People who want to buy a house say they want hardwood floors and granite countertops. But when you look at what people actually buy, the trade-offs they make are entirely different."*

The person who bought a house knows what trade-offs they actually made. The person who wants to buy a house tells you their preferences.

Interview the buyer, not the prospect. The buyer has already resolved the ambiguity. The data is in the past, not the future.

## The timeline as structure

Bobby structures the interview around reconstructing a timeline. Not a product experience timeline — a *life* timeline. What was happening in the person's life when the thought first arose? What changed? What triggered the move from thinking about it to doing something about it?

The six phases give you the framework. The interview gives you the content.

**Phase 1: First thought** — *"When did you first start thinking about this?"*

This question sounds simple. It's actually disorienting for most respondents. They'll say "I've always wanted to do this" or "I've been thinking about it for years." That's not an answer — that's a non-answer that tells you they've been in passive mode for a long time without making progress.

The follow-up is everything: *"What was going on in your life when you first started thinking about it?"*

Now you're anchoring them to a specific life context. And the answer — a new job, a relationship change, a trip, a friend's comment, something they read — is the first data point about what kind of push is feeding this job.

**Phase 2: Passive looking** — *"What were you doing about it at that point?"*

Usually: nothing, or almost nothing. Googling occasionally. Mentioning it to a partner. Clicking on an ad without following through. Passive looking is the long phase where the push exists but hasn't crossed the threshold for action. Understanding what kept someone in passive looking — and for how long — tells you about the balance between push and the combination of anxiety and habit.

**Phase 3: Active looking** — *"What changed? When did you actually start doing something about it?"*

This is the critical transition. The move from passive to active is almost always caused by a specific event. Not a general trend — a specific event. Bobby calls this the "locksmith moment."

---

## The Locksmith Moment

Bobby's term for the specific event — the precise, datable, rememberable moment — when passive looking became active looking.

He named it after an experience he had. He was in a rental car, driving Dr. Deming to the airport, and suddenly panicked: he didn't know which side the filler cap was on. He'd been passively aware that this was sometimes a problem. But in that moment — with Dr. Deming in the car, a deadline, and an unfamiliar rental — it became an active problem he needed to solve immediately.

That's the locksmith moment. Something clicked open. The struggle moved from background to foreground.

For consumer purchases, the locksmith moment is often a very specific event: a conversation, an embarrassment, a moment of contrast ("I watched my colleague do something in five minutes that I struggle with for an hour"), an expiry, an arrival, a departure.

Bobby's instruction: when you find a respondent saying they've "always wanted to do this," that's the signal to go hunting for the locksmith moment. Ask: *"Why now, though? You've been thinking about this for years — what made this the time?"*

That question — *why now?* — is one of the most productive in the entire interview. It forces the respondent to identify the specific circumstance that made the general desire become a specific intention. That circumstance is almost always a struggling moment.

For language learners, the locksmith moment might be: planning a trip to Italy for the first time. A promotion that requires presenting to a French-speaking team. A new partner whose family doesn't speak English. "I've always wanted to learn French" describes years of passive looking. "My daughter's school in Paris starts in September" is the locksmith moment.

## Consumers lie — but not on purpose

Bobby's most important warning about interviewing: respondents are not deceptive. They are simply filtering their memories through relevance, and the filter is wrong.

When someone tells the story of a decision they made, they include the things that seem relevant to the decision — the price comparison, the feature evaluation, the recommendation from a friend. They leave out the things that seem incidental — the mood they were in, the conversation they overheard, the photograph they saw on Instagram three weeks before they started looking.

The problem: those "incidental" details are often the most causally important. The decision logic they present is often rationalisation after the fact. The real drivers were running in the background.

Bobby's example: in the condo case study, elderly residents kept saying they didn't need a dining room. That was their stated preference. But when researchers probed deeper — asking about memories, asking about specific objects, asking about the dining room table specifically — something different emerged. The dining room table was "the emotional bank account of their entire life." It wasn't a piece of furniture to be decluttered. It was the centre of everything important that had ever happened in their home.

Nobody volunteered that. Nobody said "I don't want to move because of my dining room table." It emerged through skilled excavation of what seemed, to the respondent, like irrelevant detail.

The interview technique that surfaces this material is not a questionnaire. It's something closer to what Bobby recommends from two books:

**Chris Voss — Never Split the Difference:** Voss was an FBI hostage negotiator. His book describes the techniques developed for extracting truth from people who have every reason to lie or omit. The core techniques — tactical empathy, mirroring, labelling, calibrated questions — are, Bobby argues, more useful for JTBD interviews than any market research methodology. Why? Because you're investigating the past. The past is a crime scene. You need to reconstruct what actually happened, not collect what someone wants you to think happened.

Bobby listens to it once a year.

**Richards Heuer — The Psychology of Intelligence Analysis:** Heuer was a CIA intelligence analyst who wrote the definitive textbook on avoiding cognitive bias in analysis. His core principle: gather all available information *before* forming hypotheses. The instant you have a hypothesis, your brain starts filtering incoming information through it — keeping what confirms it, discarding what doesn't. The solution is to maintain a state of deliberate non-hypothesis for as long as possible during information gathering.

Bobby's application: go into an interview without a hypothesis about what the struggling moment is. Don't try to confirm your product idea. Don't try to validate a feature. Ask open questions, listen to the answer, and let the picture assemble itself. The moment you decide you know what's happening, you've stopped learning.

Both of these sit underneath Bobby's maxim: *"I was never smart enough to form a hypothesis. All I know is I could change things and see what happened."*

## The language problem: pabum words

When users describe their experience — of your product, of the struggle, of the outcome they want — they reach for the words that seem most socially acceptable and most obviously true. These words are almost useless for building products.

Bobby calls them **pabum words**: *"The easy, fast, simpler — all the things we can all agree are a good idea but have no idea what they mean."*

"Easy." "Fast." "Intuitive." "Simple." "Better." Every user wants these things. Every product team wants to build them. But they are packages — compressed, ambiguous containers of meaning that haven't been opened. When someone says they want the product to be "easier," they mean something specific. But that specific meaning is invisible inside the word "easy."

Bobby: *"Words are packages of emotions and actions that need unpacking."*

The interview technique: when a pabum word appears, don't accept it. Probe it.

*"What would easier look like, specifically? Walk me through what's hard right now."*

*"When you say fast — what are you doing right now that takes too long? Show me what the process is."*

*"Intuitive — what does that mean to you? What would have to be true for you to call it intuitive?"*

The "fun" example from the prototyping workshop is the definitive case study. A user told the team she learned for "fun." The team nodded and moved on. Bobby kept going. What else do you do for fun? Plays ukulele. What does fun feel like? "Peace and simplicity." She quoted The Big Lebowski: "Life is already too complicated."

This user's "fun" meant the *absence of stress* — not the presence of excitement, not gamification, not reward loops, not achievement badges. An entirely different product direction was implied. And it was entirely invisible inside the word "fun."

*"The product must not interpret 'fun.' It must elicit the user's specific definition."*

This applies to every pabum word. When you hear them, slow down. That's where the real signal is buried.

## "Why" — the cleansing question

Bobby's specific caution about the word "why."

*"Why is a cleansing question. A sorbet for the mind."*

When you ask "why did you do that?", you almost always get a rationalised answer — the conscious, defensible logic the person has assembled post-hoc. They tell you a story that makes them sound like a rational agent making sensible decisions based on clearly understood values.

That story is not useless. But it's not the full picture. "Why" questions trigger explanation mode, which is different from memory mode.

The alternative: *"Tell me more about that."* *"Walk me through what happened."* *"What were you thinking at that moment?"* *"What did that feel like?"*

These questions don't ask for explanation. They ask for recollection. And recollection, when it's working properly, surfaces detail that explanation prunes away.

---

## "No" is more valuable than "yes"

*"No is 10 times more valuable than yes."*

Bobby's framing: when someone says yes to a concept or prototype, they're being polite. They're giving you what they think you want. Yes is the path of least resistance.

No — especially an unprompted, spontaneous no — tells you something has actually been violated. An expectation has been broken. A real preference has been tripped. No is signal. Yes is noise until proven otherwise.

The implication for interviews: you are specifically looking for the moments when something doesn't land, when the concept generates hesitation, when the prototype causes a face to change. Those moments are worth more than all the agreement you collect.

In practice: probe the no. *"You hesitated there — what happened?"* *"You don't look convinced — what's the gap?"* *"If you said no to this, what would it have to be to become a yes?"*

---

## Context creates value. Contrast creates meaning.

Bobby's synthesis of what a good interview actually produces.

*"Context creates value."* The same product, the same feature, the same design decision has entirely different value depending on the context the user is in. A 30-second lesson is invaluable to someone studying on a commute. It's useless to someone sitting down for a serious practice session. You cannot understand what your product is worth without understanding the context it's being hired into.

*"Contrast creates meaning."* Users don't know what they want in the abstract. They discover what they want by comparison — explicitly or implicitly. What was the old solution? Why was it inadequate? What would have to be different? The meaning of "better" only surfaces when there's a comparison anchor. A good interview always establishes that anchor.

Together: you are not just trying to understand the job. You are trying to understand the specific context the user is in when they want to make progress, and the contrast between their current situation and the outcome they're trying to reach. That gap is where your product lives.

## Practical structure

Bobby's guidance on interview mechanics:

- **Who to recruit:** People who recently made a switch or a purchase. Not people who "might" or "are thinking about." Recent buyers, recent switchers, recent deciders. Recent matters because the memory is cleaner.
- **Additional filter:** Recruit people in the specific job being tested. Have them tell their story before testing anything — confirm they're actually in that job, not an adjacent one.
- **Length:** 30–45 minutes once settled. Early interviews may run longer while you're learning to excavate.
- **Sample size:** 10 interviews is sufficient to find patterns. Bobby's Intercom engagement started with only 10 people — *"not the best users, but a diverse set of recent buyers."*
- **What you're building:** A reconstruction of the timeline from first thought to decision. The exact life context at each phase. The specific event that moved them from passive to active (the locksmith moment). The language they use — including the pabum words, which need unpacking.
- **Bobby's technique:** He introduces language (often metaphors) when users run out of words. If someone can't quite describe something, he offers a frame: *"Would you say it was more like X or more like Y?"* This gives them a comparison anchor, not an answer.
- **For small struggles:** Unlike big struggles (buying a house, returning to school — emotionally vivid, embedded in memory), small struggles (buying gum, picking an app) have weak memory traces. For these, get closer to the moment in time. Consider a diary study: have users record their experience for a week before the interview.

## Three things to hold onto

1. **Interview people who bought, not people who are considering.** The buyer's decision is in the past — the emotional heat is gone, there's nothing to justify. What you get is reconstruction of a real decision. The prospect gives you stated preferences. The buyer gives you causal truth.

2. **Hunt for the locksmith moment with "why now?"** When someone says they've always wanted to do something, ask what changed. The specific event that converted passive interest into active intent is one of the most valuable data points in the entire interview. That event IS the struggling moment in its most concrete form.

3. **Slow down when you hear pabum words.** Easy, fast, fun, simple, intuitive — these are packages. The signal is inside. Ask what easy would look like, specifically. Ask what fun actually feels like. The answer changes what you build.
