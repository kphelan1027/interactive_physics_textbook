# JTBD — Session 14: Case Study: AutoBooks — JTBD Is a Sales Framework

> *"When you understand the job-to-be-done, you're not just selling a product — you're solving a problem. And that's how we win."*
> — Bobby Moesta, Growth Summit 2025

---

## The simplest true statement

Every sales conversation fails for the same reason every product demo fails: the seller is on the supply side, and the prospect is on the demand side. The seller is showing features; the prospect is trying to figure out if this is the right moment to change. AutoBooks figured out how to fix this — not by improving the demo, but by building *three* different demos, each one designed for a different stage in the buyer's timeline.

The result: 5x revenue in 12 months.

---

## Where we are

We've now seen JTBD applied to product design (Intercom), institutional transformation (SNHU), and market creation via non-consumption. In every case the pattern was the same: understand the struggling moment first, let everything else follow.

AutoBooks introduces a new territory: **sales**.

The question Bobby is answering here isn't "what should we build?" It's "why does our demo keep failing — and what does JTBD tell us about how to fix it?"

The answer changes how you think about every conversation.

---

## What AutoBooks does

AutoBooks is a fintech company based in Detroit. It builds payments and invoicing software for small businesses — but it doesn't sell directly to small businesses. It sells to **banks**, who then offer AutoBooks as a value-added service to their small business customers.

So the sales team's job is B2B: convince a bank that AutoBooks is worth adopting. Get the bank to say yes. Then the bank handles distribution.

Chris Spiek — who co-founded The Re-Wired Group with Bobby and serves as AutoBooks' CPO — was the person who applied JTBD to the sales process here. This is Bobby's framework deployed inside his own firm's client work.

---

## The problem: a demo that kept breaking

AutoBooks had a standard six-step sales process:

1. Advertising
2. Landing Page
3. Email Campaign
4. Booking Reminders
5. **Sales Demo Call**
6. Customer Success

Step five — the demo — wasn't working. And the team knew it, because **every 90 days they would rebuild the demo from scratch**. New script, new slides, new framing. Still not converting.

When Bobby looked at what was happening, he identified something that should feel familiar by now.

The struggling moment wasn't "the demo is bad." The struggling moment was: **the demo is treating every prospect the same.**

---

## The supply-side view of a sales funnel

From the supply side, a standard sales funnel looks like this:

**Awareness → Interest → Consideration → Intent → Evaluation → Purchase**

This is a progression that describes the vendor's pipeline. A lead enters at the top, flows through stages, and exits as a customer. The demo sits somewhere in the middle — "evaluation" phase, typically — and its job is to show the product compellingly enough that the prospect tips into purchase intent.

This is a sensible model if you believe every prospect who reaches the demo is at the same point. They've heard of you, they're interested, they want to see it — now show them what it does.

But that's not what was happening.

---

## The demand-side reality

Bobby's frame: every prospect, regardless of where they appear in your funnel, is somewhere on their *own* buying timeline. And that timeline looks like this:

**First Thought → Passive Looking → Active Looking → Deciding → First Use → Ongoing Use**

These aren't just labels — they're six distinct mental states. A person in *passive looking* is in a completely different headspace from someone in *deciding*. They're asking different questions. They have different anxieties. They need different information.

The AutoBooks demo was trying to serve all of them simultaneously. For someone in passive looking, the demo was moving too fast — they were still trying to figure out if they had the problem at all. For someone already in deciding, the demo was too slow — they already knew they wanted something like this, they just needed to know which trade-offs they were making.

One demo. Three different buyers. None of them getting what they needed.

Bobby's diagnosis: **the struggling moment was the demo itself**.

---

## The fix: three demos, matched to the timeline

The team built three demos, each designed for a specific stage in the buying timeline.

### Demo #1 — Passive Looking: Learning

The prospect at this stage has had a *first thought* that maybe something needs to change. They're here, but they haven't committed to the idea that they have a real problem. They're in learning mode.

What they need: a conversation that helps them recognise the problem as real. Not features. Not pricing. Not how it works. Just: here's the thing you're struggling with, and here's what it could look like if you solved it.

The goal of Demo #1 is not to close. **It's to make them want Demo #2.**

### Demo #2 — Active Looking: Possibilities

Now they know they have the problem. They're actively looking at options — probably comparing several solutions, starting to understand the landscape. They want to understand what's possible.

What they need: a more complete picture of the solution space. How does this fit with what they're already doing? What can it actually do? Where does it sit relative to other options?

The goal of Demo #2 is not to close. **It's to make them want Demo #3.**

### Demo #3 — Deciding: Trade-offs

Now they've narrowed it down. They're in the deciding phase — which means they're making trade-offs. What do they get for the price? What do they have to give up? What's the risk if it doesn't work?

This is when you talk about specifics. Terms. Implementation. What happens next.

The goal of Demo #3 is to close — or to surface the exact anxiety that's blocking them so you can address it.

---

## The three JTBD statements

Before any demo began, the sales team would present the prospect with three statements and ask: **"Which of these applies most to you right now?"**

**Statement 1:**
> *"Our executive team has placed an emphasis on growing deposits and/or increasing non-interest fee income."*

**Statement 2:**
> *"Our latest digital banking upgrade is behind us. We are now looking for our next digital banking project that will help us to continue to differentiate."*

**Statement 3:**
> *"When our customers ask us how they can get paid online, we don't have a good answer. We have a good solution for card present transactions, but when it comes to competing against non-bank providers like PayPal, Square, QuickBooks and others, we're at a disadvantage."*

Notice what these statements are *not*. They don't describe AutoBooks. They don't describe features. They don't describe price or positioning or market share.

They describe the **struggling moment of the buyer** — the context they're in, the problem they're already feeling, the outcome they're trying to achieve. The bank exec reading these statements doesn't hear "sales pitch." They hear: *"this person knows what I'm dealing with."*

This is exactly what Bobby means by being problem-aware before you're solution-aware. The prospect isn't being asked to evaluate a product — they're being asked to identify *their own job*.

Once they do, the sales team knows which demo to run.

## The results

Twelve months after implementing the JTBD-based sales approach:

- **3× lead conversion**
- **½ the time to close** (from lead to customer, with *more* interaction along the way — not less)
- **6× bank partners**
- **9× small business users**
- **5× revenue growth**
- Bonus: programme launches became faster and required less back-end work

The ½-time-to-close is worth pausing on. The conventional assumption is that longer, more thorough sales cycles produce better conversion. AutoBooks halved the time *while increasing the number of touchpoints*. They had more conversations with each prospect — but each conversation was the *right* conversation for where that prospect actually was.

This is a demand-side insight wearing a sales hat. Less of the wrong conversation is faster than more of the right one.

---

## What SNHU and AutoBooks have in common

SNHU's insight was: 80% of our students are in one job, and we were designing our entire institution for a different one. AutoBooks' insight was: every prospect is at a different point in the buying timeline, and we were running one demo for all of them.

Both are the same mistake.

You have a supply-side model of who your customer is and what they need — and you've built your process around that model. The model is wrong. Not because you're careless, but because you built it from the inside out.

The admissions team at SNHU believed students were "executing a plan" because that's what students say when they fill out an application form. The AutoBooks sales team believed all prospects needed the same demo because that's what a sales funnel looks like from the inside.

Bobby's correction in both cases: **go to the demand side. Find out where they actually are. Build for that.**

---

## The meta-lesson: JTBD is a conversation framework

Every session so far has treated JTBD as a tool for product design. Find the struggling moment. Build for it. Price for it. Market for it. 

AutoBooks extends this: **JTBD is the right framework for any conversation where one party is trying to help another party make progress.**

That includes sales. It includes customer success. It includes investor pitches, hiring conversations, and performance reviews. It includes the onboarding email you send on day one, the push notification you fire on day seven, the re-engagement campaign you run on day thirty.

In every one of those moments, you're either meeting the person where they are on *their* timeline — or you're running your internal process on them and wondering why it doesn't convert.

The sales demo is just the most visible version of this failure, because it has a number attached: conversion rate.

Bobby's observation is sharper than it looks. If your demo keeps breaking every 90 days, the problem isn't the demo. The problem is that you're designing the demo from the wrong side of the wall.

## The Memrise resonance

Think about the onboarding flow. From the supply side, onboarding is a sequence: first session, first streak, first completed lesson, first paid conversion. You optimize each step to move users to the next one.

From the demand side: every user who opens Memrise for the first time is somewhere on their own personal buying timeline. Some are in passive looking — they downloaded it after seeing an ad, but they're not sure they have a real problem yet. Some are in deciding — they've already tried Duolingo, they know they want something different, they're making trade-offs right now. Some are in first use — they're paying attention to early signals of progress, looking for confirmation that this was worth it.

The onboarding flow treats them all the same.

What would three different onboarding experiences look like? One for the person in passive looking — who needs to have their problem made real before you show them anything. One for the person in active looking — who already knows they want to learn Spanish and just wants to understand what Memrise can do that Duolingo can't. One for the person in deciding — who needs to know exactly what they're committing to, how long sessions are, and what "success" looks like in a week.

The AutoBooks insight is directly translatable: **match the conversation to where the person actually is.**

## Three things to hold onto

1. **The struggling moment can be inside your own process.** AutoBooks wasn't failing because of a bad product. The product was fine. The demo — the *sales experience itself* — was the wall customers were hitting. Your struggling moment can be in your pricing page, your onboarding email, your support ticket flow. The framework applies everywhere, not just in product design.

2. **Meeting people where they are in the timeline is more efficient than trying to move them through yours.** AutoBooks halved their sales cycle by running the right conversation at the right moment. Not by doing more — by doing less of the wrong thing. Three targeted demos replaced one bloated one. Less friction, faster close.

3. **"Which of these applies to you?" is the most underrated question in sales.** Before you show a single feature, ask the customer to identify their own struggling moment. This does three things simultaneously: it confirms they have a real job, it tells you which job they're in, and it signals to them that you understand their world. That signal is worth more than any feature walkthrough.
