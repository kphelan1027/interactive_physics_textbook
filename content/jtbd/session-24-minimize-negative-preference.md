# JTBD — Session 24: Minimize Negative Preference — The Cinnamon Gum Principle

> *"The more I actually make people like it, the more I would actually make people hate it."*
> — Bobby Moesta, March 5 2026, Prototyping Workshop, Memrise

> *"Cinnamon gum is the most popular of all gums because it's actually set at a point that nobody really loves it but nobody really hates it."*
> — Bobby Moesta, March 5 2026

---

## The simplest true statement

Maximising preference does not maximise your market. Sometimes the fastest way to grow is to stop trying to be anyone's favourite.

That is what this session is about. It is counterintuitive, but Bobby has the data to prove it — and once you see it, you cannot unsee it in every product decision you make.

---

## Where this came from

Bobby spent time at Wrigley — the chewing gum company. He was testing the strength of cinnamon flavour. The natural instinct was: find the level that the most people love, then ship that.

So they tested. As they increased the cinnamon intensity, a group of enthusiasts emerged — people who loved the intensity, who sought it out, who became vocal advocates. The love-it score went up. The product team was excited.

But Bobby noticed something else in the data.

> *"Some people loved really really hot cinnamon and some people wanted so the hotter I made it the more people loved it but there was also more people who hated it."*

It was not a simple preference scale where more cinnamon meant more preference. It was a distribution — and as the peak of the love-it group rose, so did the peak of the hate-it group. The more extreme the product, the more divided the reaction.

He then asked the question most product teams never ask: *what if we stop optimising for maximum love, and start optimising for minimum hate?*

> *"What I actually ended up doing is realising like how do I just minimise the people who hate it? And so cinnamon gum is the most popular of all gums because it's actually set at a point that nobody really loves it but nobody really hates it. Does that make sense?"*

Maximum love produces a niche. Minimum hate produces a mass market.

---

## Why this is counterintuitive

We are trained — by school, by A/B testing, by NPS — to optimise for the positive signal. Find the thing people love most. Go harder on it.

Bobby identifies this as a false model:

> *"The false notion is the more they like it, the more they'll use it. It's just not true."*

What is actually happening with extreme preference is segmentation. The more intensely someone loves a product, the more it has been tuned to their specific profile — which by definition means it has been detuned for everyone else. Push it far enough and you have a passionate core with an equally passionate rejection crowd. That is a difficult market to grow from.

The alternative is not mediocrity. It is strategic positioning: find the feature level that removes the most friction for the largest number of people. There is a specific point on the preference curve — not the maximum — where the area under the "willing to use it" side is largest.

---

## The Harry Snow moment: sensory segments

Bobby raised this principle directly during Harry Snow's prototyping review in the March 5 session. Harry's team had tested four prototypes with two users and found that one preferred Memrise-generated vocabulary, the other preferred AI-prompted vocabulary. Harry was asking: which one is better?

Bobby's first response was that two users is not enough data. But his second response was more interesting:

> *"Sometimes there's two answers right and so we have to realise that we want to look at the data and kind of say well like if I look at the averages like here's the things they have in common but like as you do more of them and you do the pairing that that I just showed Sandra the fact is is you'll actually start to learn about not only what makes people like it more, but what actually makes them hate it more."*

He called the sub-groups "sensory segments" — people who have genuinely different sensory responses to the same stimulus. This is not a bug in the research; it is information. It tells you:

1. Where you have genuine optionality (you must offer both, because neither dominates)
2. Where you have a feature that is splitting the room (design it to minimise the split, or explicitly choose one segment)

The question shifts from "which version is better?" to "which version produces the smallest hate-it group?" Those are different questions with different answers.

---

## The mechanics: why preference is a two-tailed distribution

Bobby draws this out explicitly. Every dimension you can vary in a product has two failure modes:

- **Too little:** the feature is absent, too weak, too easy, too forgettable. Not enough of the thing.
- **Too much:** the feature is overwhelming, too intense, too gamified, too demanding. Too much of the thing.

In the middle is an operating window where the feature is "good enough" — enough to not frustrate, not so much that it alienates. That window is not the maximum preference point. It is the *minimum hate* point.

Bobby draws this directly from the fun/progress discussion with Sandra Zavala:

> *"There's not enough fun and then there's too much fun. So instead of asking them outside the category, ask them inside the category of like tell me about something you did where you didn't feel you made enough progress and tell me about a place where it felt like it was just too hard to make progress."*

The same structure: two failure modes on either side of the operating window. The design work is not to find the maximum of a single axis. It is to find the window between the two failure modes.

---

## Three product situations where this changes the decision

**Situation 1: You are debating feature intensity.**

You have a streak mechanic. Your most engaged users love it. But a segment of users report that the streak creates anxiety and makes them feel like failures when they miss a day. The instinct is: keep the streak for the people who love it. 

The minimise-hate question: *what is the streak configuration that eliminates the "failure" feeling for the largest group, while retaining enough accountability signal for the users who need it?* That might be a streak that resets gently, or one that is optional, or one that shows your longest streak rather than your current streak. You are not removing the feature. You are re-calibrating it from the love-it maximum to the minimum-hate position.

**Situation 2: You are choosing between two versions.**

Your A/B test shows version A outperforms version B on average preference. But the variance is higher for version A — some users love it intensely, some strongly dislike it. Version B has lower average preference but tighter variance.

The minimise-hate analysis would examine the distributions, not just the means. If version A's higher average is driven by an extreme-preference sub-segment, you may actually be building for a niche while inadvertently pushing away a larger group. Version B's consistency might represent the larger addressable market.

**Situation 3: You are entering a competitive market.**

Your competitor is the intensely-loved option: the power users adore it, the streaks are obsessive, the brand has a cult following. You cannot out-love them for that segment.

But love and hate are correlated in their model. The users who left — or who never started — are not choosing your competitor. They are non-consumers who were pushed away by the hate side of the competitor's preference curve.

Your market is the minimum-hate market: everyone who wants to make progress but for whom the extreme product is too much. Bobby saw this at SNHU. He saw it at Intercom. Non-consumption is often the downstream effect of excessive feature intensity — products that are so optimised for their superfans that they have made themselves inaccessible to everyone else.

---

## What this means for Memrise

The Duolingo conversation in the March 5 session is directly relevant here. Kasia Bania described a user with 500 days of Duolingo streak who could not say a sentence in Italian. Bobby's comment:

> *"It might be that they say they're trying to learn a language, but they're just trying to basically be engaged in having a commitment to a routine of some sort. So it has nothing to do with learning language and more about I have a daily routine."*

Then:

> *"When you really want to learn language, come to Memrise. If you just want to do a morning routine, Duolingo works great for you. Like, call it out."*

This is the minimise-hate principle applied at the strategic level. Duolingo has maximised love for the routine-and-streak segment. The cost is that they produce exactly the kind of user who cannot speak the language — and who, when they finally want to speak the language, becomes a non-consumer of Duolingo and an opportunity for Memrise.

The minimise-hate insight for Memrise: do not try to out-streak Duolingo. That is the maximum-love game for a segment that Duolingo has already won. Instead: find the level of commitment mechanics that removes the anxiety for the user who left Duolingo in frustration, while still providing enough structure for the user who needs accountability. That is the cinnamon gum position in this market.

---

## The operational question: how do you find the minimum-hate position?

Bobby's method, from the workshop:

1. **Ask what is not the thing, not what the thing is.** "Tell me about a time when it was not fun" is more actionable than "tell me about a time when it was fun." The floor of the operating window is easier to locate than the optimum.

2. **Run paired comparisons, not ranking.** When users compare two prototypes, give a point to the preferred one and a negative point to the non-preferred one. Track who is accumulating negatives. The prototype with the fewest negatives — not the most positives — is your minimum-hate candidate.

3. **Look for high variance as a warning signal.** If some users love a prototype and others strongly dislike it, you are near an extreme. That is not necessarily wrong, but it tells you that you are serving a specific segment rather than a broad market.

4. **Actively look for sensory segments.** If two users consistently prefer opposite things, you are not looking at noise. You are looking at a genuine segmentation. That is either a product decision (you need optionality) or a targeting decision (you choose one segment deliberately).

---

## Why this is not the same as playing it safe

There is a misreading of this principle where "minimise hate" becomes "make it bland, make it generic, make it not-offensive." That is not what Bobby is describing.

The cinnamon gum example is about intensity, not ambition. Wrigley's cinnamon gum is not bland. It is distinctive. It tastes like cinnamon. The point is that the intensity was calibrated to remove the hate-it group without eliminating the thing that makes it cinnamon.

Session 19 (trade-offs) already covered the related principle: choose what to suck at deliberately. RyanAir does not try to minimise hate on snacks because they have made a deliberate choice: they suck at snacks to be excellent at price and punctuality. That is a valid strategy — but it is a segmentation play, and it only works if the customer understands the trade-off.

Minimising negative preference is different. It says: within the segment you are serving, at the feature level, find the configuration that the most people in that segment can live with — not the configuration that the superfans inside that segment will worship.

Maximum love → serves superfans → smaller market.
Minimum hate → serves the mass of the segment → larger market.

Both are legitimate strategies. But most product teams default to maximum love without ever examining the alternative.

## Three things to hold

1. **Maximising love and maximising market size are not the same thing.** As you push a feature toward the intensity that superfans adore, you push an equal number of users toward the hate-it threshold. The cinnamon gum principle: the best-selling gum is not the most-loved gum. It is the gum with the fewest people who actively hate it.

2. **Every product feature has two failure modes.** Too little: not enough of the thing (not stimulating, not structured, not rewarding). Too much: too much of the thing (too intense, too demanding, too gamified). The operating window sits between them. Your design work is to find the window, not to maximise the intensity.

3. **Non-consumption is often the hate-it market.** The people who are not using your product — or not using Memrise — are not absent because nothing appealed to them. Many are absent because a competitor's product pushed past the hate-it threshold for them. That is your market. Not the superfans who love the competitor. The people who left.

## Open Question

Bobby gave the Wrigley cinnamon gum example quickly, almost as an aside, during the Harry Snow review. But it contains a research methodology embedded inside it.

He did not just decide to set the cinnamon intensity in the middle. He had data: he knew where the hate-it threshold was, which meant he had tested prototypes that triggered hate. He had found the floor.

> **Here is the question for you, Kevin: think about one feature in Memrise's current product — streaks, difficulty level, lesson length, notification cadence — and identify its two failure modes.**
>
> *Too little:* what does a user who disengages because this feature is absent or too weak actually look like? What do they say in interviews? What is their behaviour pattern?
>
> *Too much:* what does a user who disengages because this feature is overwhelming or oppressive actually look like? The Duolingo exile with the 500-day streak who cannot speak Italian is a real person. What caused the hate side for them?
>
> Now: where is Memrise currently positioned on that feature — closer to the "too little" failure mode, or the "too much" failure mode? And is that a deliberate strategic choice, or has it just accumulated?
>
> *Bobby's answer at Wrigley was not "aim for the middle." His answer was: find where the hate-it threshold actually is, then stay just inside it. That requires knowing where the threshold is — which means you have to have seen people cross it. Have you seen them cross it at Memrise?*
