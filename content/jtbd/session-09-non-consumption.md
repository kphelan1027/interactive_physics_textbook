# JTBD — Session 9: Non-Consumption — The Real Source of Growth

> *"Clay Christensen would talk about it as the real source of growth. And non-consumption is when somebody wants to make progress but can't because of access, knowledge, money or inconvenience."*
> — Bobby Moesta (Jan 27, 2026, Memrise session)

---

## The simplest true statement

Most companies compete for the same customers — the people already in the market, already using something, already switching between alternatives. That market is real, but it's also crowded, expensive, and zero-sum.

Non-consumption is the other market. It's the people who **want** to make progress but can't — not because they chose a competitor, but because something blocked them from getting started at all. Access. Knowledge. Money. Inconvenience.

Bobby's core claim: **that market is almost always bigger than the competitive market, and almost no one is looking at it.**

---

## Where we are

Sessions 1–8 built the demand-side toolkit: the struggling moment, the four forces, the six phases, the six systems, and the supply-vs-demand lens. All of that describes how existing consumers move through a change. Non-consumption is the question underneath all of that: *who is not in that process at all, even though they should be?*

This is where Clay Christensen's concept of disruptive innovation connects most directly to JTBD. Bobby co-architected the framework with Christensen, and Christensen's most enduring insight was that disruptive products don't win by beating incumbents at their own game. They win by serving people the incumbents never saw as customers in the first place.

Bobby's lens on why: those people have a struggling moment. They have a desired outcome. They have the four forces pressing on them. But they can't get from here to there because of one specific blocker: access, knowledge, money, or inconvenience. Remove the blocker, and you tap a market that your competitors aren't even aware exists.

---

## The definition, precisely

Bobby defines non-consumption in one sentence:

*"Non-consumption is when somebody wants to make progress but can't because of access, knowledge, money or inconvenience."*

Four blockers. Any one of them is enough to stop a person from making progress, even when the push is real and the pull is visible.

- **Access:** The product or service physically isn't available to them. Geography, distribution, institutional gatekeeping.
- **Knowledge:** They don't know how to evaluate, choose, or use it. The skill ceiling is too high, the terminology too opaque, the process too unfamiliar.
- **Money:** The cost is prohibitive — not just in price, but in the entire economic equation: time, risk, upfront commitment.
- **Inconvenience:** The friction of the process itself makes the cost too high. Too many steps, too much hassle, too much time away from everything else.

The critical word is *can't*. Not "don't want to." Not "chose something else." Can't. These are not people who evaluated your product and walked away. These are people who never made it to evaluation because something stopped them before they got there.

Bobby's framing: *"If you can actually start to think about people who want to — who are struggling and want to do something but they can't — so they just give up on it, you can start to tap into a market that didn't need to be as good as Harvard. It actually just had to be better than nothing."*

That last phrase is the key to understanding the whole concept. **Non-consumers aren't comparing your product to the competition. They're comparing it to nothing.** That changes everything about what you need to build.

## The SNHU story: from 2,500 students to 250,000

This is Bobby's most detailed non-consumption case study — and it happened in education, one of the most resistant industries to disruption.

**The setup.** In 2010, Bobby was teaching at Harvard with Clay Christensen when Paul LeBlanc approached him after class. LeBlanc had just taken over as president of Southern New Hampshire University: a 2,500-student school 90 minutes north of Boston. LeBlanc had noticed something odd: 500 students were enrolled in online courses but almost never showed up physically. They'd watch lectures on video, they'd come in only for tests. They didn't want to be on campus at all.

The supply-side interpretation: these students were disengaged. The demand-side question: what is the struggling moment that caused them to be here, and what does progress actually mean to them?

**The jobs.** Bobby ran interviews. He found five distinct jobs, but one dominated everything:

1. **Help me escape** — switching careers, leaving a job that wasn't working
2. **Help me step it up** — a new sense of responsibility (a child, a parent to care for, a relationship) that made doing better suddenly urgent
3. **Help me extend myself** — learning for love of learning, no specific outcome
4. **Help me execute my plan** — already knew where they were going, needed credentials
5. **Help me do what's expected** — external pressure, family or social obligation

*"Almost 80% of them were in 'help me step it up.'"*

And here's what made that job so powerful: *"They could see beyond school. They could see basically where they wanted to go."* The struggling moment had an outcome attached to it — a salary job instead of an hourly job, a promotion, security for a child. That made the line from here to there much easier to navigate than, say, someone who wanted to extend themselves but had no end point in mind.

Bobby's observation on the admissions team's misread: they thought 80% of applicants were in "help me execute my plan." Why? Because that's the language people use when they fill out an application. But when you actually talk to them about their circumstances — the new baby, the divorce, the layoff — the real job surfaces.

**The test.** In October 2011, SNHU ran an emotional ad based on the "step it up" job. Bobby described it in the session: a single parent, studying at night and during lunch breaks, working an hourly job, getting a call from their child. *"You really don't even see SNHU till the end."* The ad didn't mention features. It didn't mention flexibility or cost. It dramatised the struggling moment.

A school that normally received 1,000–1,200 applications a year received **10,000 applications in one month**.

**The process rebuild.** LeBlanc's first response was: if these people want to do this on their own time, what needs to change in the school to serve them?

*"How long does your application process take?"*

Four months. Because the admissions office was built for traditional residential students — it needed to evaluate housing capacity, review transcripts at human pace, schedule campus visits. None of that applied to these students.

LeBlanc said: *"I want to change the application process from four months to one day."* His admissions team said impossible. He built a small team in the corner. That team would talk to applicants Monday or Tuesday, locate transcripts, assess context and outcomes, and have a plan in place by Friday of the same week.

The application process went from four months to one day.

**The coaching model.** These students had lives. People got divorced. People got cancer. People lost parents. The traditional academic adviser — who told you what classes to take and pointed you toward the career centre — wasn't built for that reality.

LeBlanc made a mandate: *"We need to become their life coach. We need to know that they need a tutor before they even know they need a tutor."*

He replaced academic advisers with life coaches. He lost 90% of his existing academic advisers in the process. What happened to churn? *"He reduced churn by almost 90%."*

**The curriculum.** LeBlanc stopped letting professors design courses. He brought in instructional designers and adult learning experts to build curricula for people who were studying at midnight between shifts, not sitting in a lecture hall with nothing else going on.

**The result.** LeBlanc retired having taken SNHU from 2,500 students to **250,000 students** — the largest university in the world. Cost went down by 70%. He had to build a stadium for graduation because the only time most students ever came to campus was to walk across the stage.

*"He realized that how many people want to go back to school but can't. And you start to realize that number is massive."*

That massive number is non-consumption. Not people who chose community college over SNHU. Not people who chose Harvard over SNHU. People who had the push, had the pull, had the struggling moment — and were stopped by access (couldn't get to campus), knowledge (didn't know how to navigate an admissions process), money (couldn't afford it), or inconvenience (couldn't restructure their life for a four-month application and in-person classes).

One product, designed for the existing market, couldn't see any of them. SNHU simply removed the blockers.

## "Competing with nothing"

Bobby's phrase for the fundamental economics of non-consumption: *"You people will usually be willing to accept an inferior alternative in the beginning because they are competing with nothing and not doing or having anything."*

This inverts the normal product quality conversation. When you compete in the existing market, you need to be measurably better than alternatives on the dimensions customers care about. That's hard, expensive, and zero-sum — every point of improvement someone else can copy.

When you compete against non-consumption, you need to be better than nothing. That's a completely different bar. You don't need the best mattress. You need a mattress buying experience that doesn't require expertise the buyer doesn't have. You don't need the best university. You need one that can start an application on Monday and close it on Friday.

Bobby put it directly for Casper: *"We didn't build the best mattress. We didn't build the most affordable mattress. Because we addressed the critical struggling moments, we were able to help people make progress."*

And: *"I don't necessarily have to be the best. I have to be the best for these people in that moment of decision."*

Non-consumers are not demanding a superior product. They are demanding a product that clears the specific blocker standing between them and the progress they already want to make. If you can identify that blocker and remove it, you can enter a market competitors have declared saturated and find that the real market was vastly larger all along.

Casper: a mattress company that supply-side analysis said had no room to enter. What supply-side analysis missed: the market wasn't saturated with people who knew how to buy a mattress. It was saturated with products for people who already knew. The non-consumers — people who wanted a new mattress but were stopped by knowledge and inconvenience — weren't being served at all. Casper became a billion-dollar company in the gap.

## The transistor radio and rock and roll

Bobby's favourite long-arc non-consumption story is the transistor radio.

*"The transistor radio was invented in 1952. Radios were either in the home or in the car. There were many people who didn't have a car or didn't have enough money to buy a radio. This was a very cheap radio. And ultimately they could take it with them."*

Access and money were the blockers. The transistor radio was inferior — *"it sounded like crap"* — but it was better than nothing. And the people it reached weren't being counted in the market for radio. They were non-consumers.

What followed: radio stations went from two or three in a market to ten, twenty, thirty. Music became a cultural force. Boom boxes. Cassette tapes. Progressively better hardware. Spotify — which Bobby marks as just another iteration of the same underlying job: *"getting music where I want, when I want."*

*"Rock and roll was built on the backbone of this thing."*

This is the long-term arc of non-consumption done right. You don't just capture the non-consumers. You create a new market. You pull in suppliers, infrastructure, adjacent industries. The struggling moment that was being ignored at 2 or 3 radio stations per market contained the demand for 30. Nobody building those first 2 stations saw it.

## The iPhone and the camera market

Bobby uses the iPhone camera story as a precision demonstration of non-consumption in technology:

*"The struggling moment was: how many people want to take a picture but don't have a camera with them? And it turns out that a bad picture on my razor of my kids playing soccer was better than no picture at all."*

The camera manufacturers — Canon, Nikon, Hasselblad, Fuji — were competing in the existing market. They were building bigger lenses, faster sensors, higher megapixel counts. They were serving people who already knew how to buy cameras, already cared about image quality, already had the knowledge and money and access to participate.

The non-consumers: parents who wanted to capture a moment but weren't carrying a dedicated camera. People who wanted documentation but found the friction of a separate device too high. People who had no particular photography intent but found themselves in a moment worth capturing.

Apple didn't target those people with a better camera. Apple targeted the struggling moment — *"I want to take a picture right now"* — with something that happened to already be in their pocket. The camera was secondary. The job was primary.

What happened to the camera market in the next ten years: Canon, Nikon, Hasselblad, Fuji all lost approximately 90% of unit sales. The number of photographs taken increased by more than 10,000x.

Bobby's summary: *"They focused on building better cameras. Apple was about how do I make you a better photographer. The moment they did that, it changed the entire business."*

The non-consumption market for "a way to capture a moment right now" was vastly larger than the existing market for "a camera." It just needed someone to reframe the job.

## What this means for Memrise

Sonia raised this directly in the session — and Bobby confirmed it.

*"Time and motivation are something users struggle with. Are those elements of non-consumption?"*

Bobby: *"Yes."*

Time and motivation are inconvenience and knowledge blockers. The person who says "I want to learn a language but I never have time" is a non-consumer of sustained language learning — not because they don't want to make progress, but because the existing product form requires a level of consistent friction they can't sustain alongside the rest of their life.

Bobby's specific suggestion for Memrise: *"Sometimes we might actually have to make the struggle bigger."*

*"When they say I want to learn this for work, I want a promotion — what happens if you don't get the promotion? What are you going to do?"*

This is a precise non-consumption strategy. The non-consumer with time and motivation barriers often has a push that hasn't been made vivid enough. They feel a low-grade dissatisfaction but haven't reached the point where they've resolved: "today is the day." The job isn't to build a better product for people already committed. The job might be to help people who are stuck in passive looking — who have the push and pull but aren't moving — feel the weight of their own struggling moment enough to act.

Marcin added the implication immediately: *"That's got really interesting implications for how we ask the questions. How do we make the problem more intense for them — or to realize the consequence of not doing anything?"*

That is exactly the question. And the answer starts with knowing whether your non-consumer's blocker is access, knowledge, money, or inconvenience — because each one requires a different intervention. SNHU's non-consumer was blocked by access and inconvenience. The transistor radio non-consumer was blocked by money and access. Casper's was blocked by knowledge and inconvenience. Memrise's "time and motivation" non-consumer is likely blocked by inconvenience and, as Bobby pointed out, an insufficiently vivid push.

## Three things to hold onto

1. **Non-consumption is when someone wants to make progress but can't — because of access, knowledge, money, or inconvenience.** They're not choosing a competitor. They're not in the market at all. But they have the struggling moment. They have the desired outcome. The only thing missing is a product that removes their specific blocker.

2. **Non-consumers aren't comparing you to the competition. They're comparing you to nothing.** That's a completely different bar. SNHU didn't need to be Harvard. Casper didn't need to be the best mattress. The transistor radio sounded like crap. The iPhone camera was worse than a Canon. All of them were better than nothing — and nothing was what non-consumers were living with.

3. **The non-consumption market is almost always larger than the competitive market.** SNHU found 250,000 students in a market their supply-side analysis would have described as crowded. Casper found a billion-dollar company in a market analysis said was saturated. Apple became the world's top camera maker without ever intending to. The competition was visible. The non-consumers were invisible — until someone went looking on the demand side.
