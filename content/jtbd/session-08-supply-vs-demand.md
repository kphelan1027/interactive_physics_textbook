# JTBD — Session 8: Supply-Side vs. Demand-Side Thinking

> *"I always say the greatest lie I was ever told was build it and they will come. Early in my career I built stuff I thought was the best and nobody came. I realised I was building it for myself as opposed to building it for others."*
> — Bobby Moesta (Jan 27, 2026, Memrise session)

---

## The simplest true statement

There are two completely different ways to think about a product. The company's way, and the customer's way. Most companies only know one of them.

Bobby calls these **supply-side** and **demand-side** thinking. And the gap between them — the reason most products fail, most pitches miss, most demos don't convert — is what he calls **The Wall**: a product-market disconnect so thick and so tall that most companies spend their entire careers shouting over it without ever crossing it.

---

## Where we are

Sessions 1–7 built the demand-side toolkit: the struggling moment, the four forces, the six phases, the six systems. That was all demand-side thinking — how customers actually experience change from the inside.

This session steps back and asks: what is the alternative? What does supply-side thinking look like? Why is it the default? And what is the structural difference that makes demand-side thinking so difficult to adopt — and so powerful when you do?

The answer is in one of Bobby's most precise frameworks, and it runs through everything: Casper, Intercom, AutoBooks, and Memrise.

---

## Supply-side thinking: what it looks like from the inside

Bobby grew up as an engineer. Supply-side thinking was his native language.

*"I grew up on what I call the supply side of the world, which is basically how do we understand what we can build and how do we push it to the market."*

Here is what supply-side thinking produces, at its most complete:

```
INPUT
  ↓
Business System
  ↓
Products → Pushed through Sales & Marketing, Design & Development
  ↓
Strategy → Features → Benefits → Experiences → Specs → Attributes
  ↓
Sales Funnel: Awareness → Interest → Consideration → Intent → Evaluation → Purchase
```

Every element of that stack is real, necessary, and well-understood by most product teams. Features, benefits, positioning, the funnel. This is the vocabulary of product management, of quarterly roadmaps, of engineering sprints, of investor decks. It tells you what you're building, how it works, and how leads move through your system.

It tells you almost nothing about why a customer changed their life.

Bobby puts it directly:

*"We will build a product and then we'll go 'all right, who needs this product?' So we'll climb up that wall and we'll look down over the entire market and we'll start to describe what's going on. Typically we'll look at the market and segments and personas and prospects and leads. But that's not what causes people to say today's the day that they need Memrise."*

The supply-side view asks: *who is this for?* It answers in demographics and personas. It never asks the more fundamental question: *when, where, and why, in what specific moment of their life, did a real person decide that today was the day they needed to do something different?*

Bobby learned this from Dr. Deming, who taught him the five W's and two H's: who, what, when, where, why, how, and how much. *"What I realized is that I have to understand who, what, when, and where to determine what, how, and how much I'm going to build. Typically, we're talking about who. But we're not talking about when, where, and why."*

Supply-side thinking gets the who. It mostly skips the when, where, and why. And those three are the whole game.

---

## Demand-side thinking: what it looks like from the inside

Demand-side thinking starts from the opposite end. Not "here's what we built — who wants it?" but "here's a group of people who are struggling — what would help them make progress?"

Bobby maps it precisely. Underneath the supply side's wall, on the customer's side, is this:

```
Current Product (the old way)
  ↓
New Context → Struggling Moment
  ↓
Desired Outcomes
  ↓
Candidates (possible solutions)
  ↓
Hire & Fire Criteria (what would make them pick / reject each)
  ↓
Trade-offs (what they're willing to give up)
  ↓
Basic/Core Quality (minimum bars that can't be compromised)
```

And the movement through this stack maps exactly to the six systems from Session 7:

```
S1 (Making Space)        ←→  Struggling Moment / First Thought
S2 (Learning How)        ←→  Desired Outcomes forming
S3 (Seeing Possibilities) ←→  Candidates / Active Looking
S4 (Making Trade-offs)   ←→  Hire & Fire Criteria / Deciding
S5 (Making Progress)     ←→  First Use
S6 (Building Habits)     ←→  Ongoing Use
```

The customers don't climb up the supply-side wall to find your product. They climb up their own wall from the inside. They have a struggling moment. They form a rough idea of what progress would look like. They look for candidates. They evaluate trade-offs. They hire one product and fire whatever they were doing before. And when they get to the top and look over, *"they just see branded products. They don't know how any of them work. It's up to us to communicate in a way that makes them understand how we can help them make the progress they want."*

---

## The Wall

Between the supply side and the demand side is what Bobby calls The Wall. In his Growth Summit slides, it's drawn as a literal barrier — ten feet thick, a hundred feet high, the product-market disconnect.

Here is the structural reason The Wall exists: **the supply side and the demand side organise reality differently.**

The supply side organises by features, products, and capabilities. *"We have live chat, onboarding flows, customer success tools, feedback widgets."* The demand side organises by struggling moments and desired progress. *"I can't get visitors to convert. I'm losing users before they find value. I need to know what's breaking before my users tell me."*

Same product. Completely different language. Completely different organising principle.

When a supply-side company talks to a demand-side customer, they describe things the customer doesn't have the vocabulary to evaluate. When a demand-side customer talks to a supply-side company, they describe problems the company hasn't thought to name.

This is why features that win engineering awards leave users indifferent. This is why customer research produces endless "users want X" lists that don't translate into products people actually hire. This is why marketing copy full of capability descriptions generates less purchase intent than a single sentence about a struggling moment.

Bobby's definition of product-market fit is crossing The Wall: building a product on the supply side that solves, in the exact right way, the struggling moment that exists on the demand side.

## Casper: what demand-side thinking looks like in practice

The Casper mattress story is Bobby's cleanest example of what happens when you start on the demand side in a market that looks completely locked.

*"If you did an analysis on the market, basically it said there was overcapacity of mattress makers and no room for a new brand."*

Supply-side analysis: market is saturated, competition is fierce, no gap to enter. Game over.

Demand-side analysis: what is the struggling moment?

*"The struggling moment was actually how to buy a mattress. It wasn't about necessarily just the mattress."*

The way Americans bought mattresses in 2005 was: go to a store with 20–100 models, lie on them for one to five minutes, try to make a decision about something you'd sleep on for the next ten years. Most people had no vocabulary for mattress quality. They were terrified of choosing wrong. They bought once every five to fifteen years and had almost no accumulated expertise.

The market wasn't saturated with solutions to the real struggling moment. It was saturated with options for people who already knew how to buy a mattress. The people who didn't know — who wanted a new mattress but didn't know how to navigate the overwhelming, anxiety-producing, time-consuming process of buying one — weren't being served at all. This is non-consumption: people who want to make progress but can't because of knowledge, access, money, or inconvenience.

Casper's three interventions weren't the best mattress in the world. They were:
1. **Simplify the choice:** good / better / best, four sizes — twelve products instead of eighty.
2. **Remove the evaluation anxiety:** all foam, vacuum-sealed, shipped to your house — you don't have to perform "choosing correctly" in a showroom.
3. **Remove the commitment anxiety:** 90-day return policy — if it's wrong, it goes back.

*"We didn't build the best mattress. We didn't build the most affordable mattress. Because we addressed the critical struggling moments, we were able to help people make progress."*

Casper became a billion-dollar company in a market where supply-side analysis said there was no room. Because it started on the demand side.

## Intercom: from "cradle to grave" to four jobs

Bobby worked with Intercom co-founder Des Traynor in 2013, when the company was at five million in revenue and had expected to do ten million. The product was a single integrated platform — CRM, live chat, customer database, email, help desk — all bundled at one price.

Their first tagline, from 2012: **"Manage your customers from cradle to grave."**

Read that again. That is pure supply-side language. It describes what the tool *can do* from the company's perspective. A customer reads it and asks: "But what's my problem that this solves?" The product made no contact with their struggling moment.

Bobby's team interviewed ten diverse customers and found four completely different jobs:

1. **Acquire:** I have traffic but no conversions. Help me chat with visitors and turn them into customers.
2. **Engage:** I have customers but they're not coming back. Help me onboard and retain them.
3. **Learn:** I can see users in the app and I want to understand where they struggle. Help me know what to build.
4. **Support:** My support is breaking as I scale. Help me manage tickets.

Same product. Four completely different struggling moments. Four completely different desired outcomes. And crucially: four completely different competitors. Acquire competed with HubSpot. Support competed with Zendesk. Learn competed with Mixpanel. They weren't one product competing with one set of companies. They were four products competing in four different markets.

The supply-side view had made them invisible: "We do all the things for your customer relationship." The demand-side pivot made them specific: *"If you're losing visitors before they convert, we're the thing that fixes that."*

The results: demand-side marketing changed the keywords they targeted — *"problem keywords were 50% cheaper than feature keywords"* — because they were now competing for searches about the customer's struggling moment, not searches about product capabilities. They repriced into four separate products. They went from five million in 2013 to on track for 75 million by September 2015.

500% growth in eighteen months. From one reframe.

And Bobby adds the cautionary tale: in 2018, a new CMO joined and shifted Intercom back to feature language — "live chat, onboarding, customer support." Sales declined within a year. By 2020 they returned to job language: acquire, engage, support. *"It's the most important story of coming back."*

The supply side is a gravitational pull. It requires active discipline to stay on the demand side.

## AutoBooks: "Where are they in their timeline?"

The AutoBooks case from Session 7 belongs equally in this session because it reveals the supply-vs-demand gap at the most granular level: not just in product design, but in a single sales interaction.

AutoBooks was rebuilding their sales demo every 90 days. It kept not working. From a supply-side perspective, this is a messaging problem — the demo isn't positioned right, the value prop isn't landing, maybe the features aren't competitive enough.

Bobby's diagnostic question was different: *"Where are they in their timeline?"*

That question is pure demand-side thinking. It doesn't ask "what should our demo say?" It asks "what is the customer's current system, and what does *that* system need from us?"

The answer revealed that the sales team was running one demo — optimised for Phase 4 (deciding) — at prospects who were across Phases 2, 3, and 4. A Phase 2 bank prospect was still learning whether the problem was even real enough to prioritise. The Phase 4 demo — specific trade-off comparisons, implementation timelines, pricing structures — was meaningless to them. Worse: it felt like pressure.

Three demos. Each designed as its own system. Each with one designed output: *"Almost 'beg' for the next demo."*

And the sales opener itself was transformed from a capability pitch into a struggling-moment identifier:

*"Which of these applies most to you right now?"*

1. Our executive team has placed an emphasis on growing deposits and/or increasing non-interest fee income.
2. Our latest digital banking upgrade is behind us. We're looking for our next digital banking project to continue to differentiate.
3. When our customers ask how they can get paid online, we don't have a good answer.

Three struggling moments. The prospect identifies where they are. The demo speaks to that. The company stops pushing a solution before the customer has even located their problem.

5x revenue in twelve months.

## Why supply-side is the default

Supply-side thinking is not wrong. It's necessary. You have to know what you can build, how it works, what it costs, how to sell it. The supply side is real.

The problem is that **supply-side thinking is what companies are organised to do.** Engineering teams think in capabilities. Product teams think in features. Marketing teams think in benefits. Sales teams think in objections. The entire organisational grammar of most companies is supply-side.

Demand-side thinking requires a different grammar: struggling moments, desired outcomes, contexts, trade-offs, hire-and-fire criteria. This is not the language of most strategy documents, roadmaps, or OKRs. Adopting it requires actively resisting the pull of the supply-side default.

Bobby's three-tips summary from the January session names this resistance explicitly:

> *"One: find struggling moments. Two: think about the progress people are trying to make, not the products or services first. Three: identify what you need to suck at. Most people try to think about the product they want to build first. If we can understand both the struggling moments and the progress, the products become obvious. They become easy."*

The supply-side approach starts with the product and asks: who needs this? The demand-side approach starts with the struggle and asks: what would help these people make progress? The supply-side approach produces good products that nobody hires. The demand-side approach produces products that might not be the best on any individual dimension — but that are exactly right for the struggling moment they're solving.

*"I've built things like Casper, which is not the best mattress company. I've worked on QuickBooks, which is not the best accounting package. I don't necessarily have to be the best. I have to be the best for these people in that moment of decision."*

## What this means for Memrise

Bobby confirmed directly in the January session that the supply-vs-demand lens applies to Memrise. Here's what it surfaces.

**The supply-side reading of Memrise** looks like: a language-learning app with courses, spaced repetition, AI features, a content library, gamification, streaks, a native speaker video feature. Features. Capabilities. A product.

**The demand-side question:** *What is the struggling moment that causes someone to say "today's the day I'm going to do something about my language"?* 

Bobby's framework predicts there are multiple answers — at minimum three or four distinct struggling moments, each of which represents a different job. Some candidates from the transcript context:

- *I have a work situation where language is holding me back* (the promotion job — progress toward professional outcome)
- *I'm visiting / moving somewhere and I feel embarrassed or cut off* (the connection job — progress toward belonging)
- *I've always said I would learn this language. Something just happened that made it now* (the identity job — progress toward a self-image)
- *I need my child to be bilingual and I want to model it* (the parenting job — progress toward family outcome)

Each of these has a different push, a different pull, a different set of anxieties, a different hire-and-fire criteria, a different definition of "early signs of progress." They are not the same job with variations. They are different jobs.

The supply-side Memrise speaks to all of them with the same interface, the same onboarding, the same marketing copy. The demand-side Memrise would ask: which of these jobs represents 80% of acquisition? What is the specific struggling moment we're best positioned to solve? And are we designing the product backward from *that* struggling moment, or forward from the feature set we've already built?

Sonia's question in the session — *"time and motivation are things users struggle with — are those elements of non-consumption?"* — is a demand-side question in disguise. Bobby's answer: yes, and we might need to make the struggle bigger, not offer a better solution. *"What happens if you don't get the promotion? What are you going to do?"* That is demand-side thinking. Making the struggling moment vivid enough that the customer can feel why today has to be the day.

## Three things to hold onto

1. **Supply-side and demand-side are two different grammars.** Supply-side speaks features, benefits, funnel stages, capabilities. Demand-side speaks struggling moments, desired outcomes, hire-and-fire criteria, trade-offs. Most companies are fluent in supply-side and barely literate in demand-side.

2. **The Wall is the product-market disconnect.** It's not a gap you close by improving your product. It's a gap you close by changing your starting point — from "here's what we built, who wants it?" to "here's who's struggling, what do they need?" Casper, Intercom, AutoBooks, and SNHU all crossed The Wall the same way: by starting on the demand side.

3. **Supply-side thinking is a gravitational pull.** Intercom crossed the wall in 2014 and grew 500% in 18 months. A new CMO in 2018 let the organisation drift back to supply-side language. Sales declined within a year. Staying on the demand side requires active discipline. Every roadmap, every demo, every piece of copy pulls toward supply-side defaults.
