# JTBD — Session 6: The Six Phases of Progress

> *"Deciding is this aspect of actually making trade-offs. And then once they make a commitment to it, they actually have to consume. And are they making the progress they want?"*
> — Bobby Moesta (Jan 27, 2026, Memrise session)

---

## The simplest true statement

Every customer who ever hired a product went through the same six phases to get there. Not the same timeline — the journey can take seconds or years. Not the same path — people loop back, stall, and sometimes abandon entirely. But the same *sequence of systems*: first thought, passive looking, active looking, deciding, first use, ongoing use. Miss one, and you're building in the dark.

---

## Where we are

Sessions 3, 4, and 5 gave us the *forces* — the why of change. The four forces explain what causes someone to move or stay. But they don't tell you *when* in the journey those forces are acting, or what a company should be doing at each moment to help the customer move forward.

That's what the six phases give you. Bobby's framing:

> *"Every time somebody basically makes a change, they go through these six phases of progress. And it can happen literally in seconds and it can actually happen in years."*

This isn't a marketing funnel. Bobby is specific about the distinction. A funnel is a supply-side construct — the company's pipeline. The six phases are a demand-side construct — the customer's *actual journey*. They're not the same thing, and the gap between them (what Bobby calls "The Wall" in his Growth Summit slides) is exactly where product-market fit goes wrong.

---

## Phase 1: First Thought — Making Space

> *"The first thought... this is where they basically start to realize the old way isn't going to work and they have to start to think about something new."*

Something happens. A friend moves into a smaller place. A lease is about to expire. A child leaves home. A colleague gets a promotion for speaking the language you've been meaning to learn. The push has been building, but this is the moment the first question forms.

Bobby's language, from Clay Christensen: *"Questions create spaces in the brain for solutions to fall into."* The first thought *is* that question. Nothing more yet. The customer isn't looking, isn't comparing, isn't anywhere near buying. They just have a question — and for the first time, they have a space in their mind where an answer could live.

**This is a marketing system.** Bobby is precise here: the job of the first thought phase is *not* to show the product. It's to create or locate the question. His example from Casper:

> *"Casper, the mattress company, just kept asking: 'How did you sleep last night?' And if we just keep asking that question, the reality is it creates the space — when the answer is 'I didn't sleep well', they start to think about what they have to do."*

Casper didn't say "buy our mattress." They asked a question. They planted the seed. The question creates the space; the space allows the product to eventually land.

The genius of the obituaries example lives in this phase. Bobby moved his condo advertising from the real estate section — where people were already in active looking — all the way back to the obituaries, where first thoughts were forming:

> *"I interviewed some people and they said, 'I think the first thought was when we went to our friend John's funeral.' They had the conversation on the way home: 'We should probably look at this, because I don't want to move without you and I don't want to move without me.'"*

The obituaries ad didn't say "here's a great condo." It said: *"Need to move? Not sure how? We have a solution team that can help."* The message matched the question — not the product. The result: 38% more traffic, 70% lower advertising cost.

**For Memrise:** What is the "first thought" for a Memrise user? Bobby would push hard on this. It's not "I want to learn Spanish." That's a desire, not a question. The first thought is the specific moment something creates the question — a trip coming up, an embarrassing moment at work, a child starting school in a new country, a parent-in-law who doesn't speak your language. The question is "how am I going to handle this?" *That* is the space. And if Memrise's marketing shows up in the places where those questions form — not in the language app comparison sites where people are already in active looking — the economics of acquisition change completely.

---

## Phase 2: Passive Looking — Learning How

> *"Passive looking is this aspect where now that they have this space, they can actually hold ideas. So this is one of my favourite examples — when I did cars. The moment somebody's lease was up three months before, they hadn't thought about a car, they didn't notice a car. Then they realized, 'Oh my gosh, my lease is up.' Now all of a sudden they notice every car on the road."*

The space is open. Attention has been primed. The customer isn't actively searching — they're noticing. They overhear a conversation, see an ad that suddenly becomes relevant, remember something a friend said. Information flows in passively but it sticks now because there's a place for it.

Bobby frames this phase as *learning* — specifically, learning how big the problem is, what a solution might look like, and whether this is worth pursuing at all. From the Growth Summit slides: the purpose of Phase 2 is **Learning How**.

This is not comparison shopping. The customer isn't evaluating your product against competitors. They're barely even aware you exist. They're asking: *"Is this a real problem? Does anyone else have this problem? What do people do about it?"*

Bobby's systems framing applies here: each phase has inputs, outputs, actions (what the customer *does*), and outcomes (what the customer *says*). In passive looking, the inputs are ambient information. The actions are things like pausing on an article, mentioning something to a partner, clicking on a post in a moment of idle scrolling. The outputs are developing beliefs about the size and shape of the problem. The customer probably doesn't remember most of it and won't flag any of it as "research."

This is why Bobby warns against interviewing people who *want* to buy something. They'll tell you about passive looking as if it barely happened — they'll call it "I started noticing the problem more." But embedded in those casual observations are the real data points: what caught their attention, what language the problem was described in, who they're mentally comparing to. That's the stuff a researcher has to excavate.

---

## Phase 3: Active Looking — Seeing Possibilities

> *"Active looking is where people invest time. They invest time doing research. They invest time to see alternatives. They might test drive. They might come visit the home. Whatever it is."*

Something tips them from passive noticing into deliberate searching. Often this is an event — a lease date confirmed, a meeting booked, a deadline materializing. The question has sharpened. Now the customer has their *binoculars out*, in Bobby's visual metaphor from the Growth Summit slides.

The purpose of this phase, per Bobby: **Seeing Possibilities**. Not evaluating features. Not comparing prices. Seeing what's even *possible* — what categories of solution exist, what the vocabulary of this space is, what other people have done.

Bobby is wry about this phase: *"I hate this part of the process because it's like a kid in a candy store. Oh, it has to have this feature, that feature. They don't even know how the features work but they're trying to compare features one to the other."*

This is the phase where your marketing can backfire spectacularly if it's wrong. If a customer is in active looking — understanding the landscape — and you hit them with a detailed feature comparison, you've answered the wrong question. They're not asking "which product is better?" They're asking "what kind of solution am I even looking for?" Content that educates, frames the problem, and expands the sense of what's possible is doing the work of Phase 3. A feature sheet is doing the work of Phase 4.

---

## Phase 4: Deciding — Making Trade-offs

> *"Deciding is this aspect of actually making trade-offs... They're trying to hack the system, but ultimately it's about making trade-offs to see how they can make that progress."*

An event causes the transition from active looking to deciding. Bobby is specific that this is a *different kind of work*. Active looking is expansive — more is more, the list of possibilities grows. Deciding is compressive — the customer has to narrow. They have to give things up. They have to answer the question: given what I know and what I can afford, which option actually gets me to the progress I want?

The purpose of Phase 4: **Making Trade-offs**.

This is where the hire-and-fire criteria come into play. What would they pick *for* a product? What would disqualify it? What are they willing to sacrifice to get the thing they care most about? These are the questions driving behaviour at this stage — not "which product has the most features" but "which product, at this price, with these trade-offs, is the one I can actually commit to?"

Bobby's AutoBooks case study from his Growth Summit presentation is the cleanest demonstration of why phase-specific design matters so much here. AutoBooks was using a single sales demo for all prospects, regardless of where they were in the timeline. Every 90 days, the sales team had to rebuild the demo because it wasn't working.

The insight: different prospects were arriving at the demo from different phases. Some were in passive looking — they needed to understand what was even possible. Others were already deciding — they needed to understand the specific trade-offs between AutoBooks and the alternative. The same demo couldn't serve both.

Bobby's solution: three demos, each mapped to a phase. Demo 1 for passive lookers: *learning*. Demo 2 for active lookers: *possibilities*. Demo 3 for deciders: *trade-offs*. And critically, the design principle for each demo was that the prospect should "almost beg for the next one" — meaning the demo's job wasn't to close, it was to pull the customer naturally forward into the next phase. The results over 12 months: 3x lead conversion, half the time to close, 6x bank partner growth, 5x revenue.

The same principle applies to any product interaction. An onboarding flow that's designed for Phase 5 (first use) shouldn't also be trying to do the work of Phase 4 (deciding). A trial experience that's designed to help people decide shouldn't be optimised for conversion — it should be optimised for *information that makes the trade-off clear*.

---

## Phase 5: First Use — Making Progress

> *"The first use is really about what are the early signs that make me feel like this is going to help me reach my satisfaction... what does it feel like? What does it see? What does it smell? All the different kinds of senses."*

The decision is made. Money has changed hands (or the app has been downloaded, or the signup completed). Now the customer faces the most vulnerable moment in the entire relationship: do they actually experience any of the progress they were hoping for?

Bobby is specific about what the product has to deliver here: *early signs of progress*. Not full delivery. Not the whole promise. Just enough to make the customer feel that the trajectory is right — that if they keep going, they'll get where they wanted to go.

This is Sonia's question from the session, crystallised into a phase: *"Sometimes the real progress only happens in the long term. What do you do about the short term?"* Bobby's answer:

> *"We want to understand how people feel when they think they're actually making progress. For example: my colleagues are now talking to me in a casual way in their native language as opposed to switching to English. We have to figure out what are the intermediate steps along the way."*

These intermediate steps are not consolation prizes. They're engineered signals. The product team's job in Phase 5 is to identify: what will users be able to *do*, *feel*, or *notice* within their first session, first week, first month — that they couldn't before, and that proves the direction is right? Not eventual fluency. Not the full job done. The first evidence of movement.

This is also where churn often begins. If Phase 5 fails — if the customer can't find those early signs — the four forces reassert themselves. Anxiety spikes. The habit of whatever they were doing before looks more attractive. The push that drove them here fades. They leave not because they found a better product, but because the new product never convincingly showed them the path.

---

## Phase 6: Ongoing Use — Building Habits

> *"System six is about how do we turn it into a habit so it actually starts to become rooted in how they work every day."*

The progress is real now. The customer has made it through Phase 5. The early signs were there. Now the question shifts: does this become part of who they are?

The purpose of Phase 6: **Building Habits**.

Bobby's framing is precise. He's not talking about engagement metrics or DAUs or retention curves. He's talking about the product crossing a threshold where it becomes part of the user's identity and routine — where they stop thinking about it as a new solution and start thinking of it as just *how things are done*. The language is no longer being *learned*; it's being *used*. The budget tool is no longer being *tried*; it's the way the books are kept.

The habit of the present, which was the friction force in Sessions 4 and 5, is now being created *for* the product. The cycle is complete: what was once a barrier to adoption is now the moat that keeps the user. And it's also the vulnerability. Because when the next struggling moment arrives — when life changes, when something new pushes — the cycle begins again from Phase 1, and the user's habit is competing against a new solution's pull.

Bobby's observation from the session: *"Once the habit becomes in place and there's a struggling moment, they go all the way back to the beginning and have a first thought."* The six phases aren't a one-time funnel. They're a repeating loop. Every user you have is either deepening their Phase 6 habit, or quietly beginning a new Phase 1 for your replacement.

---

## The timeline is not linear

Bobby makes this explicit, and it's important enough to call out separately:

> *"It's not linear. People might have been in the midst of deciding and then the pandemic happened. Other things trumped it and they went back to passive looking. They still have the struggle. They still have it. But they've got to do other things first."*

And crucially:

> *"Most of the time we think we lose to somebody, and nine times out of ten we lose to another struggling moment, not to a competitor."*

This is one of the most counterintuitive things Bobby teaches. Churn, failed conversions, users who go quiet mid-trial — these are usually not competitive losses. They're losses to *other struggling moments* that arrived and demanded priority. The pandemic pushed people back. A job change pushed people back. A baby, an illness, a work crisis. The user didn't choose a competitor. They just had a bigger problem to deal with.

This reframes how you think about re-engagement. The question isn't "how do we win them back from the competitor?" It's "has their original struggling moment returned?" Or: "has a new struggling moment arrived that we're now relevant to?" If the push is back, and the pull is still there, and you can reduce the anxiety of re-entry — you may find the timeline wasn't a loss; it was a pause.

## The six phases as six systems

Bobby always frames this as an engineer. Each phase isn't just a step in a journey — it's a *system* with its own logic:

| Phase | Purpose | System Job |
|-------|---------|------------|
| 1 — First Thought | Making Space | Marketing system — create the question |
| 2 — Passive Looking | Learning How | Content / awareness system — educate without pushing |
| 3 — Active Looking | Seeing Possibilities | Discovery system — expand the landscape |
| 4 — Deciding | Making Trade-offs | Sales / trial system — make the choice clear |
| 5 — First Use | Making Progress | Onboarding system — deliver early signs of progress |
| 6 — Ongoing Use | Building Habits | Engagement / retention system — root the product in routine |

The systems view is the most powerful operational implication of the six-phase framework. It means your marketing team, your sales team, your onboarding team, your growth team, and your retention team are each serving a *different phase* — and they should be building and measuring accordingly. Feeding Phase 3 content to Phase 1 people doesn't accelerate the funnel. It confuses it.

## What this means for Memrise

Bobby confirmed the framework applies to Memrise in the session:

> *"Can you see how it's relevant to Memrise?"*
> *"Yes, absolutely."*

Three specific implications:

**Phase 1 (First Thought):** Memrise likely acquires most of its users in Phase 3 or Phase 4 — when they're already actively searching for a language app. This is expensive and competitive. Bobby's obituaries move was about going one phase earlier — targeting people in the *forming* of the first thought, not the execution of an existing one. What are the moments in life that create the "I need to figure out this language thing" question? Job changes. Travel. New relationships. Children starting school. A product that shows up in those moments — not with features, but with the question itself — is playing a different (and cheaper) game.

**Phase 5 (First Use):** This is probably where the most users fail, and it's connected directly to Sonia's question in the session. Memrise's first session has to deliver not fluency, but a *specific feeling* — a feeling that Bobby would want identified from actual user interviews. What does it feel like to have had a genuinely good first lesson? What can the user *do* or *say* or *notice* the next day that they couldn't before? Building backward from that feeling, rather than forward from the content library, is Phase 5 design.

**Phase 6 (Ongoing Use):** The "building habits" phase is a Memrise core competency — streaks, spaced repetition, notifications. But Bobby would ask: are these building habits in service of the *job*, or in service of the product? There's a difference. A habit that feels coercive ("don't break your streak!") may be retaining the metric without retaining the meaning. A habit that feels like progress ("I just said something to my colleague in their language without thinking about it") is sticky because the user is getting what they actually came for.

## Three things to hold onto

1. **Every customer goes through the same six phases.** First thought, passive looking, active looking, deciding, first use, ongoing use. The timeline varies from seconds to years, but the sequence doesn't.

2. **Each phase is its own system.** What works at Phase 1 (asking a question to create space) is wrong at Phase 4 (where you need to help someone make a trade-off). Mismatched phase-to-message is where most marketing and onboarding fails.

3. **Churn is usually not a competitive loss — it's a timeline interruption.** Most users don't leave for a better product. Another struggling moment showed up and pre-empted them. Knowing this changes how you think about re-engagement entirely.
