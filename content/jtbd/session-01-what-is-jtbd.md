# JTBD — Session 1: What is Jobs to Be Done?

> *"People don't buy products; they hire them to do a Job in their life and make progress."*
> — Bobby Moesta (Jan 27, 2026, Memrise session)

---

## The simplest true statement

Nobody wakes up wanting to buy your product. They wake up wanting to make progress on something that's frustrating them. If your product helps them do that, they hire it. If something else does it better, they fire you.

---

## Where this came from

Bobby Moesta co-created Jobs to Be Done with Clay Christensen at Harvard in the 1990s while working on product failures at Ford. The insight wasn't academic — it came from watching people not buy things they "should" want, and asking why.

The answer was always the same: the product was fine. But it wasn't solving the right problem, for the right person, at the right moment.

Bobby has since applied this framework across 3,500 products, from Casper mattresses to QuickBooks to the iPhone roadmap. He teaches it at MIT every January.

---

## What a "Job" actually is

A Job is **the progress a person is trying to make in a particular struggling circumstance**.

Three parts to that definition:

| Component | What it means | Example |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| **Progress** | The change the person is trying to create | "I want to feel confident speaking Italian at work" |
| **Struggling circumstance** | The specific context that triggered the need | "I'm about to meet our Milan office for the first time" |
| **Person** | Not a demographic — a person in a moment | Not "25–35 year olds" — "someone who just got a new international role" |

The struggling circumstance is the key. The same person has completely different Jobs in different circumstances. A Memrise user learning Spanish for a holiday has a different Job to a Memrise user learning Spanish for a new job. Same product. Different hire.

---

## The shift this creates

Most companies build products from the **supply side**: here's what we can make, here are its features, here's why it's better.

Bobby teaches the **demand side**: what is the person trying to do? What's making them miserable right now? What would progress actually look like for them?

Supply-side thinking produces feature lists.  
Demand-side thinking produces products people actually hire.

---

## Why it matters for Memrise

Bobby confirmed this directly in the January session: "The framework is absolutely relevant to Memrise."

The question isn't "how do we make a better language learning app?" It's: **what Job are people hiring Memrise to do — and are we solving *that* problem, or a different one?**

Bobby's Intercom example is instructive: they thought they had one product. JTBD revealed they had four completely different Jobs being served by the same tool — acquire, engage, learn, support. When they restructured around those Jobs, they grew 500% in 18 months.

## Three things to hold onto

1. **Hire/fire language is intentional.** Products aren't chosen — they're hired for a Job, and fired when something does it better.
2. **The Job is in the person's life, not in your app.** You're solving a problem in their world; the app is just the tool they use to do it.
3. **Circumstance > demographics.** "35-year-old professional" tells you nothing. "35-year-old about to give a presentation in a second language for the first time" tells you everything.
