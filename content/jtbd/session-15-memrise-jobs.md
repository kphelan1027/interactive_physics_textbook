# JTBD — Session 15: Applying JTBD to Memrise — What Jobs Are Users Actually Hiring For?

> *"Can you see how it's relevant to Memorize? Can somebody give me an example?"*
> — Bobby Moesta, January 27 2026

> *"Sonia: The Intercom example will resonate with a lot of us in terms of what we're trying to do."*
> — Sonia Souto, same session

---

## The simplest true statement

Nobody downloads Memrise because they want to use a language learning app. They download it because something in their life created a struggling moment around language — and Memrise looked like it could help them make progress. The question is: what was that struggling moment? Because the answer is different for different people, and Memrise is probably serving several completely distinct jobs at once — without necessarily knowing which one each user is actually in.

---

## Where we are

Sessions 1 through 14 built the full toolkit: struggling moments, the four forces, the six phases, supply vs demand, non-consumption, the JTBD interview, and four major case studies. In every case, the pattern was the same. Start on the demand side. Find the struggling moment. Let the product follow.

Bobby confirmed at the end of the January session that this framework applies directly to Memrise. Sonia immediately identified the Intercom parallel. That's not an accident.

This session doesn't introduce new theory. It applies everything we've learned directly to the product you're building.

---

## The Intercom parallel

Recall what Bobby found at Intercom. They had one product, one price, and believed they were building "the best way to manage your customer from cradle to grave." Growth had plateaued at £5M.

Bobby's team interviewed ten customers and found four completely different jobs:

1. **Acquire** — help me convert website visitors into paying customers
2. **Engage** — help me onboard and retain the customers I have
3. **Learn** — help me understand where customers struggle in the product
4. **Support** — help me manage support tickets at scale

One product, four jobs. Different competitors for each. Different success metrics. Different pricing sensitivity. Once Intercom organised around the jobs — separate messaging, separate pricing, separate features — they went from £5M to £75M in 18 months.

When Bobby asked the Memrise team "can you see how this is relevant?" Sonia's answer was immediate: yes, the Intercom example resonates.

The question this session asks is: what are Memrise's jobs?

---

## Evidence from the sessions: four jobs already emerging

Bobby never ran a formal JTBD study on Memrise users in these sessions. But over three workshops, four distinct jobs surfaced — through the team's own prototyping work, interview findings, and casual observations. They weren't labelled as jobs at the time. They are now.

---

### Job 1: "Help me feel closer to a culture through a language"

*Sandra Zavala's job. Surfaced through her prototyping work on consumer learners.*

This job was identified through seven user interviews and a careful audit of "problem words." Sandra's initial job statement was: **"Help me improve myself through learning a language for fun and personal growth."**

The refined version: **"Help me feel closer to a culture through a language — without adding stress or obligation to my day."**

The person in this job is not primarily trying to speak the language. Out of seven users Sandra interviewed, none of them mentioned speaking as a desired outcome. They want to *understand* — to catch words in a film, recognise a song, surprise themselves. They describe this as "fun," but when Bobby pushed on what kind of fun, the answers were revealing: peace, simplicity, connection. One Norwegian learner described it using a ukulele analogy — he had someone custom-making him an instrument, didn't care if it took ten years, just wanted something peaceful in his life that was entirely his.

Bobby's response to that: **"Words are just packages of emotions and actions. 'Fun' is not a variable — it's an effect. Our job is to figure out what causes it."**

The person in this job likely doesn't measure progress in terms of test scores or curriculum completion. They measure it in moments of recognition. When they understand a joke in a TV show. When they can follow a conversation at a dinner party even if they don't join it. When the language stops feeling foreign.

This user is in **passive looking or early active looking** for most of their tenure with Memrise. They're not in a hurry. The push is gentle (I've always wanted to understand Italian), the pull is cultural (I want to feel close to this place and its people), and the anxiety is low — unless you add streaks, daily reminders, or gamification that turns their peaceful hobby into a job.

---

### Job 2: "Help me step it up — I need language for a real reason now"

*The SNHU parallel. Closest to Bobby's "new sense of responsibility" job.*

This job isn't explicitly named in the Memrise sessions, but it maps directly onto what Bobby described at SNHU: 80% of their online students were in "help me step it up" — not because of abstract ambition, but because something changed in their lives that gave them a new reason to act.

For language learning, this job is triggered by:
- A job offer in a new country
- A relationship where the partner's family speaks another language
- A promotion that requires presenting in a second language
- A move abroad that's now actually happening

Bobby was explicit about this in the January session when Sonia asked about time and motivation:

> *"Sometimes we have to make the struggle bigger. What happens if you don't get the promotion? What are you going to do? The negative part is actually an essential part to giving them the motivation to move forward."*

The person in this job is not learning "for fun." There is a real consequence attached. That consequence is what Bobby would call the emotional content that makes the struggling moment memorable — and actionable.

Critically, this is the job where **non-consumption lives**. How many people *need* to learn a language right now — for a trip, a relationship, a job — but have tried apps before and found them either too slow, too child-like, too disconnected from their actual context? They gave up. Not because they stopped wanting to make progress, but because nothing they tried was good enough to get across the river.

Harry Snow's prototyping work surfaced a very specific version of this job: **"Help me connect with my partner's family."** The locksmith moment was sitting at a dinner table, realising you're the outsider in the group, and wanting to engage. That's a highly specific context — and it generates very different requirements than "I want to learn French."

---

### Job 3: "Help me and my team learn a language for work"

*Sonia Souto's B2B job. Prototyping focused on construction workers.*

Sonia surfaced a completely different job from an existing B2B customer who was purchasing bulk subscriptions: **"Help me and my team learn a new language for work."**

The initial user she focused on: **construction workers** — people who may not have much formal education history, who have physically demanding days, and who have very limited time and cognitive bandwidth for learning at the end of a shift.

This job has completely different variables from Job 1 and Job 2:
- Progress is team-visible, not just personal
- Cognitive load matters enormously — these learners need something that doesn't feel like school
- Content relevance is non-negotiable — safety vocabulary, site instructions, working with supervisors
- The buyer (HR manager, site manager) is not the same person as the learner

Bobby's advice when Sonia presented this: three variables at three levels is hard for a first prototype run. Get it down to an L4 — two levels, four prototypes, paired comparisons. The signal you're looking for is which combination doesn't make people hate it, not which combination makes everyone love it.

The AutoBooks insight applies here directly. The HR manager buying Memrise for a construction team is in a different place in the buying timeline than a consumer learner. They need a different conversation, a different demo, a different set of struggling-moment statements.

---

### Job 4: "Give me a morning routine I can feel committed to"

*The Duolingo observation. The job nobody talks about but Bobby spotted immediately.*

This one is uncomfortable, which is probably why it's the most important.

Kasia Bania and Harry Snow interviewed a user who had a 500-day streak on Duolingo and couldn't say a single sentence in Italian. When they reported this to Bobby, his response was not "they're a bad user." It was:

> *"The irrational becomes rational with context. If somebody's done a 500-day streak and can't speak Italian, they're not in the language-learning business. They're using the app as a morning routine — as a replacement for reading the paper. The outcome is commitment and routine, not language acquisition. Duolingo might be doing a completely different job for 80% of its users."*

Bobby made this even more pointed:

> *"When you really want to learn language, come to Memrise. If you just want to do a morning routine, Duolingo works great for you. Call it out."*

This is both a warning and an opportunity.

**The warning**: if Memrise tries to serve this job, it will end up in the same race as Duolingo — streaks, gamification, dopamine loops, features optimised for daily engagement rather than actual language acquisition. That's a product strategy built on the wrong job.

**The opportunity**: there's a non-consumption gap here. The person who actually wants to make progress in a language — who is genuinely in Job 1 or Job 2 — probably tried Duolingo first, accumulated a streak, and then quit in frustration because their Italian still wasn't usable. They're now sitting in non-consumption. They want to make progress. They don't know if anything can actually help them.

That person is Memrise's real non-consumption market — and they can only be reached if Memrise is honest about which job it's serving.

## The Intercom map, applied to Memrise

If you lay the four jobs above against the Intercom model, a structure starts to emerge:

| Job | Struggling moment | Primary competition | Bobby's framework |
|-----|-------------------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Feel closer to a culture | Passive longing, mild dissatisfaction | Nothing (non-consumption) | Passive looking → cultural connection |
| Step it up | Real life event: new responsibility | Nothing / giving up (non-consumption) | Active looking → deciding |
| Help my team | Company language gap | Corporate training, generic B2B tools | B2B buying timeline (AutoBooks parallel) |
| Morning routine | Daily habit need | Duolingo, Headspace, games | Already served — risky territory |

Job 1 and Job 2 are both genuine non-consumption opportunities. Most people who want cultural connection or career-driven language skills have tried something and stopped. They don't think anything will actually work for them. The struggling moment exists. The pull exists. The anxiety is: *will this be another thing that doesn't work?*

That anxiety is the wall. Bobby's four forces tell you exactly what to address.

---

## Bobby's direct input on Memrise's hardest UX problem

Sonia put this question to Bobby directly in the January session:

> *"A lot of users tell us that time and motivation are things they struggle with. Are those elements of non-consumption?"*

Bobby's answer was yes — and it was more nuanced than "just add reminders."

> *"Sometimes we have to make the struggle bigger. For example, when they say 'I want a promotion' — ask: what happens if you don't get the promotion? What are you going to do? Most people don't want to bring up the negative part. But the negative part is actually essential to giving them the motivation to move forward."*

Marcin added: *"That has really interesting implications for how we ask questions — how do we make the problem more intense for them?"*

This is the core UX challenge for Jobs 2 and 3. The struggling moment exists, but it hasn't been made *big enough* for the user to feel the urgency. They say they want to learn. They don't feel the consequence of not learning. The product's job, in those first few sessions, is not just to teach — it's to help the user understand why this matters now.

That's a messaging problem, an onboarding problem, and a content problem simultaneously.

---

## The onboarding implication: context before content

Kevin shared a tool he built for himself — a spoken onboarding experience that asked eight context-based questions before generating a personalised learning plan. Based on Stephen Krashen's theory: you only receive input slightly above your current level, in your actual language context.

Bobby's reaction:

> *"You're on the right track. What I'd want to make sure is that at least four of the eight questions are context-based and four are outcome-based — because it's the combination of context and outcome that determines the pressure, the rigor, the amount of time they're willing to spend."*

Then he said something deeper:

> *"If we can help people learn how they learn, that's how we're actually going to help them get where they want to go. I believe everyone has their own pedagogy."*

This connects directly to the job structure. A person in Job 1 (feel closer to a culture) has a completely different pedagogy from a person in Job 2 (step it up for a work reason). One needs to feel the pleasure of recognition; the other needs to feel the consequences of not progressing fast enough. One needs peace; the other needs urgency.

One onboarding flow cannot serve both.

---

## What the supply-side view gets wrong

From the supply side, Memrise has a product: vocabulary, courses, spaced repetition, streaks, gamification. From the demand side, four completely different people are arriving at that product — and all of them are at a different phase of their buying timeline, in a different struggling moment, with a different definition of progress.

The supply-side instinct is to add features: better AI, more languages, more content. Bobby's warning at Intercom applies equally here: *the more features you add, the more anxiety you create.* If someone in Job 1 (cultural connection, peaceful, no pressure) lands in an onboarding flow that immediately asks them to set a daily streak goal, you've just triggered their anxiety — before they've felt a single moment of progress.

The struggling moment you need to solve first is not "how do we teach language better." It's "how do we make each person feel, in the first three minutes, that this product understands exactly what they're here for."

## Three things to hold onto

1. **Memrise is almost certainly serving multiple jobs simultaneously — and probably hasn't named them yet.** The Intercom parallel is real. The four jobs that surfaced through the workshops — cultural connection, step it up, team/B2B, morning routine — are a starting hypothesis, not a conclusion. But they're grounded in actual interviews Bobby's team ran, and in Bobby's own observations. Start there.

2. **The morning-routine job is a trap.** If Memrise optimises for Duolingo-style engagement, it wins the "I want to feel like I'm learning" job — but loses the "I actually want to make progress" job. Bobby's advice was explicit: call it out. Know which job you're serving. Don't try to be all things.

3. **Non-consumption is the real growth lever for Jobs 1 and 2.** Most people who want cultural connection or career-driven language learning have tried something and given up. They're not switching from a competitor. They're re-entering from nothing, with an anxiety that says "I've tried this before and it didn't work." That anxiety — not a feature gap — is the wall between Memrise and massive growth.
