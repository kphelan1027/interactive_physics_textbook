# JTBD — Session 3: The Four Forces of Progress

> *"I use it every day. I use it with my kids. I use it with my family. I use it with my clients. Every time I'm trying to understand why someone does anything new — this is the frame."*
> — Bobby Moesta (Jan 27, 2026, Memrise session)

---

## The simplest true statement

Nobody switches randomly. Every time a person moves from one product, habit, or situation to another, four forces are acting on them simultaneously. Two push and pull them toward change. Two hold them back. Understanding all four — not just the obvious two — is what separates companies that actually change behaviour from companies that just add features.

---

## Where this came from

Bobby built over a thousand homes in the Detroit metro area. Three customer types: first-time buyers, divorced families, and downsizers — people selling the house they'd raised their kids in to move into something smaller.

Nobody randomly bought a condo. So Bobby did something unusual: he didn't talk to people who *wanted* to buy. He talked to people who *had* bought.

> *"I talk to people who actually bought and did and made progress because embedded in their story is all the clues we need to understand how to help other people."*

Those interviews revealed a pattern. Every single person who made the decision to move had the same four forces acting on them. Bobby named the framework and has used it ever since — with clients, with students at Kellogg and MIT, and, by his own admission, with his own family when trying to understand why anyone does anything new.

---

## The four forces

```
              FUEL FOR CHANGE
         ┌──────────────────────────┐
         │  Push of the situation   │  ← What's making the old way unbearable?
         │  Pull of the new         │  ← What's making the new way attractive?
         └──────────────────────────┘
                      ↕
         ┌──────────────────────────┐
         │  Anxiety of the new      │  ← What questions don't they have answers to?
         │  Habit of the present    │  ← What do they love about the old way?
         └──────────────────────────┘
              FRICTION TO CHANGE
```

A person switches when the fuel exceeds the friction. Simple in theory. Complicated in practice, because companies almost always focus entirely on the fuel — and ignore the friction.

---

## Force 1: Push of the situation

The push is the discomfort accumulating in the current product or situation. It's the invisible pressure building on the left side of the river.

In the condo case:

> *"The kids have left home. The house is way bigger than they need. They had four or five bedrooms, a lot of upkeep, yards. And all of a sudden it's like, I don't want to do the yard work anymore. We can't travel because I can't leave the house for two or three weeks."*

The yard, the travel restrictions, the maintenance — none of these is a crisis on its own. But they accumulate. And at some point the stack gets heavy enough that the person begins to think: *we should probably look at this.*

Bobby's framing language for push: **"When I am..."**

- *"When the house is too big..."*
- *"When I don't want to do the outside maintenance work..."*
- *"When I want to travel but I can't..."*

It's empathetic. It lives in the customer's experience, not in the product's feature set. No product attribute ever pushed anyone anywhere. A *situation* pushed them.

**The crucial caveat:** Push alone produces complaints, not action.

> *"This is where your parents will complain about the house, but they won't do anything. All they do is complaining, complaining, complaining."*

Bobby calls this: **"Bitching ain't switching."**

Push creates the question in the brain. It doesn't create the answer. For that, you need the pull.

---

## Force 2: Pull of the new solution

The pull is when the person can see — concretely — what progress looks like. It's the vision across the river.

In the condo case, the couple is driving somewhere and spots a building. The push makes them slow down. The pull makes them stop. They walk in saying they're "just looking" or "here for decorating ideas." Both are lies — comfortable ones they tell themselves. What's really happening is they're testing whether the vision of progress is real.

What made them stop?

> *"Oh my gosh, this is perfect. It's got the gourmet kitchen. You know what? The old house needs a roof — we don't have to pay for the roof anymore because it's part of the homeowners association. Single-floor living. A place where the kids can come and stay."*

Bobby's framing language for pull: **"So I can..."**

- *"So I can have single-floor living..."*
- *"So I don't have to navigate the stairs..."*
- *"So I can actually travel without worrying about the house..."*

The pull is an outcome, not a feature. The gourmet kitchen matters not because of the kitchen — but because of what it means to the person's life. The "no roof" matters not as a technical fact about ownership structures — but because it removes a looming anxiety about future cost.

Bobby is firm on the distinction between push/pull and the tired old marketing concept of pain/gain:

> *"Most people would say that's pain and gain. But I would tell you it's more context and outcome. Being able to understand context and outcome."*

The difference: pain/gain is about your product. Context and outcome is about *their life*.

---

## Force 3: Anxiety of the new solution

Here's where most companies go wrong.

The instinct, when you want more people to switch, is to add features. More value. Better product. Stronger pull. Bobby watched this happen in his own business and learned something counterintuitive:

> *"What I learned in business school is they told me just add more features and people will buy. Well, that's actually not true. The more features I add, the more anxiety I get — because I don't need all those features."*

More features = more uncertainty = more reasons to pause.

Anxiety is the collection of unanswered questions that block the person from committing. They're not necessarily logical. They don't need to be. They just need to feel unresolved.

In the condo case:

> *"Where are the kids going to sleep when they come? Who are my neighbours? Where's the grocery store? How much can I get for my house?"*

And then there's the bigger one. Six weeks after the down payment, families would come back and say they couldn't move. Because:

> *"We spent the last six weeks cleaning out two closets. It took us literally six weeks. We've got a full basement we have to clean out and we had to use three boxes of Kleenex just to go through it. We're never going to be able to move."*

The stuff. The emotional weight of decades of accumulated possessions. That was the anxiety. Not the condo. Not the mortgage. The *packing process*.

Bobby's response was not to add a feature to the condo. It was to reduce the specific anxiety that was killing the sale:

> *"I built a storage facility across the street. I contracted with a moving company and built a sorting room in the clubhouse. When you did move, you could have the stuff stored for two years for free and you could actually sort it and give your kids everything they needed. It gave them time — as opposed to it being a big rush job."*

The result: a **28% bump in sales**.

Nothing about the condo changed. The condo was fine. What changed was one targeted piece of friction — the unresolved question of *what do we do with all our stuff?* — got answered.

Bobby's framing language for anxiety: big questions they need answered. If the question hangs open, they don't move.

---

## Force 4: Habit of the present

Habit is subtler than anxiety. It's not fear of the new. It's love of the old.

It's the things they'll miss. The trade-offs they'll have to make. The life they'll leave behind.

In the condo case, the most surprising habit force was a piece of furniture:

> *"One of the things we heard from a design perspective is that most people said they didn't want a dining room."*

So Bobby designed the condos without one. And then discovered why people weren't buying.

> *"What is the dining room table to your parents? It's the emotional bank account of their entire life. Every holiday, every birthday."*

The dining room table was a shrine. And if there was nowhere to put it, they weren't moving. Not even if they could get a new roof paid for, single-floor living, and a two-car garage. The table was a hard stop.

The solution was elegant and absurd at the same time: they offered to make the second master suite slightly smaller and add a space where the table *could* fit — technically. Even though eating at it would be nearly impossible.

> *"The trade-off they were willing to make was: if I don't know what to do with the dining room table, I'd rather have this space where it's like a shrine. We can look at it, but we can never actually eat at it. And they were willing to make that trade-off."*

Result: a **14% bump in sales**.

The insight: what people *say* they want in the abstract (no dining room, we're done with holidays) is not the same as what drives their decision at the moment of commitment. The habit force is emotional, often unconscious, and usually one layer deeper than anyone admits.

Bobby's framing language for habit: trade-offs they have to make, things they have to give up.

---

## The system view

Bobby is explicit that this is a system, not a list:

> *"I think of this as a system."*

The four forces interact. You can have enormous push and pull — fuel in abundance — and still lose the sale because anxiety or habit is too strong. The person who really wants to move but can't face packing their house is real. The person who wants to switch language apps but can't face losing their current streak is real.

This is why Bobby's diagnostic question isn't "how do we make our product better?" It's: **"Which of the four forces is the binding constraint right now?"**

Sometimes the push isn't strong enough yet — they don't feel it enough to act. Make the struggle bigger. (Bobby explicitly recommended this in the Memrise session when Sonia raised "time and motivation" as barriers: *"Sometimes we actually have to make the struggle bigger — help them realise that if you don't learn this, then you have a bigger choice to make."*)

Sometimes the pull isn't visible — they can't see what progress looks like. Show them.

Sometimes anxiety is the blocker — they'd switch if they could just answer one question. Answer it.

Sometimes habit is the anchor — they'd move if they could bring one thing with them. Let them bring it.

The 28% and 14% bumps in Bobby's condo business came entirely from working on the friction side of the equation. Not better condos. Not lower prices. Not stronger advertising. Targeted reduction of specific anxieties and specific habits.

---

## Why companies default to the wrong half

Most companies have great product intuition on the fuel side. They know how to articulate what's wrong with the status quo. They know how to paint a vision of progress. These are familiar marketing moves.

The friction side is harder because:
1. It requires going deep into the customer's life, not just their product preferences
2. Anxieties feel irrational from the outside ("just pack the house!") but are completely rational from the inside
3. Habits are often invisible to the customer themselves — they won't tell you they can't leave the dining table; they'll tell you they don't want a dining room
4. Adding features feels like progress. Answering an anxiety or honouring a habit doesn't have an obvious ROI attached

Bobby's warning is worth taking seriously:

> *"The more features I add, the more anxiety I get."*

Every unnecessary feature is a potential new question. Every added option is a new source of uncertainty. Reduction — of complexity, of switching cost, of unanswered questions — is often the highest-leverage move available.

---

## What this looks like for Memrise

The four forces frame applies directly. When a user signs up for Memrise and then doesn't come back, the question isn't "was our product good enough?" It's: which of the four forces explain the dropout?

- **Push:** Was the struggling moment that brought them here real enough and sustained enough to keep them coming back? Or was it a fleeting impulse?
- **Pull:** Can they see what progress looks like — concretely — before they've made enough progress to feel it? Early signs of progress (Bobby's "system five") are critical here.
- **Anxiety:** What question hangs open? "Will I be embarrassed if I try to speak?" "How do I know this is working?" "What if I'm too old to learn a language?"
- **Habit:** What are they giving up? Time. The feeling that they're already managing fine. An existing routine that language learning has to displace.

Bobby's most provocative insight for Memrise comes from the Casper case. The mattress company's struggling moment wasn't "I need a better mattress." It was "I don't know how to buy a mattress." The product solved a friction problem disguised as a product problem.

The equivalent for Memrise might be: the struggle some users have isn't "I don't have a good language learning tool." It's "I don't know how to become a person who actually learns a language." That's a much bigger anxiety. And it won't be solved by adding a new feature.

## Three things to hold onto

1. **Two forces create the fuel, two create the friction.** Push and pull are necessary but not sufficient. Anxiety and habit can block change even when the fuel is overwhelming.

2. **Adding features is often the wrong move.** More features can increase anxiety. The 28% bump came from removing a friction (storage), not from building a better condo.

3. **The friction forces live beneath the surface.** People will tell you they don't want a dining room. They won't tell you the dining room table is sacred. You have to investigate — not just ask.
