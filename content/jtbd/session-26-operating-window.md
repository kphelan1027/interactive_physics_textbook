# JTBD — Session 26: The Operating Window — Find the Thresholds, Not the Optimum

> *"What I want you to do is build a set of prototypes that help me find the window of fun. Not what fun is — when is it not fun anymore, and when is it not enough fun?"*
> — Bobby Moesta, February 26 2026, Prototyping to Learn Session, Memrise

> *"Once you find it then you actually have an operating window in which you can kind of work."*
> — Bobby Moesta, same session

---

## The simplest true statement

You can't design to an optimum you can't locate. But you can always find the edges of where something stops working. Find both edges, and everything in between is your design space.

That is the operating window.

---

## Where this came from

February 26 2026. Bobby is watching Sandra Zavala describe her prototyping challenge. She has been interviewing users about what "fun" means in language learning. She knows fun is a pabulum word (Session 23). She knows it is an effect, not a cause (Session 21). But she is stuck on the same problem that traps most product teams: she is trying to design *towards* fun, and she does not know where that target is.

Bobby's response draws a diagram. Two dimensions. Two axes.

> *"There's fun. This is like — not fun and not fun. This is not fun because it's too easy. And this is not fun because it's too hard. Right? And then there's progress — there's not enough progress and then there's too much progress."*

He continues:

> *"The goal is really to find these thresholds. Because I don't think there's an optimum for everybody — but there's one where you can say, for some people this is not enough fun, and for other people that's too much fun."*

The insight is clean. You are not searching for a single peak. You are searching for two boundaries. Find both, and everything between them is safe to design inside. That space is the operating window.

---

## Why "what is fun?" is the wrong question

Sandra had been asking users: what does fun mean to you? Bobby's diagnosis is exact:

> *"If you ask them what fun is, they say: 'I don't know. It's just fun.' But if you ask them what's not fun — 'It's not fun when I got to spend three hours on this. It's not fun when it's not challenging enough.' So there's the other side: when there's too little or too much."*

The reason users cannot answer "what is fun?" is that fun is a state, not a thing. It is a felt experience that arises from a combination of conditions being in the right relation to each other. You can no more point at fun than you can point at temperature. But you can tell immediately when it is too cold or too hot.

The operating window is the temperature range where people are comfortable. You find it not by asking "what is the right temperature?" but by finding the cold threshold and the hot threshold. The design space is everything in between.

Bobby's instruction to Sandra:

> *"Instead of asking them outside their category — ask them inside the category. Tell me about something you did where you didn't feel like you made enough progress. And tell me about a place where it was just too hard to make it — it felt like too big a lift to make progress."*

Two questions. Two thresholds. That is the whole method.

---

## The Wrigley experiment

Bobby had worked on this exact problem for Wrigley — chewing gum. The pattern he found is counterintuitive:

> *"The more and more you got higher and higher to liking, you'd actually get more people to hate it. So for example, it would turn out to be a situation of: how do I get the people not to love it, but not to hate it? And ultimately I could satisfy the most people if I could get them in the center of this situation."*

This is the same shape as the operating window, applied to flavour intensity. The more Wrigley pushed the flavour towards what the most enthusiastic fans loved, the more they created rejection among the majority. The operating window sat in the middle: nobody's first choice, nobody's dealbreaker. That is Wrigley's entire business model for its best-selling products. Not optimising for love. Locating the window.

If you recognise this as the same concept as Session 24 (Minimize Negative Preference), you are right — it is the same underlying logic. But there is a distinction worth holding:

- **Minimize Negative Preference** (Session 24) is the strategic insight: design for the middle of the distribution, not the peak.
- **The Operating Window** (this session) is the *methodology* for finding where that middle actually is: build two sets of prototypes to locate the thresholds, not one prototype aimed at the perceived optimum.

The first is the goal. The second is how you get there.

---

## Two sets of prototypes, not one

Bobby's instruction to Sandra is architecturally important:

> *"To me, it's two sets of prototypes that actually help you. So these are two: I'd have a focused set of prototypes here around this definition — and then a focused set of prototypes around this definition. And then put them together. Then you'll have a window here of fun and progress, and you'll know what that is and what to actually sit in."*

Most teams build one set of prototypes. They pick a direction they believe is good and test whether users prefer it over the status quo. This is prototyping to verify — it checks whether the hypothesis is right. It never locates the boundaries.

Bobby's approach runs two experiments in parallel:

**Experiment A:** Push towards the "not enough" threshold. Make the experience progressively simpler, shorter, shallower. Find the point where users say: this is so easy it stopped feeling useful. That is one edge of the window.

**Experiment B:** Push towards the "too much" threshold. Make the experience progressively more demanding, longer, deeper. Find the point where users say: this feels like too big a lift. That is the other edge.

The operating window is the space between those two edges. Every design decision you make from that point forward should land inside it. Not touching either boundary. Not trying to push to some imagined peak in the centre. Just inside the window.

## The Zest case study: operating windows in marketing

Bobby described a company called Zest — a cooking education company — that used this approach to rebuild its entire marketing system.

Zest had seven variables in its lead-generation content: four video hook styles, and three core topics (too much spending, learning to cook for someone else, wanting independence). Each variable had two levels: on or off. Seven variables at two levels would theoretically require 128 combinations to test fully.

Instead, Zest used a Taguchi-designed experiment — eight specific combinations selected by a mathematical cheat sheet — to cover the full space with 80% less testing work.

From those eight ad concepts, they discovered that the most important single finding was what *did not work*: gamified framing. When the content felt like a game, people became anxious and converted worse. The operating window for Zest's tone sat clearly on the "practical learning and healthy living" side, away from gamification entirely.

The result: conversion improved by 150%.

Notice what the Zest team was really doing. They were not asking: what is the best ad? They were testing the system — all the variables in combination — to find which variable had the biggest effect, and where the boundary was between what worked and what triggered rejection. The operating window for their content was: practical, health-adjacent, no gamification.

That is a threshold finding, not an optimum finding. It tells Zest less about what to maximise and more about what to avoid — and avoidance is, in most cases, the more durable strategic asset.

## The two-axis version: fun and progress together

Bobby's diagram for Sandra was not one axis. It was two:

```
     Not enough progress
             |
             |
Too easy ----+---- Too hard
(not fun)    |     (not fun)
             |
     Too much progress
```

The four quadrants represent four different ways an experience can fail:
- **Too easy + not enough progress:** users disengage because nothing is happening
- **Too hard + not enough progress:** users give up because the effort exceeds the reward
- **Too easy + too much progress:** users feel like the product is doing the work for them — feels hollow
- **Too hard + too much progress:** users feel overwhelmed and anxious despite making gains

The operating window sits in the middle: enough challenge to feel real, enough visible progress to feel worthwhile. Not too simple, not too demanding.

For language learning specifically, Bobby was watching Sandra think through exactly this geometry. The users who said learning was "fun" were all inside this window, though they could not articulate it. The users who said they lost the habit had, without knowing it, crossed one of the four edges.

The product team's job is to find those edges through prototyping — not to ask users where the middle is. Users live in the middle; they do not know where the edges are until they cross them. The prototypes push them to the edges on purpose, so the team can map the territory.

---

## Why this changes how you run discovery

Most product discovery works by asking users what they want — or by observing what they do — and then designing towards the things that seem most popular. This approach has a structural flaw: it concentrates design effort at the peak of apparent preference, and ignores the edges where rejection lives.

Bobby's approach inverts this. The edges contain more information than the middle. The middle is where every product already tries to compete. The edges are where you learn what makes something actually stop working — and that knowledge is more durable, more defensible, and more actionable than any preference score.

When you know both thresholds:
- You have a design constraint, not just a direction. You cannot drift too far left or too far right without crossing into rejection. That constraint is generative: it tells you exactly what to protect.
- You can recruit the right users. Users who are at the edges — who barely stayed, or who barely left — are more informative than users who are deeply inside the window. They are the ones who can tell you exactly where the boundary is and what pushed them past it.
- You can define "good enough." In the operating window framing, done is not "the best version we could build." Done is "inside the window on both axes." That is a testable, achievable, unambiguous goal.

## The Memrise application

Sandra's job — *help me improve myself through learning a language for fun and personal growth* — has a specific operating window that Memrise has not yet explicitly mapped.

Based on Bobby's guidance, the mapping exercise requires two sets of prototypes for each axis:

**Fun axis:**
- Push towards too easy (zero challenge, no production, pure passive exposure). Find the point where users say: "This doesn't feel like learning anymore."
- Push towards too hard (full production, recall under time pressure, immediate feedback on errors). Find the point where users say: "This is exhausting. I only do it when I have to."

**Progress axis:**
- Reduce progress signals entirely (no session summary, no milestone, no streak, no visible capability improvement). Find the point where users say: "I feel like I'm just drifting."
- Amplify progress signals aggressively (daily progress score, level unlocks, capability assessments, comparative benchmarks). Find the point where users say: "This feels like it's tracking me, not teaching me. It's stressful."

The operating window for Memrise's fun/personal growth job sits somewhere inside all four of those boundaries. Memrise does not currently know where any of those boundaries are — because it has never tested towards them. It has only tested towards the middle.

Bobby's recommendation to Sandra was direct:

> *"I don't think they can tell you what fun is. So the thing is — if we keep asking them 'fun' — it's almost like: what's not fun? Part of this is to build a set of prototypes to know where it's not fun anymore."*

The method: go to users who are currently in the job. Present them with increasingly simple or increasingly demanding experiences. Ask not "do you prefer this?" but "at what point did this stop working for you?" The first crossing of that line is your threshold.

## Three things to hold

1. **You find the operating window by finding both thresholds — not by searching for a peak.** Build one set of prototypes that pushes toward "not enough," and another that pushes toward "too much." The window is everything in between. That window is your design target.

2. **Users can tell you where it stops working far more easily than where it starts working.** "What is fun?" produces vague answers. "When did this stop being fun?" produces specific, actionable answers. The threshold is always a cleaner signal than the optimum.

3. **The Zest result — 150% conversion improvement — came from finding what to avoid, not what to chase.** The discovery was: gamification triggers rejection. Remove it, stay in the window. That is the whole finding. Not "add more practical content" but "stop doing the thing that crosses the left-side threshold." Avoidance is often the bigger lever.
