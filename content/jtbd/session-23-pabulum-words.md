# JTBD — Session 23: Pabulum Words — Every Abstraction Is a Package

> *"Words are just packages of emotions and actions. And how do we actually figure out what are the actions and emotions that basically are compacted into the word that they say is fun."*
> — Bobby Moesta, March 5 2026, Prototyping Workshop, Memrise

> *"Pabulum words can have so many different definitions that we don't know how to cause them."*
> — Bobby Moesta, March 5 2026

---

## The simplest true statement

When a user says "fun," "easy," "relevant," "confident," or "progress," they have not told you anything you can design to. They have handed you a sealed box. Your job is to open it.

That is the whole session. But sitting with it properly will change how you conduct every user research conversation for the rest of your career.

---

## What is a pabulum word?

Bobby introduced the term explicitly in the March 5 workshop. Sandra Zavala asked what "pabulum" meant. Bobby's answer:

> *"Words can have so many different definitions that we don't know how to cause it. So think of it this way: you need to cause fun. How do I cause fun? It's such an impossible thing."*

The word "pabulum" (from the Latin for food, nourishment) describes something soft and bland — content that seems nutritious but has been processed of everything specific. Bobby uses it to describe any word that sounds meaningful but dissolves when you try to act on it.

Pabulum words are not lies. The user genuinely means something when they say "fun." The problem is that what they mean is locked inside the word, and different users are locking completely different things inside the same word.

> *"Fun is such a bad [word] — it's a perfect example of a pabulum word because fun to me and fun to you are completely different and ultimately the same solution for some people will be up here and some people will be down here."*

The word is not the problem. The unexamined word is.

---

## The vocabulary of pabulum

Here are the pabulum words Bobby identified across the three sessions. None of them are rare. All of them come up in every user research conversation:

| Word | What it sounds like | What it actually is |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Fun | A clear product goal | A container with dozens of sub-types — none identical across users |
| Easy | Low cognitive load | Could mean simple, familiar, low-stakes, comfortable, frictionless — or all of these |
| Relevant | Content that matters to me | Depends entirely on the specific context, the goal, and what "matters" means this week |
| Confidence | Feeling able to do something | Usually measured outside the product, in a real-world moment of use |
| Progress | Moving forward | Session-level comprehension? Cumulative milestones? Real-life capability? Identity shift? |
| Convenient | Fits my life | Could mean time, location, effort, commitment, embarrassment — or something else entirely |

Every one of these words is a package. None of them is a design target until it has been unpacked.

---

## Why this happens

Users are not withholding. They are not being lazy. They genuinely do not have better language for what they mean — and they do not know that they need it.

Bobby explains this through what he calls "the limits of their language":

> *"This is a really good example of where you have a variable of local accents and they actually don't have language to talk about that at all. And but yet that's what they mean. And so part of it is to actually understand ultimately you're putting you can understand accents but ultimately that's not attracting them because they don't know they have a meaning behind it."*

He gave this example in response to a Memrise team member discovering that a feature designed for accent training was being used by test participants as a vocabulary tool. The participants were not wrong about the feature — they just did not have the word "accent training" mapped to the internal need the feature was addressing. Their word for it was "learning vocabulary."

The word the user uses is the best word *they* have. Your job is to find what is underneath it.

This also works in reverse. Sometimes users have a word for something they want — and your product *does* that thing — but they do not recognise it because you used different language. That is a marketing implication, not a product one:

> *"They have a different set of words to describe what you're talking about. So that's again those are great learnings to have because at some point we don't need to actually put the copy that says, 'Oh, you can learn different dialects.' It's like no — we'll cover the range of dialects so you can speak it no matter what part of the country you're in."*

Same feature, different language. The feature is not wrong. The label was not landing.

---

## The opening move: name the kind

The most immediate technique Bobby offers is deceptively simple. When a user gives you a pabulum word, ask them to name the *kind* of it.

> *"The challenge I would give every one of you is the moment somebody says, 'Well, I just want it to be fun.' And the next question is: what kind of fun are we talking about? Are we talking about Christmas morning fun? Are we talking about playing on the playground fun? Are we talking about reading a book fun?"*

Notice what this does. It does not challenge the user ("what do you *mean* by fun?"). It does not reduce the word ("can you be more specific?"). It offers them contrast — a set of different containers — and asks which one resonates. This is prototyping *inside the interview*: presenting two or three different interpretations and letting the user's response tell you which direction to go.

Bobby describes this as his "fundamental superpower":

> *"When they don't have language, I'll start to introduce language that are variables. And I'll always say, is it more like this or more like that? And so all of a sudden I'm actually injecting these variables because at some point I don't want to give them the answer. I want them to actually articulate the answer."*

The researcher introduces the variables. The user chooses and then, crucially, *adds language of their own*. That new language — the additions and corrections the user makes to your offered vocabulary — is the signal.

## Sandra's Norwegian learner: a textbook example

Bobby asked the Memrise team to try this during the March 5 session. Sandra Zavala reported what happened when she asked a Norwegian learner what other things felt "fun" in his life:

> *"He gave me a story about playing a ukulele. And he started over the pandemic. And he's found someone to build him a ukulele. And he told this maker, 'I don't care if it takes you 10 years to do it.' Why? Why do you think that's not important? And he just gave me this speech how he's like the dude in The Big Lebowski — life is already too complicated. I just want to do things in my life that feel peaceful."*

"Fun," for this user, meant something close to its opposite in the conventional product sense. He was not looking for gamification, challenge, or streaks. He was looking for a quality of simplicity and peace. Something that could accompany life without adding to its complexity.

Bobby's response was immediate:

> *"This is where I think it's dangerous for us to interpret what fun is. It's up to us to elicit the kind of fun that they like — what causes them to say it's fun."*

Sandra had not assumed she understood "fun." She had asked a sideways question — what else do you do that feels fun? — and the user had walked her inside his own definition. The word "fun" did not change. What changed was her understanding of what it contained, for him, in this context.

---

## The two-question rule for pabulum words

Bobby also offers the complementary question. Once you know what something is, you need to know what makes it stop being that:

> *"What happens that makes something that's fun not fun anymore? Because it's almost like a state and we have to understand the causal mechanisms — it's fun in the beginning but it's not fun in the end. And so it's like: where's the transition point? When does it go away? When do we insert the new thing to make it fun again?"*

This is the operating window logic from Session 18 (contrast and thresholds) applied to language. Every pabulum word has a floor and a ceiling:

- **Below the floor:** not enough of the thing. "Not fun because it's too easy, too predictable."
- **Above the ceiling:** too much of the thing. "Not fun because it's too hard, it feels like work."

The design target is the window between them. But you cannot find the window until you know what the word contains — which means you need both questions:

1. **What kind of ___ ?** (what is inside the word)
2. **When does it stop being ___ ?** (where are the boundaries)

Neither question alone is sufficient. The first finds the content. The second finds the edges.

## Confidence: the pabulum word that lives outside your product

Bobby gave the Memrise team a particularly difficult example: confidence.

Kasia Bania raised it during the March 5 workshop. The team had identified "build confidence in speaking" as a key output of the language learning job. But when they asked users about their confidence, the feedback kept pointing outside the product:

> *"From the interviews that we've done, when I was asking about the confidence and their feeling of progress, it quite often was a measurement of like how they are feeling outside of the learning experience — when they are for instance around their friends or the family and they communicate in that language they are learning. So it's difficult to understand how that confidence can be measured within the prototype."*

Bobby's response, translated: confidence is a pabulum word that registers in the world, not in the app. You cannot measure it inside a learning session because the user is not in the context that triggers the feeling. What you can do is simulate that context — or find the causal inputs that produce it:

> *"You might simulate a conversation and basically actually get them to realise like how confident are you in what was just said — getting them to practice by giving them a certain amount of input and giving them in a certain way that basically says: what was this conversation about, and how confident do you feel in that answer?"*

Then, crucially:

> *"We can't [have the] consumer tell you what confidence is. We have to actually discover how to cause confidence."*

Confidence is an output you can design for. But you cannot design for it by asking users to describe it. You design for it by reverse-engineering the inputs that produce the feeling. The word tells you the target. The investigation finds the path.

---

## The three-step protocol

Across both prototyping sessions, Bobby's handling of pabulum words follows a consistent three-step structure:

**Step 1: Recognise the package.**
When a user says a word that could mean many things — fun, easy, relevant, natural, motivating — mark it internally as unresolved. Do not act on the word. Do not assume you know which container it refers to. The word is a hypothesis, not a finding.

**Step 2: Ask what kind.**
Offer contrast. "Christmas morning fun or playground fun or reading-a-book fun?" Let the user correct your options. The corrections are data. The language they reach for when they reject or amend your suggestions is more valuable than the answer to the original question.

**Step 3: Find the boundary.**
Ask when it stops. "When does it stop being fun for you? When does it become too much?" The boundary is often where the design work lives. Users who love Duolingo streaks and users who find them joyless are not describing different preferences about the same thing. They are living inside different definitions of the same word.

---

## Why this matters more for Memrise than almost anyone

Language learning is made entirely of pabulum words.

- "I want to learn for fun" — which kind?
- "I want to feel closer to a culture" — what does closeness mean, and how do you produce it?
- "I want to make progress" — measured how? Inside the app? In real-life conversation? In reading? In identity?
- "I want it to be easy" — easy in what way? Cognitively undemanding? Socially safe? Temporally flexible?
- "I want to feel confident" — when? How do you know? Measured in what context?

Bobby confirmed this explicitly in the January 27 session when asked whether the JTBD framework applied to Memrise:

> *"I know it applies. It applies to every product I've ever worked on."*

The reason it applies is not just that Memrise has jobs to be done — every product does. The reason it applies with particular force is that the product's domain (language learning) is saturated with pabulum. Every goal a user can articulate is abstract. The progress is invisible. The output is a feeling, not a widget.

A team that does not unpack pabulum will keep optimising for the word. A team that unpacks it will optimise for the specific mechanism that produces the feeling the word points to. The difference between those two teams, over time, is enormous.

## Three things to hold

1. **Every abstraction in user language is a sealed package.** "Fun," "easy," "confident," "relevant," "progress" — these are not answers. They are starting points. The research conversation does not end when you get the pabulum word. It begins.

2. **Ask "what kind?" — then listen for what they add.** When Bobby offers "Christmas morning fun or playground fun or reading-a-book fun?" he is not fishing for one of his options. He is watching what the user does with them. The corrections, the additions, the "it's more like..." — that's the signal. The researcher's job is to inject enough language to unlock the user's own description.

3. **The boundary is often where the design lives.** Users can usually tell you when something stops being fun, stops feeling like progress, stops being easy — even when they cannot define the thing itself. Find the floor and the ceiling of every pabulum word. The operating window between them is your design target.

## Open Question

Bobby gave every member of the Memrise team the same challenge at the end of the March 5 session: the moment someone says a pabulum word, do not move on. Stop. Ask what kind.

> **Here is the challenge for you, Kevin: take the three pabulum words you hear most often from Memrise users — "fun," "progress," and "relevant" are probably top candidates — and write down your honest best guess at what each one contains.**
>
> Not what you think users mean in general. What does *this job's* user — the one learning for personal growth and cultural connection, not to pass an exam — mean when they say each word?
>
> Now compare that to what Sandra's seven interviews actually revealed: "fun" contained peace, simplicity, no obligation, identity as a curious person. "Progress" did not mean fluency. It meant occasional moments of recognition — a sign in a shop, a lyric in a song.
>
> How far was your best guess from what the research found?
>
> *That gap — between your confident interpretation of the pabulum word and what the user actually meant — is the gap Bobby is trying to close. It is not small. And it is not closed by being smarter. It is closed by asking "what kind?" and then genuinely listening to what they say next.*
