# JTBD — Session 7: The Six Systems of Progress

> *"As an engineer, I think about this as a set of six systems. There's a system one, which is the first thought, which is really about making space. To me, this is a marketing system."*
> — Bobby Moesta (Jan 27, 2026, Memrise session)

---

## The simplest true statement

A step tells you what happens next. A system tells you how it works.

Bobby calls each of the six phases a *system* not because he likes jargon, but because he's an engineer — and to an engineer, every system has the same anatomy: inputs, outputs, actions (what the person *does*), and outcomes (what the person *says*). Understanding the six phases as systems, rather than as steps in a funnel, is what makes them operationally useful. It tells you not just that someone is "in Phase 3," but exactly what they need from you in that moment, what they're doing, what they're saying, and therefore what you should be building.

## The engineer's frame: everything is a system

Bobby's intellectual lineage runs through Dr. Deming, Dr. Taguchi, and Clayton Christensen. All three share a belief: to understand something, you have to understand its *causal structure*. Not "what happened" but "why it happened." Not "what the customer said" but "what is actually driving their behaviour."

For Bobby, this translates into a simple but powerful discipline. Before he designs anything, he asks: what are the inputs, what are the outputs, what actions does the person take, and what do they say? This is the same diagnostic frame he applies whether he's optimising a manufacturing process, designing a sales demo, or figuring out why users churn.

Applied to the JTBD timeline, it looks like this:

```
          INPUTS
             ↓
      [Phase N] ──→ OUTPUTS
         ↑               ↓
    ACTIONS (DO)   OUTCOMES (SAY)
```

Every phase has this architecture. The phase itself is the system — it receives inputs from the previous phase and from the world, it processes them through the customer's cognition and context, and it produces outputs that either move the customer to the next phase or stall them.

And crucially: the *actions* (what the customer does) and the *outcomes* (what the customer says) are your observational signals. They're how you know which system you're in, and whether it's working.

---

## The six systems mapped

Here is how Bobby's framework plays out across all six phases, with the system purpose (from his Growth Summit slides) driving the design imperative for each:

| Phase | System | Purpose | Actions (DO) | Outcomes (SAY) |
|-------|--------|---------|--------------|----------------|
| 1 — First Thought | S1 | **Making Space** | Pausing on something relevant; mentioning it in conversation | "I've been thinking about..." / "We should probably..." |
| 2 — Passive Looking | S2 | **Learning How** | Noticing relevant content; reading without searching; asking friends | "I keep seeing this" / "Have you heard about...?" |
| 3 — Active Looking | S3 | **Seeing Possibilities** | Googling; comparing; test-driving; requesting demos | "What are my options?" / "I need to figure out which one..." |
| 4 — Deciding | S4 | **Making Trade-offs** | Re-reading; negotiating; asking specific questions about fit | "What do I get if I..." / "Is it worth the extra..." |
| 5 — First Use | S5 | **Making Progress** | Onboarding; first session; first meaningful output | "I can already see..." / "This is different because..." |
| 6 — Ongoing Use | S6 | **Building Habits** | Returning unprompted; integrating into routine | "I always..." / "I can't imagine not..." |

The *purpose* column is not decorative. Bobby is specific: the purpose of each system defines what the customer needs from you at that moment. Not features. Not content. Not push notifications. What they *need* in service of the work they're doing at that phase.

Phase 1 customers are doing the work of *making space* — they need a question, not an answer. Phase 4 customers are doing the work of *making trade-offs* — they need information that clarifies the choice, not more options. Give Phase 1 content to Phase 4 customers and you confuse them. Give Phase 4 content to Phase 1 customers and you overwhelm them before the question has even formed.

## AutoBooks: what happens when you treat all phases as one system

This is the AutoBooks case study from Bobby's Growth Summit presentation, and it's the cleanest business demonstration of why systems thinking matters.

**The company:** AutoBooks is a Detroit fintech that sells financial tools to small businesses through banks. Their product helps small business owners track invoices, get paid online, and manage cash flow — tools that banks bundle into their business accounts. AutoBooks' customer is the *bank*, who then offers the tool to *small business customers*.

**The problem:** Their sales team had a six-step process: advertising, landing page, email campaign, booking reminders, a sales demo call, and then customer success. The demo was the pivot point. And every 90 days, they had to rebuild it because it wasn't working.

Bobby's diagnosis: *"Where are they in their timeline?"*

This is the systems question. AutoBooks was treating every bank prospect as if they were in the same phase — implicitly, Phase 4 (deciding). But they weren't. Some bank prospects were in Phase 2, still learning whether this was even a problem worth solving. Others were in Phase 3, actively trying to understand the landscape of digital banking tools. Only some were in Phase 4, where they were ready to evaluate AutoBooks specifically against alternatives.

A single demo can't serve all three systems. Each system has different inputs, different outputs, different actions, and different outcomes. A demo designed for a Phase 4 decider — with specific trade-off comparisons, pricing structures, and implementation timelines — is bewildering to a Phase 2 passive looker who hasn't yet even committed to the view that this is a priority. And a Phase 2 demo — broad, educational, "here's what the problem looks like" — is frustratingly shallow for a Phase 4 buyer who already knows all that and just needs to understand why AutoBooks over the alternative.

**The solution:** Three demos, each designed as its own system.

**Demo 1 (Phase 2 — Learning):** The job of this demo is to help the bank prospect learn whether this is a problem worth having. It opens with a qualifying question: *"Which of these applies most to you right now?"* Three options:

1. Our executive team has placed an emphasis on *growing deposits* and/or increasing non-interest fee income.
2. Our latest digital banking upgrade is behind us. We are now looking for our *next digital banking project* to continue to differentiate.
3. When our customers ask us how they can get paid online, *we don't have a good answer*. We have solutions for card-present transactions, but when it comes to competing against PayPal, Square, QuickBooks and others — we're at a disadvantage.

Notice: these aren't product features. They're *struggling moments*. The bank prospect is being asked to locate themselves in their own struggle. The demo then speaks to whichever one they select. The design goal of Demo 1: the prospect should *almost beg for Demo 2*.

**Demo 2 (Phase 3 — Possibilities):** The job is to open up the landscape of what's possible. Show the range. Help the prospect see that there are real solutions to the problem they've now named. The design goal: make them feel informed enough about the possibilities that they're ready to narrow.

**Demo 3 (Phase 4 — Trade-offs):** The job is to make the decision clear. What do they get with AutoBooks that they don't get elsewhere? What are they giving up? What are they trading? This is where pricing, implementation, support structures, and competitive comparison belong — not before.

**The results over 12 months:**
- 3x lead conversion
- Half the time to close (from lead to customer)
- 6x growth in bank partners
- 9x growth in small business users
- 5x revenue

And a bonus result Bobby highlights: *"Program performance + launch is more successful, faster, and LESS work on the back end."* This is the systems insight in action — when each phase is properly designed for, the customer needs less hand-holding at each step because you met them where they actually were.

## The critical design principle: "Almost beg for the next demo"

Bobby uses this phrase specifically in the AutoBooks case, and it's worth holding onto.

The goal of each system is not to complete the sale. It's to produce the outputs that move the customer naturally into the *next* system. Phase 2 produces a customer who *wants* to understand possibilities. Phase 3 produces a customer who *wants* to make a trade-off. The work of each demo is done when the customer is pulling toward the next one.

This inverts how most companies think about sales and onboarding. The usual instinct is to load each customer interaction with as much information and persuasion as possible — to try to close, or at least to advance, from every touch. Bobby's systems view says the opposite: know which phase you're in, deliver exactly what that phase needs, produce the outputs of that phase cleanly, and let the customer's own motion carry them forward.

The same principle applies far beyond sales. An onboarding flow that tries to do the work of Phase 4 (making the trade-off case for the product) while the user is actually in Phase 5 (trying to find the first signs of progress) is working against the user's current system. The user has already decided — that's how they got here. What they need now is evidence that the decision was right. Give them a trade-off argument and you create doubt where you should be building confidence.

## Inputs and outputs at each phase: what this actually looks like

Bobby doesn't spell out every input and output explicitly in the Memrise sessions — he describes the phases, the purposes, and the design imperatives. But his engineering logic implies a specific structure for each. Here's what it looks like:

**S1 (Making Space):**
- *Inputs:* An accumulating push (frustration with current situation) + a question that creates the space
- *Outputs:* A question formed in the mind — "Is this something I need to deal with?"
- *DO:* Pause. Mention to partner or colleague. Bookmark something. Do nothing yet.
- *SAY:* "I've been thinking about..." / "We should probably look at this at some point."

**S2 (Learning How):**
- *Inputs:* The question from S1 + ambient information (articles, conversations, ads, observations)
- *Outputs:* A developing sense that this is a real, solvable problem + early vocabulary about the solution space
- *DO:* Notice relevant content; read casually; ask around; absorb without actively searching
- *SAY:* "I keep seeing this" / "Someone mentioned X" / "I'm starting to think there might be options"

**S3 (Seeing Possibilities):**
- *Inputs:* Conviction from S2 that this is worth solving + an event that creates urgency
- *Outputs:* A shortlist — a bounded set of candidates with rough understanding of what each offers
- *DO:* Google. Compare. Request demos. Test-drive. Watch YouTube reviews.
- *SAY:* "I'm looking at a few different options" / "I need to figure out which direction to go"

**S4 (Making Trade-offs):**
- *Inputs:* Shortlist from S3 + hire-and-fire criteria (what they care about, what would disqualify)
- *Outputs:* A decision — one option selected, others eliminated
- *DO:* Re-read specific details; ask pointed questions; negotiate; seek specific comparisons
- *SAY:* "What do I get if I..." / "Is it worth the extra money for..." / "What would I be giving up?"

**S5 (Making Progress):**
- *Inputs:* The decision + first interaction with the product
- *Outputs:* Evidence of early progress — specific, observable signs that the trajectory is right
- *DO:* Onboard; use the product for the first time; attempt the core action
- *SAY:* "I can already see..." / "Wait, I just did X" / "This is different from what I was using because..."

**S6 (Building Habits):**
- *Inputs:* Early signs of progress from S5 + repeated use
- *Outputs:* An established routine — the product becomes the new normal
- *DO:* Return unprompted; integrate into daily patterns; stop comparing alternatives
- *SAY:* "I always..." / "It's just part of how I..." / "I can't imagine going back to..."

## The wall between supply and demand

Bobby's Growth Summit slides visualise the gap between companies and customers as "The Wall" — the product-market disconnect. On the left: the supply side, with its business systems, features, strategy, and sales funnels. On the right: the demand side, with its struggling moments, desired outcomes, hire-and-fire criteria, and trade-offs.

The conventional sales funnel — awareness, interest, consideration, intent, evaluation, purchase — sits on the *supply side* of the wall. It describes how leads move through *your* system. It tells you nothing about where the customer is in *their* system.

The six systems are the demand-side equivalent of the sales funnel. They describe the customer's actual journey. And the mapping is not one-to-one. A customer can be in Phase 3 (active looking) without being "in consideration" in your funnel — because they haven't found you yet. A customer can be "in evaluation" in your funnel while they're actually still in Phase 2 — because the sales team booked them into a demo before they were ready.

The systems view collapses this gap. The question isn't "where are they in our funnel?" It's "which system are they in, what do they need from that system, and are we delivering it?"

## What this means for Memrise

Bobby framed this as directly relevant to Memrise in the January session. The six-systems view creates three specific questions that the product team should be asking:

**1. Which system are most acquired users actually in?**

Memrise's acquisition likely captures users across three different phases. Someone who typed "best Spanish learning app" into Google and clicked a comparison article is probably in Phase 3 (active looking) or Phase 4 (deciding). Someone who saw a Memrise Instagram ad while scrolling after watching a Spanish show is probably in Phase 2 (passive looking). Someone who signed up because a friend sent them a link might be in Phase 1 (first thought — the friend created the space).

These three people need completely different first experiences. The Phase 4 user needs the trade-off case — why Memrise over the three other apps they have open in tabs. The Phase 2 user doesn't know what they want yet; they need help understanding that their loosely-formed desire is actually solvable. The Phase 1 user just had their first question — overwhelming them with features destroys the fragile opening.

**2. What is the designed output of the onboarding system (S5)?**

Bobby's answer to Sonia's question about long-term progress applies directly here. The onboarding system (Phase 5) must produce *specific, observable early signs of progress*. Not "the user completed the first lesson." Not "the user earned X XP." Specific things the user can *do or feel* after the first session that they couldn't before.

Bobby's example from the session: *"My colleagues are now talking to me in a casual way in their native language as opposed to switching to English."* That's a Phase 6 output. What's the Phase 5 output — the specific feeling or capability that, in the first session, makes the user feel like that Phase 6 outcome is *plausible*?

The S5 design question for Memrise is: what should the user be able to say, do, or notice after Session 1 that proves to them the trajectory is right? And is the current onboarding designed backward from that output, or forward from the content library?

**3. Is the product trying to serve all six systems with the same interface?**

The Intercom lesson from Session 6 — four jobs, one product, eventually unbundled — has a systems parallel. A product that tries to serve Phase 2 users (learning how) and Phase 5 users (making first progress) with the same UX will serve both poorly. Phase 2 users need breadth — here's what's possible, here's what others do. Phase 5 users need depth and progression — here's the specific thing you're working on, here's evidence it's working.

This isn't necessarily a product-unbundling problem. It might be a sequencing and personalisation problem. But it requires the question: which phase is this screen, this notification, this email, this onboarding step *designed for*?

## Three things to hold onto

1. **Each phase is a system, not a step.** It has inputs, outputs, actions (what the customer does), and outcomes (what the customer says). The system architecture is the same for all six; the *content* of each is radically different.

2. **The output of each system is the input of the next.** Design the output you need, work backward to understand what the system needs to produce it, and build accordingly. AutoBooks built three demos because each phase needed to produce a specific output that pulled the customer into the next phase.

3. **A sales funnel and a customer system are not the same thing.** One describes how leads move through the company. The other describes how customers make progress. The gap between them is "The Wall" — and product-market fit is what happens when you close it.
