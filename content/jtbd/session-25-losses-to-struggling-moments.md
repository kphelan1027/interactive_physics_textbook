# JTBD — Session 25: You Lose to Another Struggling Moment, Not to a Competitor

> *"Most of the time we think that we lose to somebody, and nine times out of ten we lose to another struggling moment — not to a competitor."*
> — Bobby Moesta, January 27 2026, JTBD Session at Memrise

---

## The simplest true statement

When a user stops using your product, your first instinct is to ask: which competitor did they go to? Bobby says that instinct is almost always wrong. Ninety percent of the time they didn't go to a competitor at all. They went to another problem.

That reframe changes everything — what you measure, what you build, and how you think about winning them back.

---

## Where this came from

The context matters. This came during the Q&A at the end of Bobby's first session at Memrise, January 27 2026. Bobby had just explained the non-linear nature of the JTBD timeline — the idea that a person does not move cleanly from "first thought" to "satisfied customer" in a straight line. They move forward, stall, loop back, stall again.

Then someone raised the pandemic example:

> *"It's not linear. If you think about it — people might have been in the midst of deciding and then the pandemic happened and all of a sudden other things trumped it, and so they went back to passive looking. They still have the struggle. They still have it. But they've got to do other things first."*

And then — almost as a throwaway line, almost as if it were obvious once you see the framework clearly — he said it:

> *"Most of the time we think that we lose to somebody, and nine times out of ten we lose to another struggling moment — not to a competitor."*

He moved on. But if you were paying attention, that sentence contains a complete rethinking of how to do competitive strategy.

---

## The mechanism: why the non-linear timeline produces this result

The JTBD timeline (covered in Session 6) has six phases: first thought, passive looking, active looking, deciding, consuming, ongoing use. The key thing Bobby added in this session is that movement along the timeline is not one-directional.

When you are in "active looking" — you have identified a struggling moment, you are researching solutions, you might be trialling Memrise — something can happen that pushes you back to "passive looking." Not to a different product. Just backwards on the same journey.

The pandemic was Bobby's example. But it could be anything:
- A new baby. Suddenly the language goal is not the most urgent thing.
- A promotion that changes your entire work context.
- A holiday that disrupts the routine.
- A relationship that collapses.
- A financial crisis that makes the goal feel distant.

All of these are struggling moments. They just happen to be *different* struggling moments — ones that are more urgent, more immediate, more emotionally charged than the one that brought the user to your product in the first place.

When that new struggling moment wins, the user does not fire you for a competitor. They fire you because life got louder in a different direction. The job they hired you for is still there. The struggle has not gone away. It has just been outbid by another struggle.

---

## "Bitching ain't switching" — the threshold for action

Bobby introduced this phrase earlier in the same session, and it is the flip side of the same coin:

> *"If I took away the light bulb and I just have a struggling moment and no idea what progress looks like — this is where people complain but they don't actually do anything about it. The phrase I use is: bitching ain't switching."*

People do not change products, habits, or routines just because they are frustrated. The struggling moment has to reach a threshold — a level of urgency, combined with a visible path to progress — before they act.

This means that when a user churns, there are actually two possibilities:

1. **The struggling moment receded.** It got resolved some other way, or it stopped feeling urgent.
2. **Another struggling moment won.** Life delivered a more pressing problem that took the time and energy that had been allocated to language learning.

In neither case is the explanation "they went to Duolingo." In neither case is the remedy "build more features to compete with Duolingo."

The actual question is: *what happened in their life?*

---

## The Harry Snow problem: "I've always wanted to do it"

This connects directly to something Harry Snow raised in the same Q&A. Harry was asking about the "locksmith moment" — the specific point in time when a user realises they have a problem and begins looking for a solution. He was struggling to find it in language learning interviews, because people kept saying things like:

> *"Oh, it's something I've always wanted to do."*

Bobby's response is the key interview technique for this session:

> *"If you've been wanting to do it for a long time — why now? What happened that created the space?"*

The question "why now?" is the demand-side version of loss analysis. When you understand why someone started, you understand the specific struggling moment that crossed the threshold. That struggling moment has a shape — a context, an urgency, an emotional charge. 

The loss analysis runs the same question in reverse: *why did the space close?* What happened that made this struggling moment less urgent than before? What other struggling moment took over?

Bobby's condo funeral example shows how this works in practice. The team kept hearing that buyers had thought about moving "for a while." The "why now?" question revealed it was always a death — the death of a friend prompted a first thought about one's own mortality, which opened the space for a decision about housing that had been deferred for years. Without the question "why now?", that insight never surfaces.

---

## The churn interview, reframed

Most churn analysis asks: *why did you leave?* And then it presents a list of reasons — price, missing feature, found something better.

Bobby's framework makes that question almost meaningless. The actual question is: *what happened in your life that made this job less urgent?* 

And the follow-up: *is the struggling moment still there, just deferred?* Because if it is, then you have not lost that user. You have lost them temporarily, and they will come back when their life shifts again — unless you have made re-engagement so difficult that the Casper comes from the wrong side.

Bobby was explicit about the re-entry point. In the same session, he talked about how the JTBD marketing system at the "first thought" phase works by asking questions, not pushing features:

> *"Questions create spaces in the brain for solutions to fall into."*

The re-engagement version of this is: what question will be in the user's head when the struggling moment returns? Are you present when that question forms? Is your brand associated with the right question?

For Memrise, the question might be: *"I should really get back to learning Italian."* That question will form when something happens in the user's life — a planned trip to Italy, a conversation with a colleague who speaks the language, a cultural event that makes them feel the gap again. At that moment, which brand surfaces? The one that was present when the question formed, or the one that sent them six months of "we miss you" emails?

---

## Why competitive analysis becomes secondary

This is the uncomfortable implication. Competitive analysis — who are our competitors, what are they building, how do we differentiate — is almost entirely supply-side thinking. It describes the landscape of available solutions. What it does not describe is the landscape of struggling moments that bring people into the market, and the landscape of competing struggling moments that take them out.

Bobby does not say competitive analysis is useless. He says it is answering the wrong question most of the time.

The right question is not: *what are the features that make Memrise better than Duolingo?*

The right question is: *what are the struggling moments that bring people to language learning, and what are the competing struggling moments that pull them away?*

If you know the answer to the second question, you know:
- Which life events predict churn (and can be used to pre-empt it)
- Which life events predict re-engagement (and can be used to time re-activation)
- What "non-consumers" look like — people who have the struggling moment but have never started, because some other struggle always wins first

Non-consumption (Session 9) is the demand that already exists but has never converted. A significant portion of non-consumers are not absent because language learning doesn't appeal to them. They are absent because another struggling moment has been reliably winning. If Memrise can make itself easier to hire in the small windows between other competing struggles, that is a bigger growth lever than any competitive feature.

---

## The Memrise application

In the March 5 workshop, Bobby made the Duolingo observation explicit:

> *"When you really want to learn language, come to Memrise. If you just want to do a morning routine, Duolingo works great for you. Call it out."*

Read this alongside the loss analysis. If Duolingo's job is "provide a morning routine with a commitment mechanic," then the struggling moment that hired Duolingo is not "I want to speak Italian." It is "I want to feel like I have a disciplined morning habit."

When that Duolingo user churns, they are not going to Memrise. They are going to another morning routine — running, meditation, a different app. Memrise is not in the same competitive set for that user at all.

The user who comes to Memrise has a different struggling moment: something specific happened that made them actually want to speak the language. An in-law who does not speak English. A promotion that requires Spanish. A trip to Japan. A relationship.

When *that* user churns from Memrise, the question is not "did they go to Duolingo?" Almost certainly they did not. The question is: "what happened in their life that made speaking Japanese less urgent than it was three months ago?" That is the conversation Memrise needs to be able to have. Either through direct interview research, or through behavioural data that proxies for life events.

The re-engagement strategy writes itself from there: not "we miss you," but something that asks the question that will be forming when the struggling moment returns. Something that makes the space in the brain.

## Three things to hold

1. **Nine times out of ten, you lose to another struggling moment — not a competitor.** The user did not fire you because your product was worse than the alternative. They fired you because a different struggle became more urgent and ate the time and energy they had allocated to your job. The struggling moment you were hired for is probably still there, deferred.

2. **"Why now?" is the most important question in a JTBD interview** — and the same question runs in reverse for churn. You are not looking for feature complaints. You are looking for the life event that closed the space. Find that event, and you understand the shape of your retention problem.

3. **Competitive strategy is mostly supply-side thinking.** It describes available alternatives. What it misses is the full competitive landscape — which includes every other struggling moment in a user's life that bids for the same time, attention, and emotional energy that your product requires. Those are your real competitors.

## Open Question

Bobby's observation came at the end of a long session, almost as an aside. But it has a specific implication for how Memrise runs churn interviews — if it runs them at all.

The standard churn survey asks: *Why did you cancel?* And offers options: too expensive, not enough time, found something else, didn't find it useful.

Bobby would say that survey is almost entirely useless. "Not enough time" is not an explanation — it is a symptom. The explanation is: *not enough time because of what?* A new job? A new child? A health issue? A move? Each of those is a different struggling moment, with a different profile, and a different re-entry point.

> **Here is the question for you, Kevin: if you could do one churn interview this week with a lapsed Memrise user — someone who started, engaged for a while, and then stopped — what is the single question you would most want answered?**
>
> Not a survey question. A conversation question — the kind Bobby would ask.
>
> *Try to make it a "why now?" question: not "why did you leave?" but "what was happening in your life when you stopped?"*
