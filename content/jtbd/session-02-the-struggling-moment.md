# JTBD — Session 2: The Struggling Moment

> *"If there's only one thing you take away from this presentation, it should be: the struggling moment is the seed for all innovation."*
> — Bobby Moesta (Jan 27, 2026, Memrise session)

---

## The simplest true statement

People don't change until they hit a wall. The struggling moment is that wall. It's not a problem statement invented in a workshop. It's a real crack in someone's life — the moment when what they've been doing stops working, and they feel it.

That moment is where demand is born. Everything else — the product, the feature, the ad — is downstream of it.

---

## What Bobby actually said

Bobby opened the Jan 27 session by talking about his 40 years of studying the same question: *"Why do people change? Why do they pick new products? Why do they fire old products?"*

His answer, refined across 3,500 products:

> *"It's not the product that creates demand. It's a struggling moment that creates demand."*

And then, almost immediately, he said the sentence he came back to three more times in the session:

> *"The struggling moment is the seed for all innovation."*

This is not metaphor. It's a structural claim: if you don't understand the struggling moment, you don't understand *why* demand for your product exists. You're just guessing.

---

## The river diagram

Bobby uses a visual he calls "the river" to explain what struggling moments do. Here's how it works:

```
[Old way]  ←  struggle builds  →  [WALL]  →  River to cross  →  [New progress]
```

On the left: the current product or situation, slowly becoming inadequate.

In the middle: the struggling moment — the crack point where the old way can no longer handle what the person needs.

On the right: an idea of what progress could look like. A vision. The pull.

The key insight: **you need both sides of the river for demand to exist.**

Bobby put it this way:

> *"If I took away the light bulb — if there's just a push and they don't have any idea what progress looks like — this is where people complain but they don't actually do anything about it. I call it: bitching ain't switching."*

Struggling without a vision of progress produces complaints, not action. People grumble about their mattress for years. They grumble about their phone. They grumble about not speaking another language. But they don't hire anything. The struggling moment only becomes demand when the person can also *see* a way across.

---

## Three types of struggling moment

Not all struggling moments are the same. Bobby's caseload reveals three recurring patterns:

| Type | What it looks like | Example from Bobby |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Accumulating friction** | The old way keeps failing in small ways until a tipping point | Kids leave home → yard is too big → can't travel → one day it's all too much |
| **Sudden event** | A single external event that crystallises the struggle | Friends attending a funeral, realise they should sort their housing before the other dies alone |
| **Invisible ceiling** | Progress has been blocked so long the person stopped noticing | 500 SNHU students watching lectures on video but never showing up — wanting education but unable to access it |

The third type is what Bobby calls **non-consumption** — and it's where the biggest growth opportunities hide. (We'll go deep on this in Session 9.)

## The struggling roadmap

Most companies build technology roadmaps: here's what we plan to build, in what order, by what date.

Bobby builds **struggling roadmaps**.

The iPhone example from the January session makes this concrete:

> *"Think of Apple when the iPhone came out. It was replacing a PDA, a phone, and a music player. And the phone out of all of it worked the worst. Round one: the struggling moment was reception. They worked on antennas, networking, the chip. Then battery life. Then the camera. The roadmap wasn't picked because someone decided they needed to work on battery then camera. It was: at the end of every launch, what's the next set of struggling moments?"*

The iPhone team didn't start with a five-year feature list. They started with what was broken *right now*, fixed it, and then listened for what was broken *next*. Each resolved struggle revealed the next one.

The struggling moment is not just the genesis of a product — it's the navigation system for its evolution.

---

## Why you can't just ask customers what they want

There's a catch. People don't naturally narrate their struggling moments. They describe their circumstances in the abstract. They say things like "I've always wanted to learn Italian" — without surfacing the specific crack that finally made them act.

Harry Snow raised this exact problem in the January session about language learning:

> *Harry: "I've been doing JTBD interviews and I find it really hard to identify that locksmith moment — the exact moment where they realised they had the problem. With language learning, people say 'it's something I've always wanted to do.'"*

Bobby's answer:

> *"Most people don't see it as a culminating moment. So you have to ask: why now? Like what happened that created the space for it? They'll go 'I don't know.' And you're like, well, tell me what was happening. They'd say 'well, what made you look at that? I don't know.' And I'd say, 'When did you stop at the first thing?' 'Oh, we were in funeral clothes.' 'Why were you in funeral clothes?' 'Well, because we just came from a funeral.' 'What happened there?' And next thing you know, they're like, 'Oh, I guess that had a lot to do with it.'"*

The struggling moment is real. It's always there. It's just buried under the rationalisation people apply retrospectively to their own decisions.

This is why Bobby calls his interview method closer to **criminal investigation** than market research:

> *"Read Never Split the Difference by Chris Voss. The method is built on criminal and intelligence interrogation — because consumers lie to you. Not with malice. They just omit things because they don't think it's relevant. You have to pull it out of them."*

---

## Casper: the struggling moment was the buying process

Bobby's Casper case study is a clean illustration of how you can misidentify the struggling moment if you stay at the surface.

If you asked: *"What's the struggling moment in buying a mattress?"* — most supply-side thinkers would say: *"People don't know which mattress is right for them."*

The real answer was subtler:

> *"The struggling moment was actually how to buy a mattress. It wasn't necessarily just the mattress... Most people are so afraid of it because they really don't know how to buy one. They only buy it once every 5–15 years."*

Casper didn't build a better mattress. They eliminated the friction of the purchase: three tiers, four sizes, foam that ships to your door, 90-day returns. The mattress was fine. The struggling moment they solved was the anxiety of the *decision*, not the sleep.

This is the pattern: **the struggling moment is often one layer deeper than you think.**

---

## Why this matters for Memrise

Bobby confirmed in January that the framework applies directly to Memrise. The question that matters isn't "how do we make a better language learning app?" It's: **what specific struggling moment is causing people to hire Memrise right now?**

And the second, more challenging question: **are there people who want to make progress in a language but can't — because of access, time, money, or inconvenience — who aren't hiring anyone yet?** That's non-consumption, and it's where Bobby says real growth comes from.

Sonia raised something in the January session that's directly relevant: users reporting "time and motivation" as barriers. Bobby's response was striking — he said those might need to be made *bigger*, not just addressed:

> *"Most people don't want to bring up the negative. But the negative part is actually essential to giving them motivation to move forward. Sometimes we have to make the struggle bigger — help them realise that if you don't learn this, then you have a bigger choice to make."*

The struggling moment isn't just a diagnostic. It's also, sometimes, something you help the user *see more clearly*.

## Three things to hold onto

1. **The struggling moment is the origin of all demand.** Not a marketing insight. A structural fact. No one randomly decides to hire your product. Something broke first.

2. **Struggling alone doesn't create action — you need both sides of the river.** The push of the situation *and* the pull of a vision of progress. Without the pull, you just get complaints.

3. **People don't narrate their struggling moments clearly.** You have to investigate like a detective, not survey like a pollster. Ask "why now?" Ask it again. The real moment is usually one layer deeper.
