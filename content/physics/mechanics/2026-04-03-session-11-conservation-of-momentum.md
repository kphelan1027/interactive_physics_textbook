# Session 11: Conservation of Momentum — When Two Objects Meet

## The Setup — Something You've Seen

Picture a snooker table. The cue ball rolls across the felt, hits the red ball dead centre, and stops — completely dead. The red ball rolls forward at exactly the speed the cue ball had. Every bit of motion transferred. Not some of it. All of it.

Or think about a crash test. Two 1,500 kg cars hit each other head-on at 30 mph. They crumple together and stop dead. Not bounce. Not spin sideways. Just stop.

Both of these are the same physics at work: **conservation of momentum**. One of the most powerful laws in all of physics, and once you see it, you can't unsee it.

---

## Idea 1: Conservation of Momentum

### The concept first

Remember Session 10: momentum is "hardness to stop" — **p = mv**. And impulse is what *changes* momentum: FΔt = mΔv.

Here's the pivot: **go back to Session 3** — Newton's Third Law. Every force has an equal and opposite reaction force. When two objects collide, they push on each other with equal and opposite forces, for exactly the same duration of time.

So the impulse Object A delivers to B is exactly equal and opposite to the impulse B delivers to A.

Which means: whatever momentum A gains, B loses by exactly the same amount.

**The total never changes.**

> **Law of Conservation of Momentum:**
> In a closed system (no external forces), total momentum before = total momentum after.

### The financial analogy

Think of momentum like money in a closed economy. You hand Kevin £100. You're down £100. Kevin's up £100. The total in the room is unchanged — it just moved.

Conservation of momentum is the same principle. The total "quantity of motion" in a closed system is fixed. It moves between objects. It doesn't appear or disappear.

### The maths

$$m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2$$

Where:
- $m$ = mass, $u$ = velocity before, $v$ = velocity after

**Snooker example:**
- Cue ball: 0.17 kg at 3 m/s → p = 0.51 kg·m/s
- Red ball: 0.17 kg at 0 m/s → p = 0

Before: total p = **0.51 kg·m/s**

After dead-centre hit:
- Cue ball: stops → p = 0
- Red ball: moves at 3 m/s → p = 0.51 kg·m/s

After: total p = **0.51 kg·m/s** ✅

## Idea 2: Elastic vs Inelastic Collisions

### Elastic collisions
Objects bounce off each other. Momentum *and* kinetic energy are both conserved. The snooker ball is close to elastic.

### Inelastic collisions
Objects lose some kinetic energy to heat, sound, and deformation. **Perfectly inelastic** = they stick together and move as one lump.

**Key trap:** Kinetic energy is lost in inelastic collisions. Momentum is still conserved. These are different quantities.

### Perfectly inelastic maths

$$m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v$$

**Car crash example:**
- Car 1: 1,500 kg at 20 m/s | Car 2: 1,000 kg at 0 m/s
- Before: p = 30,000 kg·m/s
- After: (2,500) × v = 30,000 → **v = 12 m/s**

Momentum conserved ✅. Kinetic energy partially lost to heat and deformation.

## Idea 3: Explosions — Momentum Running in Reverse

**Before a firework shell explodes: total momentum = 0.**

After it explodes, all fragments together still sum to zero. Every fragment going one way has a counterpart going the other way.

This is why guns recoil. Rockets propel. Before: total p = 0. After: bullet goes forward (+p), gun goes back (−p). Total = 0.

### Explosion maths (from rest)

$$0 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2$$

The heavier object moves slower; the lighter one moves faster — same magnitude × mass = same momentum magnitude.

---

## The Three Equations

| Situation | Equation |
|-----------|----------|
| General collision | $m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2$ |
| Perfectly inelastic | $m_1 u_1 + m_2 u_2 = (m_1 + m_2)v$ |
| Explosion from rest | $0 = m_1 v_1 + m_2 v_2$ |

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Total momentum in a closed system never changes** — Newton's 3rd Law guarantees it.
2. **Elastic** = bounce + energy conserved. **Inelastic** = energy lost (momentum still conserved).
3. **Explosions are collisions in reverse** — starting from zero, they must always sum to zero.
