# Newton's Third Law — Action, Reaction, and Why Rockets Work

**Session 3 | Track 4 — Physics Fundamentals | 26 March 2026**

---

## The Moment That Starts This Session

You're sitting at your desk. You plant your hands on the edge and push back — your chair rolls away from the desk. You applied a force to the desk. But something pushed *you*. What was it?

It wasn't magic. It wasn't momentum. It was the desk.

When you pushed the desk, the desk pushed you — with exactly the same force, in exactly the opposite direction.

That's Newton's Third Law. And once you see it, you can't unsee it.

---

## Concept 1: Forces Always Come in Pairs

In Session 1 we established what a force is: a push or a pull that changes or tries to change the motion of something. In Session 2 we saw that the bigger the force, the bigger the acceleration — and that mass is what resists it.

Here's what neither of those sessions told you: **forces never show up alone.**

Every single force in the universe is one half of a matched pair. When object A exerts a force on object B, object B exerts an equal and opposite force on object A. Always. No exceptions.

Newton called these *action* and *reaction* forces. They happen simultaneously, like two sides of the same handshake.

Think of it like a contract: **no force is ever issued unilaterally.** Every force is a bilateral agreement between two objects. One pushes right, the other pushes left. Same magnitude, opposite direction.

> **For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.**

In vector notation:

**F₁₂ = −F₂₁**

The minus sign means opposite direction. The magnitudes are identical.

---

## Concept 2: The Pairs Always Act on *Different* Objects

This is where most people's mental model breaks.

> *"If the desk pushes me back with the same force I push it with — why don't those forces cancel out and nothing moves?"*

Because **they act on different objects**.

- You push the desk → that force acts *on the desk*.
- The desk pushes you → that force acts *on you*.

These forces can't cancel because cancellation requires two forces on the *same* object. To be in equilibrium (Session 1 — stationary objects), forces on the *same* object must balance. Action-reaction pairs are always on *separate* objects.

**A common confusion to flag:** Your weight (gravity pulling you down) and the ground pushing you up are often mistakenly called a Third Law pair. They're not — both act on you. The true Third Law pair to Earth's gravitational pull on you is *your* gravitational pull on Earth, acting upward on the Earth itself.

---

## Concept 3: Why Rockets Work

The rocket is the most powerful demonstration of Newton's Third Law because it shatters a common misconception.

Most people think rockets push against the air to move. But rockets work in space. There is no air.

What actually happens:

The engine burns fuel and expels exhaust gas downward (or backward) at enormous speed. That exhaust is pushed by the rocket. By Newton's Third Law, the exhaust pushes the rocket in the opposite direction — upward (or forward) — with equal force.

**The rocket doesn't push off the atmosphere. It pushes off its own exhaust.**

This is why rockets carry fuel: the reaction partner is the expelled mass, not the environment.

Analogy: stand on frictionless ice and throw a heavy bag to your right. You slide left. You pushed the bag; the bag pushed you back.

Now connect this back to Session 2 (F=ma): both the rocket and the exhaust experience the same force. The exhaust, being much less massive at any given moment, accelerates enormously. The rocket, being heavier, accelerates less — but continuously, as long as fuel burns.

---

## The Three Laws Together

| Law | Statement | Shorthand |
|-----|-----------|-----------|
| First | Objects don't change motion unless forced | Inertia — status quo persists |
| Second | F = ma — more force or less mass = more acceleration | Output scales with force and inversely with mass |
| Third | F₁₂ = −F₂₁ — forces come in equal-opposite pairs on different objects | No unilateral action in nature |
