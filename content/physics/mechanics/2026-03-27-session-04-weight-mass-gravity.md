## Start here: your bathroom scales are lying to you

Step on a bathroom scale. It says 80 kg. But that's not what it's measuring.

The scale contains a spring. When you stand on it, you compress that spring. The spring is measuring **force** — the force with which gravity is pulling you down toward the centre of the Earth. That force has a name: it's called your **weight**. And weight isn't measured in kilograms. It's measured in **Newtons**.

So why does the scale say 80 kg? Because it's been programmed to divide by 9.8 and give you the number in kilograms, because that's what people are used to seeing.

If you took those same scales to the Moon, they'd say about 13 kg. Not because you lost 67 kg — you didn't. Your body didn't change at all. But the Moon's gravity is about one-sixth as strong as Earth's, so the spring compresses less, and the scale gives you a different number.

That confusion — the scale showing the same kind of number whether you're on Earth or the Moon — is what this session is about. There are actually **two different things** getting mixed up here, and until you separate them, gravity will always feel a bit fuzzy.

---

## Concept 1: Mass and Weight are completely different things

**Mass** is the amount of matter in you. How many atoms. How much "stuff". It's measured in kilograms and it **never changes** — not on Earth, not on the Moon, not floating in deep space.

**Weight** is a force. It's the gravitational pull on that mass by a nearby planet or object. It's measured in **Newtons** and it **changes** depending on where you are.

Think of it like this:

> **Mass is your capital. Weight is what that capital is worth in a given market.**

If you have £100,000 in capital, that doesn't change based on location. But whether that capital is invested at a 9.8% return rate or a 1.6% return rate determines what it generates. Same capital, different output. Mass is fixed; weight depends on the gravitational environment.

The equation that connects them:

```
W = mg
```

- **W** = weight (in Newtons, N)
- **m** = mass (in kilograms, kg)
- **g** = gravitational field strength of the planet you're on (in N/kg or m/s²)

On Earth, g ≈ 9.8 N/kg.
On the Moon, g ≈ 1.6 N/kg.

So for an 80 kg person:
- **Earth:** W = 80 × 9.8 = **784 N**
- **Moon:** W = 80 × 1.6 = **128 N**

Same person. Same mass. Very different weight.

---

## Concept 2: What g actually is

The number 9.8 is one of the most important numbers in physics. It appears constantly. It's the **gravitational field strength at Earth's surface** — which means: for every kilogram of mass, Earth's gravity pulls with a force of 9.8 Newtons.

But g = 9.8 isn't just "the strength of the pull." It also tells you **how fast things accelerate when they fall freely**.

This is where F = ma (Session 2) and this session collide.

If you drop something:
- The force pulling it down = its weight = mg
- F = ma (Newton's Second Law)
- So: mg = ma
- The mass cancels: **a = g**

Every falling object accelerates at g = **9.8 metres per second, per second** — regardless of mass.

This is why g has units of both **N/kg** (force per unit mass) and **m/s²** (acceleration). Same number. Two interpretations.

---

## Concept 3: Free fall — why Galileo was right and Aristotle was wrong

For about 2,000 years, educated people believed heavier objects fall faster. Galileo tested it around 1600 — two cannonballs of different masses from the Leaning Tower of Pisa. They landed simultaneously.

**The logic:**

A 10 kg ball is 10× heavier than a 1 kg ball → gravity pulls it with 10× the force. But it also has 10× the mass → 10× the inertia. Bigger force and bigger resistance cancel exactly.

```
a = F/m = mg/m = g
```

The m cancels. Every time. The acceleration is always g = 9.8 m/s², regardless of mass.

**But feathers don't fall like cannonballs** — because of air resistance. Air drag depends on surface area and speed, not mass. In a vacuum, they fall identically.

In 1971, Apollo 15 astronaut David Scott stood on the Moon (a near-vacuum) and dropped a geological hammer and a falcon feather simultaneously. They hit the dust at the same time.

## Connecting back: answering Session 3's open question

*"Same gravitational force on you and Earth — why do YOU fall?"*

- Your mass: ~80 kg → a = F/80 → you accelerate a lot.
- Earth's mass: 5.97 × 10²⁴ kg → a = F/(5.97 × 10²⁴) → Earth moves by an unmeasurably tiny amount.

Same force. Completely different accelerations. F = ma doing its job.

## Key formulas

| Formula | What it means |
|--------|---------------|
| W = mg | Weight (N) = mass (kg) × gravitational field strength (N/kg) |
| g ≈ 9.8 m/s² | Free-fall acceleration at Earth's surface |
| a = g in free fall | All objects fall at the same rate (vacuum) |

---

## What to hold onto

1. **Mass** = amount of matter (kg). Never changes.
2. **Weight** = gravitational force on that mass (N). Changes with location.
3. **g = 9.8** — field strength (N/kg) AND free-fall acceleration (m/s²). Same number.
4. **Free fall:** mass cancels in F = ma → every object falls at g regardless of weight.
5. **Air resistance** is why feathers float — not gravity.
