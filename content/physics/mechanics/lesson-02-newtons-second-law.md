Newton's Second Law is the most important equation in classical mechanics. It tells you exactly what happens when a force acts on an object:

$$F = ma$$

Force equals mass times acceleration. That's the whole thing. But unpacking what it actually means takes a moment.

**Force** ($F$) is a push or a pull. It's measured in Newtons (N). When you kick a ball, your foot exerts a force on it. Gravity exerts a force on everything with mass.

**Mass** ($m$) is resistance to being accelerated — physicists call this *inertia*. A bowling ball has more mass than a tennis ball, which is why it takes more force to get it moving at the same rate. Mass is measured in kilograms (kg).

**Acceleration** ($a$) is the rate of change of velocity — how quickly something is speeding up, slowing down, or changing direction. It's measured in metres per second squared (m/s²). Crucially, acceleration is not the same as speed. Something can be moving fast but not accelerating (constant velocity), or moving slowly but accelerating rapidly.

The law is a relationship between these three things. Rearrange it and it becomes two more useful statements:

$$a = \frac{F}{m}$$

More force → more acceleration. More mass → less acceleration for the same force.

$$m = \frac{F}{a}$$

This tells you how to measure mass: apply a known force, measure the resulting acceleration, and divide.

## The direction matters

Newton's Second Law is a *vector* equation — force and acceleration both have direction. If you push something to the right, it accelerates to the right. If gravity pulls something down, it accelerates downward.

This is why the equation is sometimes written:

$$\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$$

The arrows indicate vectors. In problems where forces act in multiple directions, you resolve them into components (horizontal and vertical) and apply $F = ma$ separately in each direction.

## Net force

The $F$ in Newton's Second Law is the *net* force — the total of all forces acting on the object, taking direction into account.

If you push a box to the right with 10 N and friction pushes back to the left with 4 N, the net force is 6 N to the right. That's the force that determines the acceleration.

If the net force is zero — forces perfectly balanced — acceleration is zero and the object moves at constant velocity (or stays still). This is Newton's First Law, which is just a special case of the Second.

## Worked Example

**A 5 kg box sits on a frictionless surface. You push it with a force of 20 N. What is its acceleration?**

Using $a = F/m$:

$$a = \frac{20\,\text{N}}{5\,\text{kg}} = 4\,\text{m/s}^2$$

The box accelerates at 4 metres per second squared in the direction of the push.

**Now add friction.** The surface exerts a friction force of 5 N opposing the motion. What is the acceleration now?

Net force = $20\,\text{N} - 5\,\text{N} = 15\,\text{N}$

$$a = \frac{15\,\text{N}}{5\,\text{kg}} = 3\,\text{m/s}^2$$

## Open Question

Gravity pulls you toward the Earth with a force equal to your mass times $g$ (9.8 m/s²). When you stand on the floor, you're not accelerating — but gravity is still pulling. What force must the floor be exerting on you, and in what direction?
