# Physics — Session 1: What is a force?

> *The single idea that unlocks all of classical mechanics.*

---

## The simplest true statement

A force is a push or a pull. But the interesting part is what it actually *does*: it changes an object's motion.

Not causes motion. *Changes* motion.

---

## Newton's First Law — the counterintuitive one

> *An object keeps doing what it's doing unless something forces it to stop.*

A ball rolling on a frictionless surface would roll forever. Nothing needs to "keep" it moving. That's deeply unintuitive — it feels like things need continuous pushing to stay in motion. But that's only because in the real world, friction and air resistance are always secretly pushing back.

**Key idea:** Motion is the default. Rest is just a special case of "constant velocity" (velocity = 0).

---

## Newton's Second Law — the equation

$$F = ma$$

- **F** = force (Newtons, N)
- **m** = mass (kilograms, kg)
- **a** = acceleration (metres per second squared, m/s²)

This is arguably the most important equation in all of classical physics. Everything else derives from it.

What it says: the bigger the force, the more acceleration. The heavier the object, the less acceleration for the same force.

**Example:** You push a shopping trolley (20kg) with 40N of force. Its acceleration = 40 ÷ 20 = 2 m/s².

---

## Forces have direction — they're vectors

This is where it gets interesting. Force isn't just a number — it has a direction. 40N to the right and 40N to the left cancel out. The net force is zero. No acceleration.

This is why:
- A book sitting on a table isn't accelerating — gravity pulls it down, the table pushes back up. Net force = 0.
- A car braking — the engine may still be running forward, but the brakes exert force backward. The net force determines whether you speed up, slow down, or hold constant.

**Vectors:** Any quantity with both magnitude AND direction is a vector. Force, velocity, acceleration — all vectors. Mass is not (it's a scalar — just a number).

---

## Forces in everyday life

| Situation | What's pushing/pulling | Net force |
|-----------|----------------------|-----------|
| Falling apple | Gravity (down) | Downward → accelerates |
| Book on table | Gravity (down) + Normal force (up) | Zero → stays still |
| Car accelerating | Engine + friction vs air resistance | Forward → speeds up |
| Ball caught by hand | Hand exerts force backward on ball | Backward → decelerates to zero |

---

## Three concepts to remember

1. Force changes motion — it doesn't create or maintain it
2. F = ma — force, mass, and acceleration are linked; change one, you change the others
3. Forces are vectors — direction matters; opposite forces cancel

---

## Open Question

> You're standing on a weighing scale in a lift (elevator). The scale shows your weight.
>
> When the lift starts moving *upward*, does the scale reading go up, down, or stay the same? What about when the lift is moving upward at *constant speed*? And when it slows down to a stop?
>
> *(Hint: think about what the scale is actually measuring — it's not your mass.)*

**Kevin's answer:** Scale reads higher when lift accelerates upward (the scale must push your feet up harder than gravity pulls you down, so your feet press down on it more). Same reading at constant speed (no acceleration, net force = 0). Lower reading when decelerating to a stop. Key insight reached: a scale measures the contact/compression force between you and it — not mass directly. Also understood that the elevator's upward force acts on the bottom of the scale, not the top, so it doesn't cancel the downward force from your feet — it causes the increased squeeze. Connected to Newton's Third Law: forces always come in equal and opposite pairs across the contact point. ✅ (2026-03-23)
