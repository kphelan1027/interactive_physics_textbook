## Opening: The Sofa Problem

Imagine you're moving flats. There's a massive sofa in the middle of the living room. You put your shoulder against it and push — nothing happens. You push harder — still nothing. Then you really commit, and suddenly it scrapes forward.

Now here's the interesting part: once it's moving, it feels slightly easier to keep it going than it was to get it started in the first place.

That sequence — the initial resistance, the threshold, the easier slide — is exactly what today's session is about.

But first, we need to answer the question we left open at the end of Session 4.

---

## Concept 1: The Normal Force — The Floor's Answer to Gravity

At the end of last session, the open question was: *gravity is pulling you down at 9.8 m/s², but you're not accelerating. What's pushing back?*

Here's Newton's Second Law doing the work (remember: F = ma). If you're sitting still — acceleration is zero. Net force must be zero. But gravity is definitely pulling you down. So something must be pushing you up with exactly the same magnitude.

That something is the **Normal Force**.

When you stand on a floor, the floor pushes back on you — not because it's "trying" to, but because it's being compressed by your weight and matter resists compression. The floor is like a stiff spring: the more you press on it, the more it pushes back. In the case of a solid floor, it pushes back until the forces balance exactly.

**Critical point:** Normal force is always *perpendicular* to the surface — hence "normal" (in maths, normal means perpendicular). It doesn't push you sideways. It pushes you straight out from whatever surface you're resting on.

**The equation:** On a flat, horizontal surface:

> **N = mg**

Same number as weight — different direction, different object. Weight (gravity) acts downward on you from Earth. Normal force acts upward on you from the floor. They're not an action-reaction pair (those act on *different* objects); they're two separate forces on *you* that happen to cancel.

**Analogy:** Think of the Normal Force like the floor sending you a perfectly itemised invoice. You show up with your full weight — the floor charges you exactly that amount back, to the penny, every time. No more, no less. Equilibrium is the receipt.

## Concept 2: Friction — Why You Don't Slide Off Everything

So if the floor pushes straight back at you (Normal Force), what's stopping you from sliding sideways whenever you walk?

That's **friction** — and it's more interesting than it sounds.

Everything you think of as smooth — a polished floor, a table surface, your phone screen — is covered in microscopic peaks and valleys at the atomic scale. When two surfaces touch, those peaks interlock like the world's tiniest Velcro. When you try to slide one surface against another, you're tearing all those tiny interlocks apart. That tearing requires force. That required force is friction.

**Friction always opposes the direction of (attempted) motion.** Push a box to the right: friction points left. Push it left: friction points right. It's not a fixed enemy — it responds to what you're trying to do.

**Key insight:** Friction depends on two things:
1. **How rough the surfaces are** — captured by a number called the *coefficient of friction* (μ, "mu")
2. **How hard the surfaces are pressed together** — which is the Normal Force (N)

> **f = μN**

That's it. Rough surfaces (high μ), heavy load (high N) = strong friction. Smooth surfaces, light load = weak friction.

---

## Concept 3: Static vs Kinetic Friction — Why Starting is Harder Than Continuing

**Static friction** acts when the surfaces are not yet sliding. It's *reactive* — it matches whatever force you apply, up to a maximum. Push beyond that maximum, and the surfaces break free.

> **f_s(max) = μ_s × N**

Once sliding, you switch to **kinetic friction** — always less than maximum static friction:

> **f_k = μ_k × N** (and μ_k < μ_s, always)

**Analogy:** Closing a deal (breaking static friction) costs far more in sales effort than renewing it (kinetic friction). The threshold is the hard part. Once crossed, momentum does much of the work.

## Putting It Together

| Force | Symbol | Formula | Direction |
|-------|--------|---------|-----------|
| Weight | W | mg | Downward |
| Normal Force | N | mg (flat surface) | Perpendicular to surface |
| Max Static Friction | f_s | μ_s × N | Opposing intended motion |
| Kinetic Friction | f_k | μ_k × N | Opposing actual motion |

Typical μ values:
- Ice on ice: ~0.03
- Rubber on dry road: ~0.7
- Steel on steel: ~0.4

## Connecting to Previous Sessions

- **Session 2 (F = ma):** When friction equals applied force, net force = 0, no acceleration. Push harder than friction: net force ≠ 0, acceleration appears.
- **Session 4 (Weight and g):** N = mg — heavier object = more Normal Force = more friction. Same μ, different N.
- **Session 3 (Third Law):** Box exerts friction on floor in one direction; floor exerts friction on box in the other. Third Law pair — but on different objects, as always.

## Open Question

What if the floor tilts?

On a slope, Normal Force is no longer vertical — it's perpendicular to the slope, so N < mg. The weight now has a component *along* the slope pulling the object downhill. Two numbers tell you whether the object slides: the slope angle and the coefficient of friction.

**Sit with this:** If you park on a 30° slope and the handbrake fails, is the car more likely to slide on ice or dry road? What formula connects the slope angle, friction coefficient, and whether it slides?
