## The Hook — The Hill That Costs You

You're driving from London to somewhere with a long uphill stretch. You press the accelerator to maintain your speed, and you can *feel* it: the engine is working harder, fuel burning faster. On flat motorway at the same speed, it's coasting. Same car. Same speed. Very different effort.

Why? You're fighting something. And physics has a very precise name for what you're doing in both situations: **work**.

This session answers the question left open from Session 11: kinetic energy is "lost" in an inelastic collision — where does it go? To answer that properly, we first need to understand what energy *is*, and work is the bridge that gets us there.

## Concept 1: Work — Physics Has a Very Strict Definition

In everyday language, "work" means effort. You work hard, you're tired, you worked out. But in physics, the word has a very specific meaning — and it might surprise you.

**Physics definition of work:**
> Work is done when a force causes movement in the direction of that force.

Both conditions must be true:
- There must be a **force**
- There must be **displacement in the direction of the force**

No movement? **No work done.** Even if you're straining.

### The Bouncer at the Door

Imagine a bouncer holding a velvet rope, arms extended, stopping people from walking through. He's exerting a force — his muscles are burning. But the rope isn't moving anywhere. In physics: **he has done zero work**. All that effort, physiologically real, is physically zero.

Now imagine Kevin pushing a broken-down car along a car park to get it out of a space. The car moves 5 metres. Force was applied. Displacement happened in the direction of the force. **Work was done.**

### The Equation

$$W = F \times d$$

Where:
- $W$ = work done (joules, J)
- $F$ = force applied (newtons, N)
- $d$ = distance moved in the direction of the force (metres, m)

**Units:** 1 joule = 1 newton × 1 metre.

Full formula when force is at angle θ to direction of motion: $W = Fd\cos\theta$

### Product Analogy

Think of work like **investment that actually ships something**. A team can be busy, in meetings, in planning sessions — effort without displacement. Physics would say: no work done. The moment features land in users' hands (displacement), work was done.

## Concept 2: Kinetic Energy — The Energy of Motion

Remember from Session 10: momentum is $p = mv$. **Kinetic energy is different.** It captures how much work was done to get something moving.

$$KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$$

Where:
- $KE$ = kinetic energy (joules, J)
- $m$ = mass (kilograms, kg)
- $v$ = speed (metres per second, m/s)

### Momentum vs Kinetic Energy (Common Confusion)

| | Momentum | Kinetic Energy |
|--|----------|----------------|
| Formula | $p = mv$ | $KE = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ |
| Units | kg·m/s | joules (J) |
| Double the speed | doubles | **quadruples** |
| Vector? | Yes (has direction) | No (scalar) |

Double the speed → momentum doubles, but KE quadruples. This is why speeding is so dangerous — stopping requirements grow with the square of speed.

### The Work-Energy Theorem

$$W_{net} = \Delta KE = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2$$

Net work done on an object = its change in kinetic energy. Work is the mechanism by which energy is transferred into motion.

**Quick Example:** A 1,200 kg car accelerates from 0 to 20 m/s:
$$KE = \frac{1}{2} \times 1200 \times 400 = 240,000 \text{ J} = 240 \text{ kJ}$$

Your engine did 240 kJ of work to achieve that. Your brakes must absorb exactly that much energy to stop.

## Concept 3: Power — The Rate of Doing Work

Two builders lift the same bricks to the same height — same work done. Builder A takes 10 minutes. Builder B takes 2 minutes. Builder B is more powerful.

$$P = \frac{W}{t}$$

Where:
- $P$ = power (watts, W)
- $W$ = work done (joules, J)
- $t$ = time taken (seconds, s)

**1 watt = 1 joule per second.**

Alternative form: since $W = Fd$ and $P = W/t$:

$$P = F \times v$$

Power = force × velocity. This is why maintaining high speed on a motorway demands more engine power than the same force at low speed.
