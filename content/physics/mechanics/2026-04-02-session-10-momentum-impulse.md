## The Hook — Why Does an Airbag Save Your Life?

Picture this: you're driving at 30 mph and you hit a wall. You stop. Your body goes from 30 mph to 0 mph — that's a fact, physics insists on it.

The question isn't *whether* you stop. It's *how fast* you stop.

A car with no airbag stops you in about 0.01 seconds. The airbag buys you 0.1 seconds — ten times longer. That doesn't sound like much. But the difference between those two scenarios is the difference between walking away and not.

Why? That's what this session is about.

---

## Concept 1: Momentum — How Hard Is Something to Stop?

Before the maths, the idea.

Think about two situations:
- A **truck** rolling slowly through a car park at 5 mph
- A **cricket ball** flying at you at 80 mph

Which is harder to stop? The truck, obviously — it's far heavier. But if the cricket ball were going 1,000 mph, you'd think twice about stepping in front of it.

**Momentum is the combination of mass and velocity.** It captures "how hard something is to stop" in a single number.

A slow heavy thing can have the same momentum as a fast light thing. That's the key insight.

### The Maths

$$p = mv$$

- **p** = momentum (kg⋅m/s)
- **m** = mass (kg)
- **v** = velocity (m/s)

A 1,000 kg car at 13 m/s (about 30 mph) has momentum:
$$p = 1000 \times 13 = 13{,}000 \text{ kg⋅m/s}$$

That's 13,000 units of "hardness to stop." That momentum has to go somewhere in a crash.

> **Connection to previous sessions:** We covered velocity in Sessions 7–8 (SUVAT equations and motion graphs). Momentum is what happens when velocity collides with mass. It's velocity that matters, not just size.

## Concept 2: Impulse — The Only Way to Change Momentum

Momentum doesn't just change by itself. Something has to push on the object — a force — and that force has to act for some amount of time.

**Impulse** is that combination: force multiplied by the time it acts.

$$J = F \Delta t$$

- **J** = impulse (N⋅s, which equals kg⋅m/s)
- **F** = force (Newtons)
- **Δt** = time the force acts (seconds)

And here's the key equation that ties it all together — the **impulse-momentum theorem**:

$$F \Delta t = \Delta p = m \Delta v$$

The impulse equals the *change* in momentum.

To stop your 1,000 kg car (momentum = 13,000 kg⋅m/s), you need an impulse of exactly 13,000 N⋅s — no matter what. Physics won't negotiate.

> **This is Newton's 2nd Law in disguise.** F = ma is the same as F = m(Δv/Δt), which rearranges to FΔt = mΔv.

---

## Concept 3: The Time Trade-Off

**You can achieve the same change in momentum with a large force over a short time, OR a small force over a long time.**

The impulse (area under the force-time curve) is the same. But the *peak force* is wildly different.

| Scenario | Δp required | Time (Δt) | Force felt |
|---|---|---|---|
| No airbag | 13,000 N⋅s | 0.01 s | **1,300,000 N** |
| With airbag | 13,000 N⋅s | 0.1 s | **130,000 N** |

The airbag doesn't reduce the total momentum change. It spreads the stop over 10× more time, cutting the peak force by 10×.

### Real-world applications:
- **Cricket:** Pulling your hand back as you catch extends Δt, reducing F.
- **Long jump mat:** Same Δp, but 10× more time to stop.
- **Karate:** Opposite strategy — short contact time concentrates force into maximum peak.

## Analogy: Budget Cuts as Impulse

You need to absorb a £1M budget reduction:
- **Option A:** Take it all in January. One month, full pain.
- **Option B:** Spread it across 12 months. Same total, 1/12th monthly force.

The "impulse" on the P&L is identical. The *force* is completely different.

## Worked Example

A 70 kg person running at 5 m/s stops in 0.5 seconds. What average force does the ground exert?

$$\Delta p = m \Delta v = 70 \times (0 - 5) = -350 \text{ kg⋅m/s}$$
$$F = \frac{\Delta p}{\Delta t} = \frac{-350}{0.5} = -700 \text{ N}$$

If they hit a wall and stopped in 0.05 s: F = 7,000 N. Ten times worse.

## Quick Check

1. A 500 kg motorbike travels at 20 m/s. What is its momentum?
2. A force of 200 N acts on a ball for 0.03 seconds. What impulse does it deliver?
3. Why does a boxing glove hurt less than a bare knuckle punch with equal power?

*(Answers: 10,000 kg⋅m/s; 6 N⋅s; glove extends contact time, reducing peak force.)*
