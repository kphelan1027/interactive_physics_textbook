## The Hook: Sound Across a Room

You've been in this situation. Music plays from someone's phone across a room. The sound reaches you. The phone didn't move. The air molecules near the speaker didn't fly across the room and hit your eardrum — they barely shifted from where they started. And yet the sound arrived.

*Something* travelled. But *what*?

This is the central puzzle of waves. Getting the answer right changes how you see sound, light, earthquakes, Wi-Fi, and music. All of it.

---

## Building on What You Know

From Sessions 12 and 13: energy is never created or destroyed — it transforms. GPE → KE → heat/sound. A wave is going to extend this: **energy can move through space without matter moving with it.** The disturbance travels. The medium stays.

From Session 14: centripetal force does zero work because it acts perpendicular to velocity. You'll see a similar geometric idea here with transverse waves — oscillation perpendicular to travel. Same geometry, new context.

## Concept 1: A Wave is a Travelling Disturbance

Hold one end of a long rope. Someone holds the other end. Flick your wrist up and down once. A bump travels to their hand. The rope didn't travel. Each bit of rope just moved up, then came back down. The **pattern** — the disturbance — moved. The **matter** didn't go with it.

That's a wave: **a pattern of oscillation that propagates through a medium, transferring energy from one point to another, without the medium itself making the journey.**

**The crowd wave.** Everyone in the stadium stands up briefly, then sits. Nobody walked across the stadium. But the "wave" circles the whole ground at speed. The *pattern* moved; the *people* stayed. This is exactly the physics.

**Product analogy:** When a product strategy propagates through a company, the *idea* moves from team to team. Each person absorbs it and passes it on. No one moves offices. The signal travels; the medium (people, teams) stays in place. Waves work on precisely this principle.

## Concept 2: Two Ways Matter Can Oscillate — Transverse vs Longitudinal

Accept that waves are travelling disturbances. Now ask: *how* does the medium oscillate as the wave passes through it?

There are exactly two fundamental answers.

### Transverse Waves

The medium oscillates **perpendicular to** the direction the wave is travelling.

Back to the rope. Your wrist moves up and down. The wave travels *along* the rope (horizontally). The rope segments move *vertically*. Up-down is 90° to along-the-rope. That's transverse.

Other examples:
- **Ripples on a pond:** the water surface moves up and down; the ripple travels outward
- **A guitar string:** vibrates up and down; the wave travels along the string's length
- **All electromagnetic waves** (light, radio, X-rays, Wi-Fi): the electric and magnetic fields oscillate perpendicular to the direction of travel

*Visual test:* Draw an arrow for the wave direction. If the oscillation arrows are 90° to it, it's transverse.

### Longitudinal Waves

The medium oscillates **parallel to** — in the same direction as — the wave's travel.

The best physical tool: a Slinky. Push one end toward the other. You see alternating **compressions** (coils bunched together) and **rarefactions** (coils spread apart) travelling down its length. The coils themselves move back and forth *along* the direction of travel.

**Sound is a longitudinal wave.** When the speaker cone pushes outward, it compresses the air molecules in front of it. Those compressed molecules push the next layer, which pushes the next — a compression ripple travels outward at ~340 m/s. Each air molecule only oscillates a tiny distance around its home position. Your eardrum reads the incoming pattern of compressions and rarefactions. The *frequency* of that pattern is what you hear as pitch.

| | Transverse | Longitudinal |
|---|---|---|
| Oscillation direction | ⊥ to travel | ∥ to travel |
| Key feature | Peaks and troughs | Compressions and rarefactions |
| Examples | Rope, light, water surface | Sound, seismic P-waves, Slinky |

## Concept 3: The Medium — and the Famous Exception

Waves propagate through a **medium**: any material the disturbance travels through.

- Sound through **air**: ~340 m/s
- Sound through **water**: ~1,480 m/s
- Sound through **steel**: ~5,000 m/s

Why faster in denser/stiffer solids? The molecules are tightly packed and spring back rapidly — more elastic restoring force → faster propagation.

**The famous exception — electromagnetic waves:**

Light, radio waves, X-rays, microwaves, Wi-Fi. All travel through a *vacuum*. No medium required.

This is extraordinary. In the 19th century, physicists invented a fictional substance called the **"luminiferous aether"** to fill space — convinced that waves needed something to propagate through. The Michelson-Morley experiment (1887) proved it didn't exist. Light needs nothing.

Why? Because electromagnetic waves aren't mechanical disturbances of a medium. They're oscillating electric and magnetic fields that *sustain each other*: a changing electric field generates a magnetic field, which generates an electric field, which propagates the wave forward. Self-sustaining. Medium-independent. Speed in vacuum: **c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s**.

## The Maths — Foundations Only

Every wave has three properties:

| Symbol | Name | Definition | Unit |
|--------|------|-----------|------|
| **λ** (lambda) | Wavelength | Distance from one peak (or compression) to the next — length of one complete cycle | metres (m) |
| **f** | Frequency | Number of complete cycles passing a fixed point per second | Hz (cycles/s) |
| **v** | Wave speed | How fast the disturbance travels through the medium | m/s |

These three are related — we'll derive the wave equation next session. Preview: if wave speed stays constant (same medium), a higher frequency *must* mean a shorter wavelength.

## Open Question

**If you double the frequency of a sound wave — go up one octave on a piano — but the wave is still travelling through the same room air... what happens to the wavelength? And what stays the same?**

The answer to that gives you the wave equation: v = fλ.
