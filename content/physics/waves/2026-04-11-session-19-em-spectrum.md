## The Hook: Two Types of Radiation, One Key Difference

You look at your phone screen for three hours. No damage.

Your dentist puts a lead apron on you before taking an X-ray. Two seconds of exposure. They leave the room.

Both are electromagnetic radiation. Both travel at exactly the same speed — 300,000,000 metres per second. Both are invisible except for the tiny slice your eye can detect.

The **only** difference between them is frequency.

That single number — how fast the wave oscillates — determines whether EM radiation is completely harmless background noise or something that can shred your DNA. Understanding why is what today's session is about.

## Concept 1: What EM Radiation Actually Is

We said in Session 15 that EM waves are "self-sustaining." Here's what that means.

When an electric charge oscillates, it creates an oscillating electric field. That oscillating electric field generates an oscillating magnetic field. That oscillating magnetic field generates an oscillating electric field. They bootstrap each other — two fields, perpendicular to each other and to the direction of travel, leapfrogging through space.

No medium needed. They're their own medium.

And they always travel at the same speed in a vacuum:

> **c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s**

This is one of the fundamental constants of the universe. It doesn't matter whether it's a radio signal, visible light, an X-ray, or a gamma ray from a nuclear explosion — in vacuum, they all travel at exactly c.

Using v = fλ (Session 17), with v = c for all EM waves:

> **λ = c / f**

Higher frequency → shorter wavelength. Always. For every EM wave.

## Concept 2: The Spectrum — Ordered by Frequency

The electromagnetic spectrum is just the full range of all possible frequencies of EM radiation. There's no gap between the bands — they blend into each other. The names are convenient human labels for regions that behave differently or have different practical uses.

From lowest to highest frequency:

| Band | Frequency | Wavelength | Real-world examples |
|------|-----------|------------|---------------------|
| **Radio** | 10³–10⁸ Hz | km to metres | AM/FM radio, TV broadcast, mobile signals |
| **Microwave** | 10⁸–10¹² Hz | cm to mm | WiFi (2.4/5 GHz), 5G, microwave ovens (2.45 GHz), radar |
| **Infrared** | 10¹²–4×10¹⁴ Hz | mm to 700 nm | Heat radiation, thermal cameras, TV remotes, night vision |
| **Visible** | 4–7×10¹⁴ Hz | 700–380 nm | The only band the human eye can detect. Red → violet |
| **Ultraviolet** | 10¹⁵ Hz | 380–10 nm | Sunburn, vitamin D production, sterilisation |
| **X-rays** | 10¹⁷–10¹⁹ Hz | 10 nm–10 pm | Medical imaging, airport security, material analysis |
| **Gamma rays** | 10¹⁸–10²² Hz | <10 pm | Nuclear reactions, cancer radiotherapy, sterilisation |

**The visible spectrum is a tiny sliver.** Human eyes evolved to detect light in the 380–700 nm range — which happens to be where the Sun outputs most of its energy.

## Concept 3: Why Frequency = Energy = Danger Threshold

EM radiation comes in discrete packets of energy called **photons**. Each photon carries a fixed amount of energy determined by its frequency:

> **E = hf**

Where:
- E = energy (joules)
- h = Planck's constant = 6.63 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s
- f = frequency (Hz)

**Higher frequency = higher energy per photon.** Always.

### The ionisation threshold

Chemical bonds require a minimum energy to break. Below that energy, photons bounce off or pass through. Above it, photons can break bonds, ionise atoms, and — in biological tissue — cause mutations.

That threshold lies at roughly the UV range:

| Region | Ionising? | Biological effect |
|--------|-----------|-------------------|
| Radio, Microwave, Infrared, Visible | **Non-ionising** | Heating only at high intensities |
| UV, X-ray, Gamma | **Ionising** | Can break DNA bonds; mutational risk |

### Why sound is different

Sound energy is proportional to amplitude (loudness), not frequency. A loud bass note carries more energy than a quiet high note.

For EM radiation, a single high-frequency photon carries more energy than any number of low-frequency photons individually. One X-ray photon ionises; a trillion radio photons don't — because each one has too little energy per packet.

## The Maths: Wavelengths Across the Spectrum

Using λ = c/f with c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s:

| Wave type | Frequency | Wavelength |
|-----------|-----------|------------|
| FM radio (100 MHz) | 10⁸ Hz | **3 m** |
| WiFi (2.4 GHz) | 2.4 × 10⁹ Hz | **12.5 cm** |
| 5G (28 GHz) | 2.8 × 10¹⁰ Hz | **1.07 cm** |
| Infrared (body heat) | ~10¹³ Hz | **30 μm** |
| Red light | 4.3 × 10¹⁴ Hz | **700 nm** |
| Violet light | 7.5 × 10¹⁴ Hz | **400 nm** |
| UV-B (sunburn) | ~10¹⁵ Hz | **300 nm** |
| X-ray (medical) | ~10¹⁸ Hz | **0.1 nm** |

## Summary: Three Ideas

1. **EM radiation is self-sustaining** — E and B fields bootstrap each other. All travel at c. λ = c/f.
2. **The spectrum is one continuous range** labelled by frequency. Low f = long λ = low photon energy. High f = short λ = high photon energy.
3. **Frequency determines photon energy (E = hf)** — above the UV threshold, photons ionise atoms and damage DNA. Below it: non-ionising. Sound has no photon equivalent, so frequency only changes pitch.
