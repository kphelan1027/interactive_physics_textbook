# Accounting — Session 13: Working Capital

> *The gap between paying out and getting paid — and why Memrise's gap works in its favour.*

---

## What You Already Know

In Session 12 you built a picture of the balance sheet — assets on one side, liabilities and equity on the other, always balancing. You saw that current assets (cash, receivables) and current liabilities (deferred revenue, payables) sit at the top of each column. Working capital is simply the relationship between those two groups. It tells you whether the business can pay its short-term bills without reaching for a credit line.

## The simplest true statement

**Working capital is current assets minus current liabilities — and for a subscription business, having negative working capital is often a sign of strength, not distress.**

---

## The Basic Formula

```
Working Capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities
```

For a traditional business — say, a manufacturer — positive working capital is essential. They buy raw materials on credit, make a product, sell it, and wait 60 days to be paid. In the gap between paying suppliers and collecting from customers, they need cash reserves. Negative working capital means they can't cover their near-term obligations.

But subscription businesses invert this entirely. Let's see why.

---

## Memrise's Working Capital Picture

Using approximate figures from the balance sheet we built in Session 12:

**Current Assets**

| Item | Approximate value |
|---|---|
| Cash and cash equivalents | ~£5–7M |
| Trade receivables (B2B) | ~£500k |
| **Total current assets** | **~£5.5–7.5M** |

**Current Liabilities**

| Item | Approximate value |
|---|---|
| Deferred revenue | ~£2.3M |
| Trade payables | ~£200k |
| Accruals and other payables | ~£400k |
| **Total current liabilities** | **~£2.9M** |

```
Working Capital ≈ £6.5M − £2.9M ≈ +£3.6M
```

Memrise shows positive working capital overall — largely because of the substantial cash position from investor funding. But here's the interesting layer: strip out the investor cash and look at the operating working capital from subscription revenue alone.

---

## B2C: The Structural Advantage

When a consumer subscribes annually to Memrise — let's say they pay £59.99 upfront in January — Memrise receives the cash immediately. The service obligation runs for 12 months. Accounting records ~£5 of revenue per month and holds the rest in deferred revenue. But the cash is already in the bank.

This means B2C subscriptions create a situation where Memrise is funded by its customers before it delivers the service. The deferred revenue liability represents real money already collected. If Memrise had no investor capital at all, the subscription model would still generate cash before obligations — the opposite of most businesses.

**The consumer cash cycle:**

```
Customer pays upfront → Cash arrives immediately → Service delivered over time
```

No waiting. No credit risk. No accounts receivable for B2C.

---

## B2B: Where the Timing Lag Appears

Enterprise and team customers behave differently. A school or corporation signs a contract and receives an invoice. Payment terms are typically 30–60 days. Until that invoice is paid, Memrise has delivered (or committed to deliver) the service but hasn't collected the cash.

This creates **accounts receivable** — the ~£500k visible on the balance sheet. Memrise has earned the right to receive that money but doesn't have it yet. The measure of how quickly customers pay is called Days Sales Outstanding (DSO):

```
DSO = (Accounts Receivable ÷ Annual Revenue) × 365
```

If Memrise's B2B AR is ~£500k and B2B revenue runs at, say, £2M/year, DSO is roughly 90 days. That's on the longer side — worth managing actively by tightening payment terms or offering early payment incentives.

**The B2B cash cycle:**

```
Contract signed → Invoice issued → 30–60 days → Cash arrives
```

---

## Days Payable Outstanding: The Other Side

Memrise also has time before it must pay its own suppliers. Days Payable Outstanding (DPO) measures how long the company takes to pay:

```
DPO = (Accounts Payable ÷ Annual COGS) × 365
```

The longer DPO, the longer Memrise keeps its own cash. This is why large companies stretch payment terms to 60–90 days — it's free financing. Memrise's trade payables of ~£200k suggest relatively short payment cycles, which is typical for a software business with few physical suppliers.

---

## Three Concepts to Remember

1. **Working capital = Current Assets − Current Liabilities** — positive is not always better; for subscription businesses, the sign matters less than the quality of what drives it.
2. **B2C subscriptions create negative operating working capital in your favour** — cash arrives before the obligation, making subscribers Memrise's implicit short-term lenders.
3. **B2B billing creates accounts receivable and DSO risk** — the longer customers take to pay, the more cash is tied up in invoices, not the bank account.
