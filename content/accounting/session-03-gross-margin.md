# Accounting — Session 3: COGS, Gross Margin, and the App Store Tax

> *Gross margin is what you actually have left to run the company — after paying for the product to exist.*

---

## What You Already Know

In Session 2, we saw that Memrise recognised £443,490 in revenue in January 2026 — but left on a cliffhanger: before any of that reaches salaries or profit, something comes off the top.

---

## What is COGS?

COGS stands for Cost of Goods Sold. The name comes from manufacturing — if you're a baker, COGS is the flour, butter, and electricity that went into each loaf. It's the *direct* cost of producing the thing you sold.

For software, COGS is less obvious — there's no flour. But there are still direct costs: things without which the product literally couldn't be delivered:

- Hosting / infrastructure (the servers that run the app)
- Payment processing fees
- Platform fees — the cut Apple and Google take on every in-app purchase
- Customer-specific support costs (for enterprise SaaS)

Everything else — salaries, marketing, office rent — sits *below* gross profit in operating expenses.

---

## Memrise's COGS: Almost Entirely One Thing

| Line | January 2026 | % of Revenue |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| App Store Fees | £116,422 | 26.3% |
| Withholding Tax | £1,129 | 0.3% |
| Affiliate Commission | £0 | — |
| **Total COGS** | **£117,551** | **26.5%** |

App Store Fees are **99% of all COGS**. There is no infrastructure cost line here at all — that's in OpEx.

---

## The Gross Margin Cascade

```
Revenue                     £443,490   ← what Memrise earned this month
Cost of Sales               (£117,551) ← what it cost to deliver it
                             ---------
Gross Profit                £325,939   ← what's left to run the company
Gross Margin                   73.5%   ← Gross Profit ÷ Revenue
```

**73.5% gross margin** — for every £1 of revenue, 73.5p is available to pay salaries, marketing, infrastructure, and produce a profit. The other 26.5p goes to Apple and Google.

---

## Why January is Always the Worst Month

| Month | Revenue | App Store % | Gross Margin |
|-------|---------|------------|-------------|
| Jan 2025 | £581,895 | **33.4%** | **66.4%** |
| Feb 2025 | £564,143 | 13.3% | 86.6% |
| May 2025 | £558,373 | **12.1%** | **87.8%** |
| Jul 2025 | £550,097 | 19.6% | 80.2% |
| Dec 2025 | £452,751 | 15.3% | 84.6% |
| **Jan 2026** | **£443,490** | **26.3%** | **73.5%** |

The App Store % moves because **channel mix moves**. Apple only takes 30% of purchases *through the App Store* — not from Memrise's website.

The most likely explanation for January spikes: **New Year resolutions**. January is when Memrise sees a surge of new subscribers downloading the app and subscribing in-app (where the 30% fee applies), rather than going to the website.

A CFO reading this doesn't just see "73.5% gross margin." They see: "If we could shift even 10% of January purchases to web-direct, we'd add ~£13,000 straight to gross profit."

---

## What's Not in COGS (And Why That Matters)

Where's the infrastructure cost? Memrise runs on servers — hosting sits in OpEx under "Running Costs" (£76,012 in January 2026). This is an accounting classification choice. If you moved Running Costs into COGS:

```
Revenue                     £443,490
"Adjusted" COGS             (£193,563)   ← App Store + Running Costs
Adjusted Gross Profit       £249,927
Adjusted Gross Margin          56.4%
```

Still reasonable — but a meaningfully different picture. When comparing Memrise's gross margins to competitors, always ask: what does the other company include in COGS?

---

## Three Concepts to Remember

1. COGS = direct costs of delivering the product (for Memrise: almost entirely App Store fees)
2. Gross margin = what's left after COGS — your runway before operating expenses
3. January gross margin is structurally worse because channel mix skews toward App Store
