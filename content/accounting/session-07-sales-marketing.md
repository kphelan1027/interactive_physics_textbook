# Accounting — Session 7: Sales & Marketing: The Engine

> *Every pound spent acquiring a customer is an investment — the question is the return*

---

## What You Already Know

In Session 6, you learned that Sales & Marketing sits in OpEx — it's not a delivery cost, it's the cost of creating demand and winning customers. You saw that Memrise's S&M spend runs at approximately £150k/month, making it the second-largest OpEx line after payroll. Today you'll look at that £150k not as a cost, but as an investment — and learn the framework for deciding whether it's a good one.

## The simplest true statement

If you spend £1 to acquire a customer who gives you back £3 over their lifetime, you have a good business; if you spend £1 to get back 80p, you have an expensive hobby.

---

## The Three Numbers That Define Acquisition Economics

**CAC — Customer Acquisition Cost.** The total sales and marketing spend required to acquire one new customer. Calculated as:

```
CAC = Total S&M Spend ÷ Number of New Customers Acquired
```

If Memrise spends £150k in January on S&M and acquires 3,000 new paying subscribers, the blended CAC is £50. But this blended number hides important variation between channels.

**LTV — Lifetime Value.** The total gross profit you expect to generate from a customer over their relationship with Memrise. The standard formula:

```
LTV = ARPU × Gross Margin % ÷ Monthly Churn Rate
```

If Memrise's average revenue per user is £8/month, gross margin is 73.5%, and monthly churn is 3%, then:

```
LTV = £8 × 0.735 ÷ 0.03 = £196
```

**Payback Period.** How many months before you recover the CAC from the gross profit generated by that customer.

```
Payback Period = CAC ÷ (ARPU × Gross Margin %)
```

At CAC of £50 and monthly gross profit per user of £5.88 (£8 × 73.5%):

```
Payback Period = £50 ÷ £5.88 ≈ 8.5 months
```

## B2C vs B2B: Two Very Different Engines

Memrise operates two acquisition channels with fundamentally different economics. This is important because a blended CAC obscures the story.

### B2C (Consumer)

Memrise's consumer business benefits from organic acquisition — app store discoverability, word of mouth, SEO, and brand recognition built over more than a decade. This means a significant portion of B2C subscribers arrive with zero paid acquisition cost. Paid channels (social ads, influencer, app install campaigns) supplement organic.

**B2C characteristics:**
- Lower CAC (partially organic)
- Higher volume, smaller contracts
- Higher churn (consumers cancel freely)
- Lower LTV per customer — but many more customers
- Shorter payback periods on paid channels

### B2B (Business)

Memrise's B2B offering — selling to corporates for employee language learning — has a longer sales cycle and requires direct sales effort. A B2B sales rep might spend weeks closing a deal.

**B2B characteristics:**
- Higher CAC (direct sales time, proposals, procurement processes)
- Lower volume, larger contracts (£5k–£50k+ annually per account)
- Lower churn (institutional inertia, procurement lock-in)
- Higher LTV per customer
- Longer payback period, but more predictable

For B2B, the LTV:CAC framing is essential. An account paying £30k/year with 85% gross margin and 15% annual churn has an LTV of approximately:

```
LTV = £30,000 × 0.85 ÷ 0.15 = £170,000
```

If it cost £15k in sales time to close that account, LTV:CAC = ~11x. That's exceptional. The challenge is the payback period: at £30k revenue × 85% margin = £25.5k gross profit per year, the £15k CAC is recovered in about 7 months.

---

## The Healthy Benchmark

The widely cited rule for SaaS and subscription businesses:

| Metric | Healthy | Warning Zone |
|---|---|---|
| LTV:CAC | >3x | <2x |
| CAC Payback Period | <12 months (B2C), <18 months (B2B) | >24 months |
| S&M as % of Revenue | 20-40% (growth stage) | >60% (unsustainable) |

For Memrise, S&M at £150k against revenue of £443k is approximately 34% — within the normal range for a growth-stage company, but only sustainable if the LTV:CAC justifies it.

---

## What the £150k S&M Line Actually Contains

When you look at "Sales & Marketing ~£150k/month," this likely includes:

- Paid digital advertising (app install campaigns, social)
- B2B sales team cost (partial — some may be in payroll separately)
- Marketing tooling and software
- PR and content production
- Conference and event spend
- Agency fees

The key discipline is measuring *which* of these components is driving new customer acquisition, and at what CAC. Not all £150k is equally productive — some spending builds brand over years, some acquires customers this month.

---

## Three Concepts to Remember

1. **CAC is an investment, not a cost** — the question is always whether LTV justifies it, and how long you wait for payback.
2. **B2C and B2B have opposite profiles** — B2C is high-volume, low-CAC, higher-churn; B2B is low-volume, high-CAC, lower-churn. Blending them without segmentation hides both stories.
3. **LTV:CAC above 3x is the target** — below 2x, you're likely destroying value with every customer acquired.
