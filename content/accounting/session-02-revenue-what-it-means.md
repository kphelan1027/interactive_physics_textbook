# Accounting — Session 2: Revenue — What the Number Actually Means

> *Revenue is not money received. Revenue is money earned.*

---

## What You Already Know

In Session 1, I pointed you at a number on the January 2026 balance sheet: **£2.3M of "Deferred revenue"** — listed as a *liability*.

You probably thought: that's odd. Why would £2.3M of customers paying us be a bad thing?

That's the thread we're pulling on today.

---

## The Rule

The accounting principle (IFRS 15 — the international standard Stephen and Tim at Novabook will have applied) is:

> **Recognise revenue when you deliver the thing you promised — not when you receive the money.**

For Memrise, "delivering the thing you promised" means: providing the app for the period the customer paid for.

So the rule becomes:
- Someone pays £120 for a year's access → Memrise earns **£10 per month**
- After month 1: £10 is revenue. £110 is still "owed to the customer" — it's deferred revenue
- If Memrise shut down tomorrow, they'd theoretically owe that £110 back. That's why it's a *liability*

## What Memrise's Revenue Actually Looks Like (January 2026)

| Line | Amount | % of Total |
|------|--------|------------|
| Lifetime subscriptions | £180,383 | 40.7% |
| Annual subscriptions | £213,466 | 48.1% |
| Monthly subscriptions | £47,957 | 10.8% |
| Quarterly | £1,441 | 0.3% |
| Semi-annual | £5 | ~0% |
| Other | £239 | ~0% |
| **Total** | **£443,490** | |

Three things a CFO notices within seconds:

**1. Lifetime and Annual dominate (89% of revenue)**
Monthly is an afterthought. Memrise users make long-term commitments. That changes how you think about growth, risk, and cash timing.

**2. Lifetime subscriptions are amortised — not recognised upfront**
£180K of "Lifetime" revenue recognised in a single month doesn't mean £180K of lifetime subscriptions were *sold* this month. It means Memrise has a large stock of lifetime subscribers acquired over years, and their original payments are being drip-fed into the P&L month by month over an estimated service period. The £180K in January 2026 is actually a portion of cash that came in months or years ago.

**3. Revenue is falling — consistently**

| Month | Total Revenue |
|-------|--------------|
| Jan 2025 | £581,895 |
| Jun 2025 | £550,242 |
| Sep 2025 | £513,633 |
| Dec 2025 | £452,751 |
| Jan 2026 | £443,490 |

**24% year-on-year decline.** Not a blip — a trend. Every single month lower than the last. The recognised revenue pool is draining faster than new subscribers fill it.

---

## The Deferred Revenue Connection

Every time someone pays for an annual or lifetime subscription, Memrise receives the **cash immediately** but can only recognise a **fraction** as revenue each month. The rest sits on the balance sheet as "owed to the customer."

| Date | Deferred Revenue (Balance Sheet) |
|------|----------------------------------|
| Jan 2025 | £3,486,771 |
| Jun 2025 | £2,859,239 |
| Dec 2025 | £2,221,126 |
| Jan 2026 | £2,307,401 |

Falling from £3.5M to £2.3M over the year. New subscribers aren't filling the pot as fast as old ones drain it. This is deferred revenue *burning off* — a lagging signal of subscriber acquisition health.

---

## Three Concepts to Remember

1. Revenue ≠ cash received — it's earned when the service is delivered (IFRS 15)
2. Deferred revenue is a *liability* — cash received for service not yet delivered
3. A shrinking deferred revenue pool means new subscriptions aren't replacing old ones fast enough
