# Accounting — Session 20: Reading an Investor Pack

> *The numbers are the same; the questions they answer are entirely different — and knowing both perspectives makes you a better CEO.*

---

## What You Already Know

In Session 19 you designed a board financial report — the five questions it must answer, the P&L variance structure, the KPI trends, and the risk register. You saw that the board pack is an argument, not a data dump. Now we look at the same financial information through a different pair of eyes: the investor's. For a PE-backed company like Memrise, investors read the same pack but with different priorities, different time horizons, and different definitions of what constitutes "good news."

## The simplest true statement

**A PE investor reads your financials to answer one question: is this company on the path to generating the cash returns that justify the investment — and if not, what needs to change and how quickly?**

---

## VC vs PE: Different Investors, Different Lenses

Memrise is PE-backed, which matters enormously for understanding how investors read the numbers.

| Dimension | Venture Capital | Private Equity |
|---|---|---|
| Return model | One massive winner covers all losses | Predictable return on each deal |
| Time horizon | 7–10 years | 3–5 years |
| Primary metric | Revenue growth rate | EBITDA margin + FCF |
| Attitude to losses | Accepted if growth is high | Requires a credible path to profitability |
| Revenue quality | Growth > quality | Recurring, predictable > one-off |
| Key question | "Can this be a £100M+ business?" | "Can we exit at 3–5× our investment?" |

A VC investor looking at Memrise's current P&L might focus on: is the product differentiating, is there a large TAM, can AI change the growth trajectory? A PE investor looks at the same numbers and asks: at what EBITDA multiple does a trade buyer or secondary PE fund acquire this, and are we building toward that number?

---

## What PE Investors Prioritise in the Pack

**1. EBITDA trajectory**

Not the current EBITDA (which may be negative), but the direction and pace of improvement. A PE investor wants to see a credible path from, say, −£3M EBITDA today to break-even in 18 months and +£2M in 36 months. What they are modelling is the exit multiple:

```
Enterprise value at exit ≈ Exit EBITDA × Sector multiple
```

If Memrise can reach £3M EBITDA and EdTech/language companies trade at 8–12× EBITDA, enterprise value at exit is £24–36M. The investor compares that to their entry valuation to determine whether the return is acceptable.

**2. Revenue quality**

Not all revenue is equal. Investors apply a hierarchy:

| Revenue type | Quality | Multiple deserved |
|---|---|---|
| Multi-year contracted B2B ARR | Highest | Premium multiple |
| Annual auto-renewing subscriptions | High | Standard SaaS multiple |
| Monthly subscriptions | Medium | Discount for churn risk |
| One-off or project revenue | Low | Revenue multiple only |

Memrise's revenue is predominantly annual and monthly subscriptions — good quality by any measure. But the declining trend is a quality concern: recurring revenue that is shrinking is less valuable than flat or growing recurring revenue, even at the same absolute level.

**3. Cash runway relative to milestones**

PE investors want to know whether the company can reach its next value inflection point without needing additional equity capital (which dilutes their stake). The key milestone for Memrise is EBITDA breakeven — the point at which the business sustains itself from operations.

If runway is 14 months and breakeven requires 18 months, the board faces a binary choice: raise more equity (dilution) or accelerate the path to profitability by cutting costs. PE investors will have a strong preference for the latter unless they have conviction the market opportunity justifies dilution.

**4. Unit economics and cohort health**

As covered in Session 16, PE investors will dissect LTV:CAC and payback periods. They are particularly alert to:

- Whether recent cohorts churn faster than older ones (product degradation signal)
- Whether CAC is rising over time (market saturation signal)
- Whether ARPU is stable or declining (pricing pressure or mix shift)

A deteriorating cohort picture — even if total MRR looks stable — is a red flag that the investor will push hard on.

**5. Headcount efficiency**

Revenue per employee and cost per employee are watched closely. As a rough benchmark:

```
Memrise ~£5.3M ARR ÷ ~80 employees ≈ £66k ARR per employee
```

For a SaaS company at this stage, that is on the lower end. PE investors will often push for headcount restructuring if this ratio doesn't improve — or at least for a credible plan showing how it improves without proportional headcount growth.

## Reading the Pack as a Dual-Language Document

As CEO, your job is to be fluent in both the operational language (what's driving each metric) and the investor language (what each metric implies for valuation and return). When you present to investors, you need to anticipate the investor's question behind every number you share.

| You say | Investor hears | Investor's follow-up question |
|---|---|---|
| "Revenue declined 24% YoY" | "Return at risk" | "What's the credible reversal story and what's it worth?" |
| "Gross margin improved to 82%" | "Unit economics are stable" | "Does this hold as you scale B2B?" |
| "We've reduced monthly burn by £50k" | "Path to profitability improving" | "What's the exit EBITDA in 24 months on current trajectory?" |
| "B2B ARR growing 15% QoQ" | "Revenue quality improving" | "What's the B2B ARR target at exit, and is that the acquirer's entry point?" |

The investor pack conversation is not about explaining the past — it's about building confidence in the future. Every historical number is only interesting insofar as it predicts the future trajectory.

---

## The Narrative Arc the Investor Needs

A strong investor financial narrative for Memrise at this stage might be:

> "We are a £5.3M ARR business with a declining consumer revenue trend and a growing B2B segment. We have 14 months of runway. We are investing in AI-powered personalisation that we believe will structurally improve retention. Our path to EBITDA breakeven requires reducing monthly burn by £X over 12 months while growing B2B ARR to £Y. At that point, the business is self-sustaining and the revenue mix has shifted toward higher-quality recurring contracts. We are on track against that plan, with B2B growing ahead of target and consumer churn within model."

That is four sentences. It answers every investor priority: revenue quality, trajectory, runway, path to profitability, and evidence of execution.

---

## Three Concepts to Remember

1. **PE investors value EBITDA trajectory over revenue growth** — they are building to an exit multiple, not a TAM story, so profitability timing matters more than growth rate.
2. **Revenue quality affects the multiple, not just the absolute value** — declining recurring revenue is worth less than flat recurring revenue at the same absolute number; B2B contracted ARR is worth more than monthly consumer subscriptions.
3. **The board pack and the investor pack are the same document read differently** — as CEO, being fluent in both readings makes you a more effective communicator with each audience.
