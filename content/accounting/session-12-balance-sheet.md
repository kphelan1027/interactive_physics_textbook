# Accounting — Session 12: The Balance Sheet

> *Everything the company owns, owes, and is worth — frozen at a single moment in time.*

---

## What You Already Know

In Session 11 you worked through free cash flow — the cash a business generates after paying for operations and capital investment. The most important insight was simple: cash is real, profit is an opinion, and FCF is what keeps the lights on. Now we zoom out. FCF tells you how cash moved over a period. The balance sheet tells you where everything stands right now.

## The simplest true statement

**Assets always equal Liabilities plus Equity — always, by definition, without exception.**

---

## The Balance Sheet as a Photograph

The P&L is a film — it records what happened between two dates. The balance sheet is a photograph — it records what exists at one precise moment. As soon as you turn the page, it's already out of date. That's not a flaw; it's the point. It gives you a snapshot of financial position: what the company owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and what's left for shareholders (equity).

The fundamental equation:

```
Assets = Liabilities + Equity
```

Rearranged:

```
Equity = Assets − Liabilities
```

If Memrise owns £8M of assets and owes £4M to others, equity is £4M. If accumulated losses have eroded that equity into negative territory, the balance sheet shows it plainly. There is no hiding here.

---

## Walking Through Memrise's Balance Sheet

Let's populate the equation with realistic Memrise figures. These are approximate and for illustration only.

**Assets**

| Category | Item | Approximate value |
|---|---|---|
| Current assets | Cash and cash equivalents | ~£5–7M |
| Current assets | Trade receivables (B2B invoices outstanding) | ~£500k |
| Non-current assets | Capitalised development costs (intangibles) | ~£2–3M |
| Non-current assets | Equipment, fixtures | ~£100–200k |
| **Total assets** | | **~£8–10.5M** |

A note on intangibles: Memrise capitalises some software development costs. Rather than expensing every engineer's salary in the month it's paid, costs that create a durable asset (a new product feature with a measurable useful life) can be spread across future periods. This is why the balance sheet shows £2–3M in intangibles even though it's a software company with no factories.

**Liabilities**

| Category | Item | Approximate value |
|---|---|---|
| Current liabilities | Deferred revenue (subscription pool) | ~£2.3M |
| Current liabilities | Trade payables | ~£200k |
| Current liabilities | Accruals and other payables | ~£300–500k |
| **Total liabilities** | | **~£2.8–3M** |

Deferred revenue is the largest liability. It represents cash already collected from subscribers for future service. Memrise has the cash — but hasn't yet "earned" it under accounting rules. Until the subscription period passes, it sits on the liabilities side. This is one of the counterintuitive features of SaaS accounting.

**Equity**

```
Equity = Total assets − Total liabilities
       ≈ £9M − £2.9M
       ≈ £6.1M
```

But look inside equity and you'll find two layers: the share capital and share premium (money investors put in), minus accumulated losses (every year of losses since incorporation). Memrise, like most venture-backed companies, carries significant accumulated losses. Equity is positive only because investors have injected more capital than the losses have consumed.

---

## Why the Two Sides Always Balance

This is not a coincidence or a mathematical trick. Every transaction hits both sides simultaneously. When Memrise collects a £100 annual subscription:

- Cash (asset) goes up £100
- Deferred revenue (liability) goes up £100

When that month of subscription is delivered:

- Deferred revenue (liability) goes down ~£8.33
- Revenue (which flows to retained earnings, i.e. equity) goes up ~£8.33

The balance sheet stays balanced through every transaction because double-entry bookkeeping requires it. Two entries, opposite sides, equal magnitude.

---

## Three Concepts to Remember

1. **Assets = Liabilities + Equity** — this is not a formula to memorise, it is the definition of how a company's finances work. Every transaction preserves it.
2. **Deferred revenue is a liability, not a bonus** — cash in the bank from annual subscriptions is not yet income. It appears on the liabilities side until the service is delivered.
3. **Equity includes accumulated losses** — a company can have positive equity and still carry years of losses inside it. Share capital holds up the equity line; retained losses drag it down.
