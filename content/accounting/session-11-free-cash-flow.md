# Accounting — Session 11: Free Cash Flow

> *The number that separates a self-sustaining business from one that needs a patron*

---

## What You Already Know

In Session 10, you learned that the Cash Flow Statement has three sections — operating, investing, and financing — and that Memrise's operating cash outflow of approximately £550-600k/month is the core burn rate that determines runway. Today you'll zoom in on one metric that sophisticated investors treat as the gold standard: Free Cash Flow. It's a refinement of operating cash flow, and for a software company like Memrise, it tells you something precise about the path to self-sufficiency.

## The simplest true statement

Free Cash Flow is the cash left after you've paid for everything you need to run and maintain the business — if it's positive, you don't need anyone's money; if it's negative, you do.

---

## The Formula

Free Cash Flow has a beautifully simple definition:

```
Free Cash Flow = Operating Cash Flow − Capital Expenditure (Capex)
```

That's it. You take the cash generated (or consumed) by running the business, and you subtract the cash spent on maintaining and growing the asset base. What's left is "free" — available to return to investors, pay down debt, or reinvest for growth.

For Memrise in January 2026 (approximate):

```
Operating Cash Flow:  ~-£580k
Capital Expenditure:  ~-£25k  (equipment purchases, some capitalised dev)
Free Cash Flow:       ~-£605k
```

The gap between operating cash flow and FCF is small for Memrise — which is characteristic of asset-light software businesses. Unlike a manufacturer who spends heavily on machinery, or a media company that spends on content libraries, Memrise's primary asset is software built by its people. The cash cost of that software is already captured in operating cash flow (via payroll), so capex is a small incremental amount.

---

## Why FCF and Not EBITDA?

Investors have grown sceptical of EBITDA (as you learned in Session 5) because it adds back depreciation and amortisation — real costs of real past investments. FCF is harder to flatter because:

1. **It's cash.** There's no accounting discretion in cash. You either have it or you don't.
2. **It includes capex.** A company spending aggressively on capital investments to grow will show a worse FCF than EBITDA — and that's the honest picture.
3. **It includes working capital changes.** A business that's collecting from customers slowly or paying suppliers quickly will have a worse FCF than EBITDA. These timing differences matter.

Many growth-stage companies used to report "adjusted EBITDA" as their primary metric, adding back stock compensation, restructuring charges, and other convenient items. Investors have become increasingly demanding of FCF as the true measure — particularly as interest rates rose and the cost of capital became real again.

---

## FCF for a SaaS Business: A Different Profile

Software businesses have a distinctive FCF profile over their lifecycle:

| Stage | FCF Profile | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Early growth | Deeply negative | Investing in product, team, and acquisition |
| Scaling | Improving (less negative) | Revenue growing faster than costs |
| Mature | Positive, often strongly | Low capex, high margins, stable churn |

For Memrise, the current FCF of approximately -£600k/month is consistent with a growth-stage software company investing ahead of revenue. The question investors ask is not "is FCF positive?" — for most growth companies, it isn't — but "**is FCF improving, and at what trajectory does it reach zero?**"

A company moving from -£800k/month FCF to -£600k/month FCF over 12 months is on a trajectory to reach breakeven in a calculable time. A company stuck at -£800k with no visible trajectory is a different proposition.

---

## FCF Breakeven: What It Means for Memrise

FCF breakeven — the point at which FCF reaches zero — is a milestone that transforms a company's strategic options:

- **Before FCF breakeven:** The company must raise external capital to survive. Every fundraise dilutes founders and early investors. The timeline is fixed by runway.
- **At FCF breakeven:** The company can survive on its own revenue. It may still choose to raise capital to accelerate growth, but it's now a choice, not a necessity. This transforms the negotiating position with investors.
- **After FCF breakeven:** The company generates cash it can deploy — into new markets, product investment, acquisitions, or returning to shareholders.

For Memrise, reaching FCF breakeven requires roughly £600k/month of additional operating cash flow improvement. That could come from:

- Revenue growth: adding ~£600k/month of revenue at 73% gross margin generates ~£440k additional gross profit — not enough alone without cost discipline
- A combination: ~£400k revenue growth + ~£200k cost reduction would approximately close the gap
- The maths make clear that revenue growth is necessary but insufficient on its own at current cost levels

---

## Capex in a Software Business: What to Watch

For Memrise, capex is primarily:

- **Equipment** — laptops, monitors, any on-premise hardware
- **Capitalised software development** — the cash equivalent of engineering time spent on new feature development (discussed in Session 8)

As Memrise grows, the capitalised software development figure becomes worth watching. If engineers are building significant new capabilities, the "investing activities" section of the cash flow statement will show higher outflows — and these represent genuine bets on future revenue. FCF will worsen during heavy investment phases, which is rational but requires explanation to investors.

A useful sanity check: if capex is rising but FCF is worsening, you want to see whether revenue is growing in tandem. If it isn't, the investments aren't paying off.

---

## Three Concepts to Remember

1. **FCF = Operating Cash Flow minus Capex** — for a software company with low capex, FCF is close to operating cash flow. The gap widens for capital-intensive businesses.
2. **FCF is harder to flatter than EBITDA** — it includes all cash costs and working capital movements. Sophisticated investors prefer it because it's more honest.
3. **FCF breakeven transforms strategic options** — before it, the company depends on external capital; after it, growth is a choice, not a necessity. For Memrise, the path there requires revenue growth combined with cost discipline.
