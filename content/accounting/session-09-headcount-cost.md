# Accounting — Session 9: Headcount is Your Biggest Cost

> *The payroll line hides more than it reveals — here's how to read it properly*

---

## What You Already Know

In Session 8, you learned that engineers' time splits between capitalised development (building new things, spread over years) and expensed R&D (maintenance, hitting the P&L now). Both categories represent people costs. Today you'll step back and look at the full headcount picture — because at ~£550k/month, payroll is larger than Memrise's entire revenue line, and understanding it is the single most important financial skill for a CEO making hiring decisions.

## The simplest true statement

The number on an offer letter is not what an employee costs — the true cost is 25-35% higher once you add everything the company pays on top.

---

## The Fully-Loaded Cost Per Head

When a hiring manager says "we want to hire a senior engineer at £80k," the finance team hears a different number. The fully-loaded cost includes everything the company pays as a consequence of employment:

| Component | Approximate Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Base salary | £80,000 | The offer letter number |
| Employer National Insurance | ~£9,400 | 13.8% on earnings above ~£9k/year |
| Employer pension contribution | ~£2,400 | Typically 3-5% of salary |
| Private health insurance | ~£1,200 | Common at tech companies |
| Equipment (laptop, peripherals) | ~£800/year | Amortised cost of hardware |
| Software licences per head | ~£500/year | Slack, GitHub, Notion etc. |
| Office space allocation | ~£600/year | Pro-rata desk/facilities cost |
| Recruitment cost (amortised) | ~£800/year | Amortised over typical 3-year tenure |
| **Total fully-loaded cost** | **~£95,700** | ~1.2x base salary |

The rule of thumb: fully-loaded cost is typically **1.25x to 1.35x base salary** for UK office-based roles. For senior hires or those in London, it can be higher. For very junior roles with basic benefits packages, it can be closer to 1.2x.

---

## The Memrise Maths

Memrise has approximately 70-80 staff. With payroll running at ~£550k/month (~£6.6M/year), and assuming an average fully-loaded cost of ~£85k/year per head:

```
£6.6M ÷ £85k = ~78 heads at average fully-loaded cost
```

This reconciles. But the average conceals significant variation:

| Seniority Band | Approximate FLC | Annual Cost |
|---|---|---|
| Senior/Staff engineer, VP-level | ~£120-150k | Expensive per head |
| Mid-level individual contributor | ~£85-100k | The typical case |
| Junior / associate | ~£55-70k | Lower per head, more management overhead |
| Part-time / contractor | Varies | No employer NI, no pension, but higher day rates |

A team of 80 that is heavily senior-weighted will have a higher total payroll than 80 at the average. This matters when you're planning: every hire is a specific cost at a specific seniority, not a generic "1x average."

---

## The Hiring Decision as a Finance Decision

Every time Memrise considers a hire, it's a multi-year financial commitment. If you hire someone today at £80k base:

- Year 1 cost: ~£96k
- 3-year cost: ~£288k (assuming no pay rises — unrealistic)
- With 5% annual salary increases: ~£302k over 3 years
- Plus recruitment cost to replace if they leave in year 2: ~£15-25k

A hire isn't a monthly cost — it's a multi-year obligation that compounds. This is why headcount decisions should be treated with the same rigour as capital investments.

The useful question to ask before every hire: **what revenue or cost-saving does this person need to generate to justify their fully-loaded cost?** A product manager costing £120k/year needs to ship product that generates more than £120k in incremental value. That's not a precise calculation, but it sharpens the decision.

---

## Bench vs Project Work

Not all headcount time generates equal value. In any given month, employees may be:

- **Directly productive** — building features, closing deals, running campaigns
- **On bench** — between projects, in onboarding, or in a team that's paused
- **In overhead** — meetings, admin, cross-functional work that's necessary but not directly value-generating

For a company at Memrise's stage, where payroll is 1.7x revenue, the utilisation of the team is a critical lever. Moving from 70% utilisation to 85% utilisation across 80 people doesn't require any new hires — it requires clearer prioritisation and fewer dead-end projects.

---

## Reading the People P&L

As CEO, the questions to ask monthly:

1. **Headcount trend** — are we growing, flat, or shrinking? Is it intentional?
2. **Revenue per head** — for Memrise, ~£443k revenue ÷ ~78 heads ≈ £5,680/head/month. This is the productivity ratio to grow over time.
3. **Payroll as % of revenue** — currently ~124% (payroll exceeds revenue). The strategic goal is to get this below 80%, then below 50% through revenue growth, not cost cuts alone.
4. **New hire ramp time** — how long does a new engineer take to be productive? During ramp, you're paying fully-loaded cost for partial output.

---

## Three Concepts to Remember

1. **Fully-loaded cost is 1.25-1.35x base salary** — employer NI, pension, benefits, equipment, and recruitment all add up. A £80k offer letter costs the company ~£96k/year.
2. **Payroll is a multi-year commitment, not a monthly expense** — every hire compounds through salary growth, recruitment replacement costs, and management overhead.
3. **Revenue per head is the key productivity ratio** — for Memrise, growing this ratio (via revenue growth, not just cost cuts) is the path to sustainable unit economics.
